<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • Commission Notice 2022/C 355/01 • Regulation (EU) 2023/915</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Foundation</h2>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires identification of "any hazards that must be prevented, eliminated or reduced to acceptable levels"—chemical as surely as biological. The 2022 Commission Notice defines chemical hazards as those with potential to cause adverse health effects.</p>

<p>Chemical hazards fall into three categories:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Naturally occurring</strong>: mycotoxins in cereals, nuts, spices</li>
  <li><strong>Intentionally added</strong>: additives used improperly or excessively</li>
  <li><strong>Unintentionally introduced</strong>: cleaning residues, lubricants, pest control substances</li>
</ul>

<p>The Notice clarifies that not all significant hazards require a CCP. Prerequisite programmes (PRPs) often provide sufficient control for chemical hazards.</p>

<h2>2. Hazard Analysis</h2>

<h3>2.1 Mycotoxins</h3>

<p>Maximum levels for aflatoxins, ochratoxin A, and other mycotoxins are established in Regulation (EU) 2023/915. Control operates through <strong>supplier assurance</strong>: approved suppliers, certificates of analysis, visual inspection, and appropriate storage.</p>

<h3>2.2 Process Contaminants: Acrylamide</h3>

<p>Commission Regulation (EU) 2017/2158 establishes benchmark levels and mitigation measures. Controls include frying temperature management, oil quality monitoring, and cooking to golden yellow rather than dark brown.</p>

<h3>2.3 Cleaning Chemical Residues</h3>

<p>Controlled through documented cleaning procedures specifying concentration, contact time, and rinsing requirements. Chemical storage must be separate from food handling areas per Annex II of 852/2004.</p>

<h3>2.4 Allergens as Chemical Hazards</h3>

<p>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 amended 852/2004 to introduce explicit allergen management requirements. Control requires identification of all allergens present, prevention of cross-contact, and accurate communication to consumers.</p>

<h2>3. Critical Limits and Monitoring</h2>

<p>Critical limits for chemical hazards may take several forms:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Type</th>
      <th>Example</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Regulatory maximum</td>
      <td>Mycotoxin limits in 2023/915</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Process parameter</td>
      <td>Frying temperature ≤175°C</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Concentration specification</td>
      <td>Cleaning chemical dilution ratio</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>Where hazards are controlled through PRPs, monitoring must be appropriate to the control measure: supplier documentation review, visual storage inspection, cleaning completion sign-off.</p>

<p>For CCPs, monitoring must detect loss of control in time for corrective action. Records must show actual values, not merely ticks.</p>

<h2>4. Corrective Actions</h2>

<p>The fifth HACCP principle requires corrective actions addressing both immediate product disposition and systemic cause.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Deviation</th>
      <th>Immediate Action</th>
      <th>Preventive Action</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Missing certificate of analysis</td>
      <td>Quarantine ingredients</td>
      <td>Review supplier status</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Chemical stored in food area</td>
      <td>Remove; inspect adjacent food</td>
      <td>Retrain staff</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Allergen cross-contact</td>
      <td>Quarantine; assess disposition</td>
      <td>Review segregation procedures</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>Corrective action records must document deviation, product disposition, root cause investigation, preventive action, and verification of effectiveness.</p>

<h2>5. Verification and Documentation</h2>

<p>Verification (HACCP Principle 6) requires periodic evaluation: documentation review, physical inspection, and analytical testing where warranted. Frequency should be risk-based: supplier documentation per delivery or annually; chemical storage weekly; allergen controls monthly.</p>

<p>Essential records include:</p>

  <thead>

      <th>Record</th>

    <tr>

      <td>Current + 12 months</td>

    <tr>

      <td>Supply duration + 12 months</td>

    <tr>
      <td>Cleaning records</td>
      <td>3 months minimum</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Allergen matrix</td>
      <td>Current; superseded 12 months</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Monitoring records</td>
      <td>3–12 months</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Corrective actions</td>
      <td>12 months minimum</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>Records must be completed contemporaneously, show actual values, identify the person responsible, and be legible and protected.</p>
