<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • SFBB Adaptation for Thai Cuisine</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Framework</h2>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires HACCP-based procedures. The seven principles are: hazard identification, CCP determination, critical limits, monitoring, corrective actions, verification, and documentation commensurate with business size.</p>

<p>The 2022 Commission Notice (2022/C 355/01) formalises <strong>Operational Prerequisite Programmes (OPRPs)</strong>—essential controls not suited to binary critical limits. For Thai kitchens, bulk rice cooling and curry paste holding are more appropriately OPRPs than CCPs.</p>

<p>The FSA's Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) framework organises compliance around the "Four Cs": Cross-contamination, Cleaning, Chilling, and Cooking. While no dedicated Thai cuisine SFBB pack exists, operators may adapt Asian-cuisine frameworks or the general catering pack. SFBB constitutes documented compliance when completed and maintained.</p>

<h2>2. Cuisine-Specific Hazards</h2>

<h3>2.1 Rice and Bacillus cereus</h3>

<p>The most significant microbiological hazard in Thai operations is <strong>Bacillus cereus</strong> in cooked rice. Spores survive cooking and, if rice cools too slowly, germinate to produce heat-stable toxins that reheating cannot destroy.</p>

<p>Thai cuisine is built on rice—jasmine, sticky, noodles. Research in Thailand demonstrated <em>B. cereus</em> widely present on rice in all forms. A 2022 Pattaya outbreak investigation confirmed transmission via kitchen surfaces and utensils.</p>

<h3>2.2 Fermented Ingredients</h3>

<p>Nam pla (fish sauce) and kapi (shrimp paste) achieve safety through high salt and low water activity. The hazard is <strong>cross-contamination</strong>: spores transferred via utensils to ready-to-eat foods. Research identified shrimp paste as a common carrier of <em>B. cereus</em>. Control requires designated spoons, refrigeration post-opening, and handwashing after handling fermented ingredients.</p>

<h3>2.3 Fresh Herbs and Raw Vegetables: Som Tam</h3>

<p>Green papaya salad contains raw, unheated ingredients—no kill step exists. Pathogens present on raw ingredients will be present in the finished dish. Traditional use of the same mortar and pestle throughout service creates a cross-contamination vector.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Thorough washing of all salad vegetables in potable water</li>
  <li>Dedicated, sanitised mortar and pestle for raw salad preparation</li>
  <li>Preparation in small batches, held refrigerated until service</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.4 Coconut Milk</h3>

<p>Fresh and canned coconut milk support rapid bacterial growth. Curries held in bains-marie must maintain ≥63°C, verified at 2-hour intervals. Any coconut milk-based dish held below 63°C for >2 hours must be discarded.</p>

<h2>3. The Rice Cooling Protocol</h2>

<p>Given the centrality of both rice and <em>B. cereus</em> to Thai kitchen operations:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Step</th>
      <th>Action</th>
      <th>Critical Parameter</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>1</td>
      <td>Remove rice from cooker immediately</td>
      <td>Do not leave on "warm" &gt;30 minutes</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>2</td>
      <td>Decant into shallow containers</td>
      <td>Depth ≤5 cm</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>3</td>
      <td>Cool in blast chiller or with airflow</td>
      <td>93°C to 20°C within 2 hours</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>4</td>
      <td>Transfer to refrigerator</td>
      <td>20°C to ≤5°C within further 2 hours</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>5</td>
      <td>Label with date and time</td>
      <td>Use within 48 hours</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>6</td>
      <td>Reheat to ≥75°C core temperature</td>
      <td>Before service or fried rice preparation</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>Alternative approach: Small-batch cooking at 2-hour intervals throughout service eliminates the cooling requirement for service rice.</p>

<h2>4. Documentation and Verification</h2>

<h3>4.1 Records That Attract Scrutiny</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rice cooling logs:</strong> Time/temperature from cooking to ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Refrigerator temperature records:</strong> Daily min/max ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Hot-hold temperature checks:</strong> Curries and soups ≥63°C at 2-hour intervals</li>
  <li><strong>Cooking temperature verification:</strong> Especially for chicken and minced products</li>
  <li><strong>Allergen matrix:</strong> Current and verified against supplier specifications</li>
</ul>

<h3>4.2 Internal Verification</h3>

<p>Verification (HACCP Principle 6) requires periodic evaluation:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Weekly management walk-through observing rice cooling and date-marking</li>
  <li>Monthly thermometer calibration (ice-point and boiling-point methods)</li>
  <li>Quarterly documentation review</li>
  <li>Annual HACCP plan review triggered by menu changes or new suppliers</li>
</ul>

<p>These activities should be recorded, demonstrating active management to enforcement.</p>

<h2>5. Food Safety Culture</h2>

<p>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 amended Annex II of 852/2004 to require evidence of food safety culture: management commitment, employee awareness, open communication, and sufficient resources. For Thai restaurants, this means:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Documented staff training records</li>
  <li>Evidence of management presence during service</li>
  <li>Regular team briefings on food safety</li>
  <li>A process for employees to raise concerns without fear of reprisal</li>
</ul>
