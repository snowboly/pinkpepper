<p><em>Council Regulation (Euratom) 2016/52 • RIFE Monitoring • Risk-Based Compliance</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Context</h2>

<p>Radiological hazards are not explicitly enumerated in Regulation (EC) No 852/2004. Their regulatory treatment derives from Council Regulation (Euratom) 2016/52, which establishes maximum permitted levels of radioactive contamination following a nuclear accident or radiological emergency.</p>

<p>The regulation operates as emergency response legislation—it activates following a declared emergency and does not impose routine monitoring obligations on individual operators during normal conditions.</p>

<p>The FSA's annual Radioactivity in Food and the Environment (RIFE) report provides routine surveillance confirming that UK food meets safety standards. This monitoring underpins the conclusion that radiological hazards are controlled at supply chain level.</p>

<h2>2. Determining Applicability</h2>

<p>The hazard analysis must consider radiological hazards with appropriate proportionality:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Risk Category</th>
      <th>Relevance</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Higher vigilance</td>
      <td>Wild mushrooms/game from Chernobyl-affected regions; specific Pacific seafood post-Fukushima</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Routine assurance sufficient</td>
      <td>Imported seafood; speciality ingredients from regions with nuclear facilities</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Minimal operator concern</td>
      <td>UK/EU agricultural products; processed foods; established supply chains</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>For typical UK/EU restaurants, the overwhelming majority of ingredients fall into the third category. The analysis should conclude that radiological hazards are controlled through supplier assurance and regulatory monitoring.</p>

<h2>3. Documentation Approach</h2>

<p>Auditors increasingly expect evidence that radiological hazards have been considered, even where no operator-level controls are required. The documentation should include:</p>

<p><strong>1. Hazard identification statement</strong><br />
<em>Example:</em> "Radiological hazards are recognised as a potential hazard category, controlled under normal conditions through RIFE monitoring and, in emergency, through maximum levels under Euratom 2016/52."</p>

<p><strong>2. Risk assessment</strong><br />
<em>Example:</em> "This establishment sources from UK/EU suppliers. No ingredients originate from regions subject to radiological restrictions. Public water supply is used."</p>

<p><strong>3. Control determination</strong><br />
<em>Example:</em> "Control is through supplier assurance and reliance on competent authority monitoring. No operator-level CCPs are identified."</p>

<p><strong>4. Emergency preparedness reference</strong><br />
<em>Example:</em> "In a declared emergency, this establishment would comply with maximum permitted levels established under Euratom 2016/52 and follow FSA guidance."</p>

<h2>4. When Enhanced Controls Apply</h2>

<p>Higher-risk scenarios warranting specific attention:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Importers of Pacific seafood</strong>: verify any applicable import restrictions</li>
  <li><strong>Specialist wild food sourcing</strong>: supplier assurance should address radiological parameters</li>
  <li><strong>Private water supplies</strong>: testing may be required</li>
  <li><strong>Proximity to nuclear installations</strong>: acknowledge in hazard analysis</li>
</ul>

<p>For these operations, control remains supplier assurance: approved status, geographic origin verification, certificates of analysis where required, and full traceability.</p>

<h2>5. Post-Brexit Position</h2>

<p>As of April 2026, Council Regulation (Euratom) 2016/52 forms part of retained EU law in Great Britain. The RIFE programme continues as the primary UK monitoring mechanism. Northern Ireland applies EU legislation directly under the Windsor Framework.</p>

<h2>6. Practical Actions</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Review current hazard analysis</strong>—does it mention radiological hazards? If not, add brief section concluding control through supplier assurance.</li>
  <li><strong>Confirm supplier approval procedures</strong> include consideration of geographic origin.</li>
  <li><strong>Add to HACCP review agenda</strong> as standing item to document periodic reconsideration.</li>
  <li><strong>Familiarise with RIFE programme</strong>—ability to reference provides authoritative support.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not over-engineer</strong>—a takeaway with UK/EU supply chain does not need monitoring equipment.</li>
</ol>
