<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • Commission Notice 2022/C 355/01 • SFBB Adaptation for Middle Eastern Cuisine</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Framework</h2>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires food business operators to implement procedures based on HACCP principles. The 2022 Commission Notice (2022/C 355/01) formalises <strong>Operational Prerequisite Programmes (OPRPs)</strong>—essential controls not suited to binary critical limits—and incorporates requirements on allergen management and food safety culture introduced by Regulation (EU) 2021/382.</p>

<p>The FSA's Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) framework organises compliance around the "Four Cs": Cross-contamination, Cleaning, Chilling, and Cooking. While no dedicated Middle Eastern SFBB pack exists, operators may adapt the general catering pack supplemented with cuisine-specific hazard awareness. The flexibility provisions in EU law recognise that the nature of the activity and size of the establishment determine appropriate control measures.</p>

<h2>2. Cuisine-Specific Hazards</h2>

<h3>2.1 Bulk-Cooked Legumes and Grains: Cooling Imperative</h3>

<p>Lebanese and Middle Eastern cuisine relies heavily on batch-cooked chickpeas (hummus), fava beans (foul medames), lentils (mujadara), and bulgur wheat (tabbouleh base). These high-protein, high-moisture foods support pathogen growth if improperly cooled.</p>

<p><em>Bacillus cereus</em> and <em>Clostridium perfringens</em> spores survive cooking and germinate during slow cooling. Research from the Gulf region confirms that application of HACCP principles significantly reduces microbiological counts in prepared foods.</p>

<p>Decant into shallow containers (depth ≤5 cm); use blast chiller or ice-water bath. Small-batch preparation during service eliminates the cooling requirement for service items.</p>

<h3>2.2 Raw Meat Preparation: Kibbeh Nayeh and Cross-Contamination</h3>

<p>Raw meat dishes—kibbeh nayeh (raw lamb with bulgur), habra nayeh (raw lamb), and raw liver preparations—present significant pathogen risk. Unlike cooked items, there is no kill step.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Source meat from approved suppliers with documented food safety management systems</li>
  <li>Meat must be extremely fresh; prepared in small batches to order</li>
  <li>Dedicated, sanitised preparation area and equipment</li>
  <li>Strict separation from ready-to-eat foods</li>
  <li>Consumer advisory notice required for raw menu items</li>
  <li>Preparation surface sanitised immediately after use</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.3 Dairy and Labneh: Fermentation Safety</h3>

<p>Labneh (strained yogurt), fresh cheeses, and yogurt-based sauces rely on fermentation and acidity for preservation. The hazard is post-fermentation contamination and temperature abuse.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Labneh and fresh cheeses held refrigerated at ≤5°C</li>
  <li>Dedicated utensils; no bare-hand contact</li>
  <li>Date-marking of opened containers; use within manufacturer guidance</li>
  <li>Yogurt-based sauces (tarator, toum) held refrigerated; small-batch preparation</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.4 Fresh Herbs and Salad Items: Tabbouleh and Fattoush</h3>

<p>Middle Eastern cuisine features abundant fresh, unheated produce: parsley, mint, tomatoes, cucumbers, onions, and lettuce. No kill step exists.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Thorough washing in potable water; consideration of pre-washed produce</li>
  <li>Dedicated colour-coded boards (brown for unwashed vegetables, green for RTE salad)</li>
  <li>Preparation in small batches, held refrigerated until service</li>
  <li>Separate disposal of trimmings</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.5 Garlic Sauce (Toum) and Oil-Based Emulsions</h3>

<p>Traditional toum (garlic emulsion) and other oil-based sauces present <em>Clostridium botulinum</em> risk if held anaerobically at ambient temperature. Garlic-in-oil mixtures have been implicated in botulism outbreaks.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Acidification (lemon juice/citric acid) to pH ≤4.6</li>
  <li>Refrigerated storage at ≤5°C at all times</li>
  <li>Small-batch preparation; discard after 7 days maximum</li>
  <li>Never hold at ambient temperature</li>
</ul>

<h2>4. Documentation and Verification</h2>

<h3>4.1 Records That Attract Scrutiny</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cooling logs for legumes and grains:</strong> Time/temperature from cooking to ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Refrigerator temperature records:</strong> Daily min/max ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Hot-hold temperature checks:</strong> Stews, rice, soups ≥63°C at 2-hour intervals</li>
  <li><strong>Toum preparation records:</strong> pH verification; batch date; 7-day discard</li>
  <li><strong>Allergen matrix:</strong> Current and verified; particular attention to sesame/tahini</li>
  <li><strong>Raw meat service advisory:</strong> Documented consumer communication</li>
</ul>

<h3>4.2 Internal Verification</h3>

<p>Verification (HACCP Principle 6) requires periodic evaluation:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Weekly management walk-through observing cooling practices and raw meat separation</li>
  <li>Monthly thermometer calibration</li>
  <li>Quarterly documentation review</li>
  <li>Annual HACCP plan review triggered by menu changes</li>
</ul>

<p>Records should be completed contemporaneously, show actual values, and identify the responsible person.</p>

<h2>5. Food Safety Culture</h2>

<p>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 requires evidence of food safety culture: management commitment, employee awareness, open communication, and sufficient resources. For Middle Eastern restaurants:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Documented training on legume cooling, raw meat handling, and sesame allergen awareness</li>
  <li>Front-of-house training on tahini/sesame communication</li>
  <li>Clear procedures for toum acidification and refrigeration</li>
  <li>A process for employees to raise concerns without fear of reprisal</li>
</ul>
