<p>Starting a new café, takeaway, restaurant, or home-based cake business is exciting. But before you sell your first sandwich or slice of cake, you have a legal obligation to manage food safety.</p>

<p>In the UK, this is done through a system called <strong>HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points)</strong>. For a new business, the word "HACCP" sounds technical and intimidating. But for small businesses, it doesn't require a PhD in microbiology—it requires common sense and a written plan.</p>

<p>Here is a practical checklist to ensure your new venture is compliant with Food Standards Agency (FSA) requirements and ready for your first Environmental Health Officer (EHO) inspection.</p>

<h2>Part 1: The "Safer Food, Better Business" (SFBB) Foundation</h2>

<blockquote><strong>The Myth:</strong> "I need to write a 100-page HACCP manual from scratch."<br /><strong>The Reality:</strong> The FSA provides a free pack called <strong>Safer Food, Better Business (SFBB)</strong>. For 90% of small caterers and retailers, filling this out <strong>is</strong> your HACCP system.</blockquote>

<h3>Checklist Item 1: Get the Right Pack</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Download or order the <strong>SFBB pack specific to your business type</strong> (Caterers, Retailers, Childminders, etc.).</li>
  <li>You can find this free on the <strong>Food Standards Agency website</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Checklist Item 2: The "Safe Methods"</h3>

<p>SFBB contains sections called <strong>"Safe Methods"</strong>. These are your HACCP controls. You must read them and confirm you will follow them. Key sections for new businesses include:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cross-contamination:</strong> Have you planned separate areas/chopping boards for raw meat and ready-to-eat food?</li>
  <li><strong>Cleaning:</strong> Do you have a written cleaning schedule (even a simple laminated list in the kitchen)?</li>
  <li><strong>Chilling:</strong> Do you know your fridge must be <strong>below 5°C</strong> (legally below 8°C, but 5°C is best practice)?</li>
  <li><strong>Cooking:</strong> Do you have a probe thermometer to check that chicken/poultry reaches <strong>75°C</strong> in the thickest part?</li>
</ul>

<h2>Part 2: The "Day 1" Essential HACCP Checklist</h2>

<p>Before you open your doors on Day 1, ensure these five physical controls are in place. These are the Critical Control Points that EHOs will look for first.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>The Control</th>
      <th>What the EHO Wants to See</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>

    <tr>
      <td><strong>1. Fridge &amp; Freezer Temps</strong></td>
      <td>A <strong>log book</strong> or <strong>app</strong> record showing fridge temps taken first thing in the morning.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>2. Probe Thermometer</strong></td>
      <td>A <strong>digital probe</strong> (not an infrared gun for core temp) and <strong>antibacterial wipes</strong> to clean it between checks.</td>

    <tr>
      <td><strong>3. Hand Wash Basin</strong></td>
      <td>Hot water, <strong>liquid soap</strong>, and <strong>paper towels</strong> (no dirty tea towels hanging nearby). This basin must <strong>never</strong> be used for washing veg or filling kettles.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>4. Allergen Matrix</strong></td>
      <td>A <strong>written list</strong> of the 14 allergens in every dish you sell. This cannot just be "in the chef's head."</td>

      <td><strong>5. PPDS Label (If wrapping sandwiches)</strong></td>

<h2>Part 3: Opening Checks Before You Serve a Customer</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Fridge Check:</strong> Is the display fridge at &lt;5°C? Record it.</li>
  <li><strong>Hot Hold Check:</strong> If you have a hot cabinet, is the food holding above <strong>63°C</strong>? Record it.</li>
  <li><strong>Allergen Check:</strong> Did you change the bread supplier this morning? If the new bread has <strong>Sesame Seeds</strong>, you must update the allergen list immediately.</li>
  <li><strong>The "Oops" Plan:</strong> Do staff know what to do if the fridge breaks down? (Answer: Call manager, move food to another working fridge or discard after 4 hours). Write this down.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Part 4: The 5 Most Common HACCP Mistakes New Businesses Make</h2>

<p><strong>Mistake 1: Fridge logs left blank because "We were too busy."</strong><br />
Fix: Set a <strong>recurring alarm</strong> on your phone for 10:00 AM and 3:00 PM. Do not turn the fryer on until the log is filled.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 2: Allergen info is "in the manager's head."</strong><br />
Fix: <strong>Print it out.</strong> An EHO will test your staff by asking <em>"Does the coleslaw contain egg?"</em> If staff say "I think so," you fail the inspection.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 3: Using the same tongs for raw bacon and cooked bacon.</strong><br />
Fix: Colour code. Red handle = Raw meat. Black handle = Cooked meat.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 4: Not knowing about Natasha's Law (Pre-packed for Direct Sale).</strong><br />
Fix: If you wrap a sandwich in clingfilm for self-selection, you <strong>cannot</strong> just write "Cheese Sandwich." You must list <strong>all</strong> ingredients with <strong>allergens in bold</strong>.</p>

<p><strong>Mistake 5: Thinking "HACCP" is a one-time certificate.</strong><br />
Fix: HACCP is <strong>live</strong>. Every time you change a recipe (e.g., swapping mayonnaise for salad cream), you must update the written allergen list.</p>

<h2>Part 5: What Happens When the EHO Visits?</h2>

<p>Your first inspection will usually happen within weeks of registering (you did register at least 28 days before opening, right?).</p>

<p>They will ask for three things:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>"Show me your SFBB diary."</strong> They want to see the last 7 days of fridge checks.</li>
  <li><strong>"Show me your probe thermometer."</strong> They will watch you test it in ice water to see if it reads 0°C.</li>
  <li><strong>"Tell me what's in this sandwich."</strong> They are testing your allergen process.</li>
</ol>

<blockquote><strong>Summary:</strong> If you keep your fridges cold, your chicken hot, your chopping boards separate, and your allergen information <strong>written down</strong>, you will pass your HACCP compliance check with flying colours.</blockquote>
