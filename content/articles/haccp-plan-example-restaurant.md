<h2>HACCP Plan Example for a Restaurant</h2>
<p>Restaurant HACCP must control high-risk points across receiving, cold storage, prep, cooking, hot holding, cooling, reheating, and service. This example is designed for mixed menu kitchens with busy service periods.</p>
<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Use separate controls for cook-serve and cook-cool-reheat food lines. They carry different risks and need different monitoring.</blockquote>

<h3>Fast answer</h3>
<p>A restaurant HACCP plan should include at minimum CCPs for cooking, cooling where applicable, reheating, and hot holding, with clear records and escalation rules for failures.</p>

<h3>UK vs EU practical differences</h3>
<ul><li>Core hygiene obligations are aligned through HACCP-based procedures.</li><li>Some authorities emphasize documented allergen communication controls more heavily in mixed-service venues.</li><li>Imported ingredient traceability checks may be deeper in certain EU jurisdictions depending on supply chain complexity.</li></ul>
<h3>Compliance references</h3>
<ul><li>Regulation (EC) 852/2004 Annex II</li><li>Regulation (EU) 1169/2011 allergen information</li></ul>
<h3>Common mistakes and fixes</h3>
<ul><li>Mistake: One limit for all products. Fix: Use product-specific limits for poultry, minced meat, sauces, and ready-to-eat items.</li><li>Mistake: Cooling monitored once at the end. Fix: Log staged cooling checkpoints and total cooling time.</li><li>Mistake: Reheat done by appearance only. Fix: Probe center temperature to defined limit before service.</li></ul>
<h3>What auditors ask for as evidence</h3>
<ul><li>Opening and service temperature logs for fridges and hot holding</li><li>Cook, cool, and reheat logs for high-risk dishes</li><li>Allergen matrix linked to current menu and specials</li><li>Training records for line cooks and supervisors</li></ul>
<h3>Enforcement-style examples</h3>
<ul><li>Undercooked poultry findings commonly follow missing probe records.</li><li>Poor cooling controls in large batches are a frequent root cause in complaints.</li><li>Cross-contact failures in allergen service can lead to severe enforcement outcomes.</li></ul>
<h3>Action plan</h3>
<ol><li>Classify menu items by risk and process path.</li><li>Set CCPs for cooking, cooling, reheating, and holding.</li><li>Assign monitoring ownership by station and shift.</li><li>Run a weekly verification spot-check with management sign-off.</li><li>Review controls when menu or equipment changes.</li></ol>
<h3>Final checklist</h3>
<ul><li>Menu-specific hazard analysis complete</li><li>CCP logs completed in real time</li><li>Corrective actions documented with disposition</li><li>Verification checks completed and signed</li></ul>
<h3>Frequently asked questions</h3>
<h4>Is cooking always a CCP in restaurants?</h4>
<p>Not always, but in many high-risk operations it is treated as a CCP due to direct pathogen control.</p>
<h4>Can I combine cooling and reheating in one record?</h4>
<p>You can, if the log still captures distinct limits, timestamps, and corrective actions per step.</p>
<h4>Do I need separate controls for buffet service?</h4>
<p>Yes. Buffet and self-service add holding and cross-contact risks that need explicit controls.</p>
<h4>What records are checked first?</h4>
<p>Usually temperature records, corrective actions, allergen controls, and staff competency evidence.</p>
