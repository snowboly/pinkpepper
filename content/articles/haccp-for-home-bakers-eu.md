<h3>Introduction</h3>
<p>Many home bakers overlook a simple yet critical step: registering their food business with the local authority before selling any baked goods. This oversight can lead to immediate penalties and jeopardize your business.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always confirm your food business registration status with your local authority. This is a free and straightforward process that can save you from costly fines.</blockquote>

<h4>What you'll learn</h4>
<ul>
 <li>How to identify and control key food safety hazards in your home baking business.</li>
 <li>Key temperature and cooling requirements to prevent spoilage and pathogen growth.</li>
 <li>Labeling requirements under Natasha's Law to ensure compliance and consumer safety.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
 <li>Food business registration status with the local authority (EC 852/2004 Art. 6(2)).</li>
 <li>Proper allergen documentation and ingredient labeling for products sold (UK Food Information Regulations 2014 + Natasha's Law).</li>
 <li>Separation of domestic and business food preparation areas, ensuring no cross-contact with household items.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Home Bakers</h3>

<h4>Biological Hazards</h4>
<p>Key pathogens include <strong>Salmonella</strong>, <strong>Staphylococcus aureus</strong>, and <strong>Bacillus cereus</strong>. These can thrive in home baking environments due to shared spaces and equipment.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday when you're preparing multiple cake orders, you might use raw eggs in buttercream, increasing the risk of <strong>Salmonella</strong>. Always use Lion-marked eggs or pasteurised liquid egg in raw applications.</p>
<p>Ensure filled cakes and pastries reach a core temperature of <strong>75°C</strong> using a probe thermometer. This is critical for killing pathogens.</p>

<h4>Chemical Hazards</h4>
<p>Allergen cross-contact is a significant concern, particularly with common allergens like nuts and gluten. A shared kitchen environment means that flour and nut products can easily contaminate each other.</p>
<p>On a busy baking day, if you use the same mixing bowls for nut-free and nut-containing products without proper cleaning, you risk cross-contamination. Document cleaning procedures thoroughly.</p>
<p>All prepacked-for-direct-sale items must have a full ingredient list, with allergens emphasised, as per <strong>Regulation 1169/2011 Annex II</strong>. This keeps your customers informed and safe.</p>

<h4>Physical Hazards</h4>
<p>Physical contaminants can include items like hair, pet fur, or broken equipment. These can inadvertently end up in baked goods, especially in a domestic kitchen.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday, while decorating cakes, a pet might wander into the kitchen, risking contamination. Keep pets out of the kitchen during production and document this in your procedures.</p>
<p>Ensure all equipment is in good repair to prevent any physical hazards from broken items. Regularly check for and remove any debris or contaminants.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>In summer, buttercream and cream-filled cakes can deteriorate rapidly at ambient temperatures. If you're selling at outdoor markets, display these items in a cooled case or limit offerings to shelf-stable products only.</p>
<p>During the Christmas season, high-volume orders with multiple flavours increase the risk of allergen cross-contact. Plan production schedules to avoid simultaneous baking of allergen-containing and allergen-free batches.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Baking Temperature</strong>
        <p>Measure the core temperature of filled cakes and pastries, which must reach at least <strong>75°C</strong>.</p>
        <p>Use a calibrated probe thermometer to check the temperature at the thickest part of the item, ideally at the end of the baking time.</p>
        <p>If the temperature is below 75°C, continue baking and recheck until the correct temperature is achieved.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Cooling</strong>
        <p>Cool baked goods to ambient temperature within <strong>90 minutes</strong>.</p>
        <p>Use a timer to track the cooling period; after this time, refrigerate cream or custard-filled items at <strong>below 5°C</strong>.</p>
        <p>If cooling exceeds 90 minutes, discard the item to prevent bacterial growth.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Allergen Separation</strong>
        <p>Ensure all business baking is done with clean equipment and surfaces to prevent cross-contact with allergens.</p>
        <p>Document cleaning steps and ensure that all utensils and surfaces are cleaned before starting a new batch.</p>
        <p>If cross-contact is suspected, discard the affected batch and thoroughly clean the area before proceeding.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Labelling (Natasha's Law)</strong>
        <p>All prepacked-for-direct-sale items must have a full ingredient list with allergens highlighted.</p>
