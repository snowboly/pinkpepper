<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>One of the most frequent issues I see during audits is the pooling of meltwater around displayed fish. This not only creates an unappetizing appearance but also raises serious food safety concerns. If the drainage is blocked, your fish can sit in contaminated water, leading to cross-contamination and potential health risks.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always check for drainage blockages daily. If you find pooling water, fix the drainage immediately and keep an eye on ice levels throughout the day.</blockquote>

<h4>What You'll Learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to manage ice display to maintain fish quality and safety.</li>
    <li>Key temperature controls to ensure the integrity of your cold chain.</li>
    <li>Essential traceability requirements for shellfish and fish products.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Ice management: Is the fish embedded in flaked ice with no pooling?</li>
    <li>Cold chain integrity: Are core temperatures of fresh fish <2°C and frozen fish <-18°C?</li>
    <li>Shellfish traceability: Are classification zone documents available and complete?</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Fishmongers</h3>

<h4>Biological Hazards</h4>
<p>Key pathogens include Vibrio spp., Anisakis, Listeria monocytogenes, and Norovirus.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday when fresh oysters are sold, improper handling can lead to Norovirus contamination. Ensure that oysters are sourced from Class A or B zones and that classification zone documents are retained for a minimum of 2 years.</p>
<p>Control measures: Maintain product core temperature below 2°C for fresh fish and <em>ensure regular temperature checks</em> every 2 hours.</p>

<h4>Chemical Hazards</h4>
<p>Histamine is a significant concern for scombroid fish like tuna and mackerel.</p>
<p>On a hot summer day, if fish is left out during busy periods, histamine levels can rise rapidly. Monitor time and temperature closely.</p>
<p>Control measures: Keep fish at <2°C and ensure it is consumed or processed within 24 hours of being out of refrigeration.</p>

<h4>Physical Hazards</h4>
<p>Physical hazards can include foreign objects like bones, scales, or equipment parts.</p>
<p>On a hectic Christmas Eve, when filleting is rushed, a bone might be left in smoked salmon. Staff must remain vigilant during preparation.</p>
<p>Control measures: Implement a visual inspection protocol during gutting and filleting, ensuring all staff are trained to inspect for Anisakis and other foreign objects.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>During summer, ice melts faster due to higher ambient temperatures. Increase the frequency of ice replenishment to maintain display ice management.</p>
<p>At Christmas, high-volume sales of smoked salmon put pressure on stock rotation and use-by compliance. Implement strict stock rotation and monitor use-by dates closely, especially during peak periods.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Display Ice Management</strong>
        <p>Measure: Ensure fish is embedded in flaked ice, not just placed on top.</p>
        <p>Limit: Maintain a consistent layer of ice that covers the fish completely.</p>
        <p>Monitor: Check ice levels every hour; use a thermometer to ensure the temperature around the fish remains at or below 2°C.</p>
        <p>Corrective Action: If ice is insufficient, replenish immediately and drain any meltwater to prevent pooling.</p>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Cold Chain Integrity</strong>
        <p>Measure: Core temperature of fresh fish must be <2°C; frozen fish must be <-18°C.</p>
        <p>Monitor: Use a calibrated probe thermometer to check core temperatures at delivery and every 4 hours during storage.</p>
        <p>Corrective Action: If temperatures exceed limits, discard the product or re-chill to acceptable temperatures immediately.</p>
    </li>

    <li>
        <strong>Shellfish Traceability</strong>
        <p>Measure: Ensure all shellfish documentation includes classification zone and harvester registration number.</p>
        <p>Limit: Retain documentation for a minimum of 2 years.</p>
        <p>Monitor: Review documentation upon delivery and store it in a dedicated file for easy access.</p>
        <p>Corrective Action: If documents are missing or incomplete, do not accept the shellfish and contact the supplier for correct documentation.</p>
    </li>

    <li>
        <strong>Cross-Contamination Prevention</strong>
        <p>Measure: Use separate boards and knives for raw, cooked, and smoked fish.</p>
        <p>Limit: No shared equipment between raw and ready-to-eat (RTE) products.</p>
        <p>Monitor: Inspect equipment every shift to ensure separation is maintained.</p>
        <p>Corrective Action: If cross-contamination is suspected, cease operations, sanitize all affected equipment, and retrain staff.</p>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Mistake: Meltwater pooling around displayed fish → Fix: Ensure drainage is clear and monitor ice levels frequently.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Shellfish movement documents are missing or incomplete → Fix: Keep a checklist for required documentation and review it with every delivery.</li>
    <li>Mistake: No separate boards/knives for raw vs smoked/RTE fish → Fix: Implement a color-coded system for equipment and train staff on its use.</li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management and receiving checks are vital to maintaining food safety in your fishmongery. Every delivery is an opportunity to verify product quality and safety before it reaches your display. Implementing rigorous checks helps prevent issues down the line.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
 <li>Core temperature of fresh fish must be <2°C; frozen fish must be <-18°C.</li>
 <li>Perform a smell check; fresh seafood should have a clean, sea-like aroma, not ammonia.</li>
 <li>For shellfish, verify that all documents include the harvester registration number and classification zone.</li>
 <li>Check smoked fish for vacuum seal integrity; ensure products are within their use-by dates.</li>
 <li>For wild-caught fish intended for raw consumption, confirm that the supplier has parasite-frozen the product or that you will freeze it in-house.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
 <li>If fresh fish core temperature exceeds 2°C or frozen fish exceeds -18°C.</li>
 <li>Any signs of spoilage, such as an off smell or discoloration.</li>
 <li>Missing or incomplete shellfish movement documents.</li>
 <li>Compromised vacuum seals on smoked fish products.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always keep a thermometer at your receiving area. When you receive fish, take the core temperature immediately to avoid delays in identifying temperature abuse.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3> 
<ul> 
    <li>Check display ice management: fish embedded in flaked ice, replenish as needed.</li> 
    <li>Monitor core temperature of fresh fish: must be <2°C.</li> 
    <li>Monitor core temperature of frozen fish: must be <-18°C.</li> 
    <li>Inspect for meltwater pooling around displayed fish: ensure drainage is clear.</li> 
    <li>Verify shellfish traceability: check classification zone documents are retained.</li> 
    <li>Ensure raw, cooked, and smoked fish are physically separated in display and storage.</li> 
    <li>Check for separate boards and knives for raw vs smoked/RTE fish.</li> 
    <li>Confirm handwashing protocols are followed after handling raw fish.</li> 
</ul> 

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3> 
<ul> 
    <li>Shellfish movement documents: complete with harvester registration number and classification zone.</li> 
    <li>Temperature logs for fresh and frozen fish deliveries: core temperature readings at <2°C and <-18°C.</li> 
    <li>Ice replenishment records: frequency and quantity of ice added to displays.</li> 
    <li>Staff training records: proof of training on allergen awareness and cross-contamination protocols.</li> 
    <li>Visual inspection logs for Anisakis during gutting by filleting staff.</li> 
</ul> 

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3> 
<p>Filleting staff must understand knife hygiene between species to prevent cross-contamination. They should conduct visual inspections for Anisakis during gutting and report any concerns immediately.</p> 
<p>Counter staff need to have allergen knowledge specific to fish, crustaceans, and molluscs, recognizing each as separate allergens. This includes proper signage and communication with customers.</p> 
<p>All staff must handwash between handling raw and ready-to-eat (RTE) products without exception. Implement a clear protocol for when and how to wash hands effectively.</p> 
<h3>Conclusion</h3>

<p>Fishmongers in the EU/UK must adhere to key regulations, including EC 853/2004 Annex III for live bivalves and fishery products, EC 2073/2005 for histamine limits, and EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. III for movable premises. Compliance requires maintaining cold chain integrity with core temperatures below 2°C for fresh fish and ensuring shellfish traceability for at least two years.</p><p>Cross-contamination prevention between raw and ready-to-eat products is non-negotiable.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>

<ol>
    <li>Check your display ice management; ensure fish is embedded in flaked ice and drainage is clear.</li>
    <li>Review shellfish movement documents; ensure all are complete and retain them for a minimum of two years.</li>
    <li>Separate boards and knives for raw and ready-to-eat fish; label them clearly to avoid mix-ups.</li>
    <li>Conduct a staff training session on handwashing protocols, emphasizing the importance of washing between raw and RTE products.</li>
    <li>Inspect all incoming deliveries; verify core temperatures and check for proper documentation from suppliers.</li>
</ol>
