<p>Exporting food from Great Britain to the European Union is not one single workflow. The route changes with the product type, the animal-origin content, the processing method, the intended use, the destination member state, and the current disease or safeguard position. A shelf-stable biscuit, a dairy product, a composite ready meal, and a wild-caught fish consignment do not follow the same path.</p>

<p>This guide applies to exports from <strong>England, Scotland, and Wales</strong> into the European Union. It does not cover Northern Ireland, personal consignments, or goods that only transit through Great Britain. It reflects the public regulatory position available on 20 June 2026, but the live certificate, establishment, and entry-control requirements must still be checked again before each shipment.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Core rule:</strong> Do not book the shipment until you have confirmed the exact product route, the export certificate requirements, and the EU entry conditions for the consignment in front of you.</blockquote>

<h2>1. Classify the product properly before looking for the certificate</h2>

<p>Start with the full product specification, not the marketing name. Record:</p>

<ul>
  <li>the commodity code and commercial description;</li>
  <li>the complete ingredient list and percentage of each animal-origin ingredient;</li>
  <li>the process and heat treatment applied;</li>
  <li>whether the product is chilled, frozen, or shelf-stable;</li>
  <li>whether it is a product of animal origin, a composite product, or standard food of non-animal origin;</li>
  <li>the manufacturing establishment and any cold store or dispatch establishment involved;</li>
  <li>the destination member state and point of entry; and</li>
  <li>whether organic, catch-certificate, or other parallel controls also apply.</li>
</ul>

<p>Classification drives the route. For example, a shelf-stable mixed food may still require an EU certificate depending on the processed animal-origin ingredients it contains. A fishery product may need both SPS export certification and a separate catch-document pathway. Do not guess from the product name alone.</p>

<h2>2. Identify which export regime applies</h2>

<p>Before applying for any certificate, decide which EU import regime the product will enter under. In practice, Great Britain exporters are usually working within one of these groups:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Products of animal origin (POAO)</strong> such as meat, dairy, fish, eggs, and processed POAO;</li>
  <li><strong>Composite products</strong> containing processed animal products with plant ingredients;</li>
  <li><strong>Fishery products</strong> with potential catch-certificate requirements in parallel;</li>
  <li><strong>Organic food</strong> with an additional certificate-of-inspection route; or</li>
  <li><strong>Standard non-animal-origin food</strong> that may not need SPS certification but still needs customs, food-law, and customer-specific compliance checks.</li>
</ul>

<p>The route has to be confirmed against the current EU import rules, not only the UK export process. The export certificate is only one part of the chain. The receiving EU operator still needs the consignment to satisfy the correct entry regime on arrival.</p>

<h2>3. Confirm that Great Britain and the establishment are eligible to export</h2>

<p>For animal-origin products and many composite-product routes, the European Union must recognise both the country and the relevant establishment for that product category. That means checking whether:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Great Britain is listed for the relevant export category;</li>
  <li>the production, processing, or cold-store establishment appears on the relevant EU-approved list if the route requires it; and</li>
  <li>there are no disease restrictions, regional bans, or safeguard measures blocking the consignment.</li>
</ul>

<p>Approval for the domestic GB market is not enough by itself, and approval for one export category does not automatically cover every other category. Re-check the live position before dispatch, especially for meat, dairy, eggs, fish, and products affected by disease controls.</p>

<h2>4. Confirm who is responsible for the export paperwork</h2>

<p>Before any certificate application starts, make the responsibilities explicit between the GB exporter, the destination importer, the customs agent, and any logistics provider. At minimum, agree:</p>

<ul>
  <li>who applies for the export health certificate or other official export document;</li>
  <li>who arranges the official veterinarian or certifying officer where required;</li>
  <li>who pre-notifies the consignment in the EU system;</li>
  <li>who books the correct EU border control post;</li>
  <li>who submits the customs declaration on each side; and</li>
  <li>who carries the cost if the certificate is wrong or the consignment is rejected.</li>
</ul>

<p>Many failed exports are not caused by the law itself. They fail because nobody owns the certificate application, the TRACES notification, or the check on which establishment number actually appears on the paperwork.</p>

<h2>5. Obtain the correct certificate and supporting documents</h2>

<p>For many animal-origin exports, you will need an <strong>export health certificate (EHC)</strong> or another official certificate matched to the product and route. That certificate normally has to be supported by product and establishment information that the certifier can rely on.</p>

<p>Depending on the commodity, the export file may also need:</p>

<ul>
  <li>commercial documents and packing lists;</li>
  <li>temperature evidence or storage declarations;</li>
  <li>catch certificates and, where relevant, processing statements or storage evidence for fishery products;</li>
  <li>a certificate of inspection for organic goods;</li>
  <li>laboratory evidence if a product is moving under a special import condition; and</li>
  <li>any customer-specific declarations tied to composition or standards.</li>
</ul>

<p>Ask for a draft certificate review before final sign-off. Check the description, net weight, package count, dispatch establishment, destination details, and transport details against the commercial file. Fixing a mismatch before dispatch is easy. Fixing it after the truck reaches the EU border is not.</p>

<h2>6. Handle EU pre-notification and border-entry planning early</h2>

<p>For consignments entering the EU under SPS controls, the consignment must usually be pre-notified by the EU importer or representative in <strong>TRACES NT</strong>. The consignment must then arrive through an EU <strong>Border Control Post (BCP)</strong> designated for the exact commodity.</p>

<p>That means the export workflow has to include three linked checks before the vehicle leaves Great Britain:</p>

<ul>
  <li>the consignment has the correct signed certificate and supporting file;</li>
  <li>the importer has pre-notified the shipment in TRACES NT; and</li>
  <li>the chosen BCP is authorised for the product type and temperature regime.</li>
</ul>

<p>A certificate on its own does not get the goods through the EU border. The importer-side notification and the correct BCP route matter just as much.</p>

<h2>7. Coordinate the customs and SPS layers together</h2>

<p>The shipment has to clear both SPS and customs controls. Those are different systems, but a bad handover between them causes delays quickly. Ensure the customs broker has the right certificate references, commodity code, and route details. Ensure the importer knows which document references are attached to the TRACES notification and the import declaration.</p>

<p>Where goods move under controlled temperature or tight shelf-life windows, delays at the BCP and customs interface need to be planned into the logistics model. Do not build the entire export plan around the assumption of a frictionless border crossing.</p>

<h2>8. Know the common reasons consignments fail at the EU border</h2>

<p>In practice, the usual failure points are operational, not theoretical. Common examples include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>the wrong certificate model was used;</li>
  <li>the establishment number on the goods or documents does not match the approved list;</li>
  <li>the importer pre-notified the wrong commodity or destination details;</li>
  <li>the goods arrived at a BCP not designated for that product;</li>
  <li>catch-certificate or organic documentation was missing from the file; or</li>
  <li>disease restrictions changed between order and dispatch.</li>
</ul>

<p>Build a final export-release check around those predictable failure points. That is more valuable than adding generic process steps nobody actually reviews.</p>

<h2>9. Apply post-export controls inside the business</h2>

<p>Even after the shipment leaves, the GB exporter still needs a working file that supports traceability, customer queries, complaint handling, and possible recall or rejection events. Keep the export pack organised so you can show:</p>

<ul>
  <li>what was shipped and when;</li>
  <li>which certificate and batch references applied;</li>
  <li>which establishment handled the consignment;</li>
  <li>which customer received it;</li>
  <li>what supporting evidence was used; and</li>
  <li>what happened if the consignment was delayed, sampled, or challenged.</li>
</ul>

<p>This is where traceability, complaints, and recall systems stop being theory and become an export-control requirement in practice.</p>

<h2>10. Worked examples</h2>

<h3>Cheese exported from England to France</h3>

<p>This is a dairy POAO route. Confirm the exact certificate model, the establishment approval status, the destination BCP, and the importer&rsquo;s TRACES process. Check whether the exporter, cold store, or dispatch site appearing on the documents is the one actually approved for the route.</p>

<h3>Wild-caught fish exported from Scotland to Spain</h3>

<p>This is both a fishery-product SPS route and a potential catch-certificate route. Do not stop at the export certificate. Confirm whether the catch-document chain also needs validated evidence and whether processing or storage in a different country changes the document pack.</p>

<h3>Shelf-stable composite meal exported from Great Britain to Ireland</h3>

<p>Do not assume shelf stability removes the certificate route. Confirm the animal-origin ingredients, processing treatment, and current EU composite-product conditions before deciding whether the consignment needs a certificate.</p>

<h2>11. First-export checklist</h2>

<h3>Before accepting the order</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Confirm the commodity code and full specification.</li>
  <li>Confirm whether the product is POAO, composite, fishery, organic, or another controlled route.</li>
  <li>Check country and establishment eligibility.</li>
  <li>Confirm the destination EU member state and entry route.</li>
  <li>Confirm who owns certification, pre-notification, customs, and BCP booking.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Before dispatch</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Review the draft export certificate against the commercial file.</li>
  <li>Confirm disease restrictions and live route conditions again.</li>
  <li>Confirm the importer has arranged TRACES NT pre-notification.</li>
  <li>Confirm the BCP designation for the actual product and temperature regime.</li>
</ul>

<h3>After dispatch</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Retain the export pack in an auditable file.</li>
  <li>Track any border query or sampling event.</li>
  <li>Review certificate, customer, and logistics issues before the next shipment.</li>
</ul>
