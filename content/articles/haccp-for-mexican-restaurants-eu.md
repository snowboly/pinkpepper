<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • SFBB Adaptation for Mexican Cuisine</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Foundation</h2>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires food business operators to "put in place, implement and maintain a permanent procedure based on the HACCP principles." The seven principles are: hazard identification, CCP determination, critical limits, monitoring, corrective actions, verification, and documentation commensurate with business size.</p>

<p>The FSA's Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) framework provides pre-written HACCP-based systems. While no dedicated Mexican cuisine SFBB pack exists, operators may adapt the general catering pack supplemented with cuisine-specific hazard awareness. SFBB constitutes documented compliance when completed and maintained.</p>

<p>The European Commission's 2022 Notice (2022/C 355/01) clarifies that for certain businesses, Good Hygiene Practices are sufficient to control hazards. The Notice formalises Operational Prerequisite Programmes (OPRPs)—essential controls not suited to binary critical limits. For Mexican kitchens, bulk bean cooling and rice holding are more appropriately OPRPs than CCPs.</p>

<h2>2. Cuisine-Specific Hazards</h2>

<h3>2.1 Beans, Rice, and Cooling</h3>

<p>Research evaluating Mexican-style foodservice operations identified cooling and reheating as critical control points. Beans and rice—prepared in large batches—present the most significant microbiological hazard due to improper cooling.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Parameter</th>
      <th>Safe Threshold</th>
      <th>Observed Risk Condition</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Cooking temperature</td>
      <td>Sufficient to kill vegetative pathogens</td>
      <td>Ground meat occasionally undercooked</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Hot-holding</td>
      <td>≥60°C</td>

<p>Beans cooled in deep containers with lids represent the single most predictable hazard. Control measures include monitoring container depth, observing whether lids are used, and checking refrigeration airflow.</p>

<h3>2.2 Time-Temperature Control</h3>

<p>Inspection data from Mexican restaurant operations consistently identifies temperature control violations: hot-held items (queso, rice, beans, pulled pork) below safe temperatures, and cold-held items (salsa, guacamole, diced tomatoes) held above 5°C.</p>

<p><strong>Hot-holding:</strong> TCS foods must be maintained at ≥60°C (EU). Queso presents particular challenges due to viscosity—temperature gradients can leave surface areas below safe thresholds.</p>

<p><strong>Cold-holding:</strong> Salsa, guacamole, and prepared vegetables must be maintained at ≤5°C. Prep refrigerators during service are high-risk points.</p>

<h3>2.3 Cross-Contamination: Raw Meat Storage</h3>

<p>Inspection reports consistently identify improper raw meat storage: raw steak stored above ready-to-eat foods, raw chicken over raw beef. Annex II of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires raw meats stored below ready-to-eat foods.</p>

<p>Storage hierarchy (top to bottom):</p>

<ol>
  <li>Ready-to-eat foods (cheese, tortillas, prepared vegetables, salsas)</li>
  <li>Raw fish and seafood</li>
  <li>Raw beef and pork (whole cuts)</li>
  <li>Raw ground meats</li>
  <li>Raw poultry</li>
</ol>

<h3>2.4 Produce Safety: Salsas and Guacamole</h3>

<p>Mexican cuisine relies heavily on fresh, unheated produce: tomatoes, onions, coriander, chillies, avocados. These items receive no kill step. Control requires thorough washing, small-batch preparation, and refrigerated holding. Lime juice provides some antimicrobial effect but is not a substitute for temperature control.</p>

<h2>3. The Cooling Protocol for Beans and Rice</h2>

<p>Research confirms that foods cooled in traditional lidded deep pans cool too slowly. The following protocol addresses the specific hazard:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Step</th>
      <th>Action</th>
      <th>Critical Parameter</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>1</td>
      <td>Remove from cooker after cooking completes</td>
      <td>Do not leave at ambient</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>2</td>
      <td>Decant into shallow gastronorm containers</td>
      <td>Depth ≤5 cm</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>3</td>
      <td>Cool uncovered in refrigerator or blast chiller</td>
      <td>60°C to 20°C within 2 hours</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>4</td>
      <td>Continue refrigeration</td>
      <td>20°C to ≤5°C within further 2 hours</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>5</td>
      <td>Cover and label with date</td>
      <td>Use within 72 hours</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>6</td>
      <td>Reheat to ≥74°C core temperature</td>
      <td>Before service</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>Alternative approach: Small-batch preparation throughout service eliminates the cooling requirement for service items. End-of-service surplus must still be cooled properly or discarded.</p>

<h3>3.1 Monitoring Cooling Effectiveness</h3>

<p>Research indicates that cooling can be monitored effectively by observing container size, product depth, whether lids are used, and whether containers are stored with adequate airflow. This provides a practical, observable method without requiring continuous temperature logging of every batch.</p>

<h2>4. Documentation and Verification</h2>

<h3>4.1 Records That Attract Scrutiny</h3>

<p>Based on inspection patterns, the following records warrant particular attention:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cooling logs:</strong> Time/temperature from cooking to ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Date-marking:</strong> All items made in-house and kept >24 hours must be date-marked—beans, rice, salsas, queso, cooked meats, guacamole</li>
  <li><strong>Refrigerator temperature records:</strong> Daily min/max readings ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Hot-hold temperature checks:</strong> Queso, beans, rice, cooked meats at ≥60°C</li>
  <li><strong>Sanitizer concentration:</strong> Chlorine-based sanitizers at correct ppm; test strips must be used to verify</li>
</ul>

<h3>4.2 Internal Verification</h3>

<p>Verification (HACCP Principle 6) requires periodic evaluation:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Weekly management walk-through observing cooling practices and date-marking</li>
  <li>Monthly thermometer calibration</li>
  <li>Quarterly documentation review</li>
  <li>Verification of sanitizer concentration using test strips</li>
</ul>

<p>These activities should be recorded, demonstrating to enforcement that the system is actively managed.</p>

<h3>5.1 Queso: A Particular Challenge</h3>

<p>Queso appears repeatedly in inspection reports as a hot-holding violation. Its viscosity creates temperature stratification. Controls include stirring at minimum hourly intervals, using appropriate-sized inserts fully immersed in steam table water, and verifying temperature at surface and centre.</p>

<h3>5.2 Date-Marking Requirements</h3>

<p>Refrigerated, ready-to-eat, TCS foods prepared in-house and kept longer than 24 hours must be date-marked. This applies to cooked beans, rice, prepared salsas, guacamole, queso, and cooked meats (carnitas, barbacoa, chicken, ground beef). Maximum storage: preparation date plus 6 days.</p>

<h2>6. Staff Training Requirements</h2>

<p>Article 4 and Annex II Chapter XII of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 require food handlers to be "supervised and instructed and/or trained in food hygiene matters commensurate with their work activity."</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>All kitchen staff:</strong> Food hygiene training covering temperature control, cooling procedures, cross-contamination prevention</li>
  <li><strong>Prep staff:</strong> Specific training on produce washing, batch preparation, and date-marking</li>
  <li><strong>Front-of-house:</strong> Allergen communication and the absolute prohibition on guessing</li>
</ul>

<p>Research recommends that the Person in Charge hold a food safety certification. Training should be documented and refreshed at least annually.</p>
