<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • SFBB for Chinese Cuisine</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Foundation</h2>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 mandates that food business operators "put in place, implement and maintain a permanent procedure based on the HACCP principles." The seven principles are: hazard identification, CCP determination, critical limits, monitoring, corrective actions, verification, and documentation commensurate with business size.</p>

<p>The FSA's Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) pack for Chinese cuisine provides a pre-written HACCP-based system. When completed and maintained, this satisfies the Article 5 requirement for documented compliance.</p>

<p>The European Commission's 2022 Notice (2022/C 355/01) clarifies that for certain businesses, "Good Hygiene Practices are sufficient to control hazards." This flexibility is particularly relevant where cooking is immediate and holding times are minimal.</p>

<h2>2. Cuisine-Specific Hazards</h2>

<h3>2.1 Rice and Bacillus cereus</h3>

<p>The most significant hazard in Chinese operations is <em>Bacillus cereus</em> in cooked rice. Spores survive cooking and, if rice cools too slowly, germinate to produce heat-stable toxins.</p>

<p>Rice left in a cooker on "warm" overnight is hazardous. Rice cooled in deep gastro trays is hazardous. Fried rice from inadequately cooled rice is hazardous regardless of wok temperature.</p>

<h3>2.2 Marinade Management</h3>

<p>Raw meat marinades become contaminated with surface pathogens. Reserved marinade used as finishing sauce without boiling creates cross-contamination. Soy (regulated allergen) and sesame (ninth most prevalent EU allergen) are ubiquitous and cannot be removed once incorporated.</p>

<h3>2.3 Wok Service</h3>

<p>The hazard arises in mise en place: pre-cooked meats and sauces held at ambient temperature during service. Toxins from <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em> produced during holding are heat-stable.</p>

<h3>2.4 Allergen Communication</h3>

<p>EU Regulation 1169/2011 requires allergen information for non-prepacked foods. Chinese menus present particular challenges: soy sauce contains wheat (gluten) and soya; oyster sauce contains molluscs; hoisin contains sesame.</p>

<h2>3. The Cooling Protocol</h2>

<p>Given rice's centrality to both cuisine and hazard:</p>

<p>Alternative: small-batch cooking throughout service rather than single bulk cook.</p>

<h2>4. Documentation and Verification</h2>

<p>SFBB records must be completed contemporaneously—not retrospectively before inspection. Most scrutinised records:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Rice cooling logs</li>
  <li>Refrigerator temperature records (≤5°C)</li>
  <li>Hot-hold temperature checks (≥63°C)</li>
  <li>Cleaning schedules for rice cookers and wok areas</li>
</ul>

<p>Internal verification includes weekly management walk-through, monthly thermometer calibration, and quarterly documentation review.</p>
