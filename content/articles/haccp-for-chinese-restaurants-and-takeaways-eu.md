<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • Food Safety Management Systems • SFBB for Chinese Cuisine</em></p>

<h3>Editor's Note</h3>

<p>Chinese cuisine presents a distinctive set of food safety challenges within the European regulatory framework. The very characteristics that define this culinary tradition—high-temperature wok cooking, rapid cooling of par-cooked ingredients, batch preparation of rice and sauces, and the widespread use of marinades and pre-prepared components—introduce hazards that differ materially from those encountered in Western kitchens. While the foundational legislation remains Regulation (EC) No 852/2004, its application to a Chinese takeaway or restaurant requires careful, cuisine-specific interpretation.</p>

<p>This guide addresses that interpretative gap. It is structured for the proprietor who understands their kitchen intimately but may find the language of Codex Alimentarius opaque, and for the environmental health officer seeking to understand why certain risks manifest differently in this setting. The objective is not to alter the law but to illuminate its practical operation amid woks, steamers, and rice cookers.</p>

<h2>1. The Regulatory Foundation: What the Law Actually Requires</h2>

<h3>1.1 The Core Obligation</h3>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 mandates that every food business operator "put in place, implement and maintain a permanent procedure or procedures based on the HACCP principles". The language is deliberately flexible—"procedures based on" rather than "a HACCP plan"—acknowledging that small and micro-enterprises cannot reasonably be expected to operate the same documentation systems as multinational food manufacturers.</p>

<p>The seven HACCP principles, as codified in Article 5(2), are:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Hazard identification</strong> — identify any hazards that must be prevented, eliminated or reduced to acceptable levels</li>
  <li><strong>Critical Control Point (CCP) determination</strong> — identify the steps at which control is essential</li>
  <li><strong>Critical limits</strong> — establish thresholds separating acceptability from unacceptability</li>
  <li><strong>Monitoring procedures</strong> — establish and implement effective monitoring at CCPs</li>
  <li><strong>Corrective actions</strong> — determine what to do when monitoring shows deviation</li>
  <li><strong>Verification</strong> — establish procedures to confirm the system works</li>
  <li><strong>Documentation and record-keeping</strong> — commensurate with the nature and size of the business</li>
</ol>

<p>For the Chinese takeaway owner, the final principle contains the operative phrase: <em>commensurate with the nature and size of the business</em>. This is not a concession; it is a statutory recognition that a takeaway employing four people cannot maintain the documentary apparatus of a food processing plant.</p>

<h3>1.2 The Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) Route</h3>

<p>The Food Standards Agency has developed the Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) pack specifically for Chinese cuisine. This is not an alternative to HACCP—it is a pre-written HACCP-based system designed to satisfy Article 5 requirements without requiring the operator to construct hazard analyses from scratch. The Chinese cuisine pack addresses operational realities specific to the sector: bulk rice cooking, high-volume wok service, and the extensive use of pre-marinated meats.</p>

<p>Crucially, SFBB constitutes documented compliance. When an environmental health officer requests evidence of a food safety management system, a completed and maintained SFBB folder meets the Article 5(4)(a) requirement to "provide the competent authority with evidence of their compliance".</p>

<h3>1.3 The EU Flexibility Framework</h3>

<p>The European Commission's 2022 Notice on the implementation of food safety management systems (2022/C 355/01) provides authoritative guidance on flexibility. It clarifies that for certain food businesses, "it is not possible to identify critical control points and that, in some cases, GHP are sufficient to control the hazards". This is particularly relevant to takeaways where cooking is immediate and holding times are minimal.</p>

<p>The notice also formalizes the concept of <strong>Operational Prerequisite Programmes (OPRPs)</strong>—control measures that are essential to food safety but differ from CCPs in that they cannot be monitored through absolute critical limits. For Chinese kitchens, cooling of cooked rice is more properly classified as an OPRP than a CCP: it is essential, but monitoring is procedural rather than binary.</p>

<h2>2. Cuisine-Specific Hazard Analysis</h2>

<p>The starting point of any HACCP-based system is hazard identification. Chinese cooking introduces several hazards that are either amplified or uniquely presented by traditional methods.</p>

<h3>2.1 The Rice Problem: Bacillus cereus</h3>

<p>The most significant microbiological hazard in Chinese restaurant operations is not undercooked meat—it is <strong>Bacillus cereus</strong> in cooked rice. This spore-forming pathogen survives normal cooking temperatures and, if rice is cooled too slowly, germinates and produces heat-stable toxins that subsequent reheating cannot destroy.</p>

<p>Research conducted in Cantonese-style restaurants established several critical parameters that remain the operational standard:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Parameter</th>
      <th>Safe Threshold</th>
      <th>Observed Risk Condition</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Cooking temperature</td>
      <td>Exceeds 93°C</td>
      <td>Spores survive cooking</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Hot-holding temperature</td>
      <td>≥55°C (131°F)</td>
      <td>Below this, growth occurs</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Cooling layer depth</td>
      <td>&lt;9 cm (3.5 inches)</td>
      <td>Thicker layers cool too slowly</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Frying/re-frying temperature</td>
      <td>≥74°C (165°F) per grain</td>
      <td>Inadequate for toxin destruction</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>The practical implications are direct: rice left in a rice cooker on "warm" setting overnight is a hazard. Rice cooled in a deep gastro tray rather than a shallow gastronorm is a hazard. Fried rice prepared from rice that was inadequately cooled is a hazard regardless of wok temperature.</p>

<h3>2.2 Marinade Management: Cross-Contamination and Allergens</h3>

<p>Chinese cuisine makes extensive use of marinades containing soy sauce, sesame oil, oyster sauce, and cornflour. Three distinct hazards arise:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Biological</strong>: Raw meat marinades become contaminated with pathogens from the meat surface. If reserved marinade is used as a finishing sauce without boiling, cross-contamination occurs.</li>
  <li><strong>Chemical/Allergen</strong>: Soy (soya) is a regulated allergen under EU Regulation 1169/2011. Sesame is the ninth most prevalent allergen in the EU. Both are ubiquitous in Chinese cooking and cannot be "removed" from a dish once incorporated.</li>
  <li><strong>Physical</strong>: Cornflour slurries left uncovered may attract foreign bodies; marinade containers reused without proper cleaning introduce chemical residues.</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.3 High-Volume Wok Service: Time-Temperature Abuse</h3>

<p>The wok station is the heart of the Chinese kitchen and, paradoxically, the locus of both safety and risk. Proper wok cooking achieves temperatures sufficient to eliminate vegetative pathogens. The hazard arises in the <strong>mise en place</strong>: pre-cooked meats, par-cooked vegetables, and prepared sauces held at ambient temperature during service.</p>

<p>The "danger zone" of 5°C–63°C applies. A tray of pre-cooked char siu held beside the wok for two hours of dinner service may exceed permissible time-temperature parameters even if it will be reheated in the wok, because any toxins produced during holding—particularly from <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em>—are heat-stable.</p>

<h3>2.4 Allergen Communication: The Menu as a Legal Document</h3>

<p>Chinese restaurant menus present particular allergen communication challenges. Many dishes are composites of multiple components, each with its own allergen profile. Soy sauce contains wheat (gluten) as well as soya. Oyster sauce contains molluscs. Hoisin sauce contains sesame paste. Spring roll wrappers contain wheat and egg.</p>

<p>EU Regulation 1169/2011 requires that allergen information be provided for non-prepacked foods either in writing or orally with clear signposting to obtain it. The SFBB Chinese cuisine pack includes dedicated allergen charts, but their effective use requires that the kitchen accurately knows the composition of every sauce and marinade.</p>

<h2>3. Constructing the Food Safety Management System</h2>

<h3>3.1 The SFBB Chinese Cuisine Framework</h3>

<p>The SFBB pack for Chinese cuisine is structured around four key safety management areas:</p>

<p><strong>1. Cross-contamination</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Separation of raw meat preparation from ready-to-eat food assembly</li>
  <li>Dedicated chopping boards (colour-coded system)</li>
  <li>Proper cleaning and disinfection of wok stations between allergen-containing dishes</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>2. Cleaning</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Effective cleaning of extraction systems to prevent grease accumulation and pest attraction</li>
  <li>Daily cleaning schedules for rice cookers and steamers, which harbour starch residues that support bacterial growth</li>
  <li>Deep cleaning protocols for wok ranges</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>3. Chilling</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Cooling procedures specifically addressing rice, stocks, and braised meats</li>
  <li>Refrigerator temperature monitoring (≤5°C legal requirement; ≤4°C best practice)</li>
  <li>Date-coding of prepared components</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>4. Cooking</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Verification that reheated rice reaches 75°C minimum</li>
  <li>Visual checks for thorough cooking of chicken and minced meat products</li>
  <li>Oil management (frying oil quality and change frequency)</li>
</ul>

<h3>3.2 Documentation That Satisfies Enforcement</h3>

<p>The SFBB pack contains daily and weekly records. To satisfy an environmental health officer, these records must be <strong>completed contemporaneously</strong>—not retrospectively filled before an announced inspection. The most scrutinized records in Chinese takeaways are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rice cooling logs</strong> (time and temperature from cooking to ≤8°C)</li>
  <li><strong>Refrigerator temperature records</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Hot-hold temperature checks</strong> for items in bains-marie</li>
  <li><strong>Cleaning schedules</strong> for rice cookers and wok areas</li>
</ul>

<p>The legal requirement for retention is not specified in primary legislation but is typically expected to be a minimum of three months or the shelf life of the product plus six months.</p>

<h3>3.3 The Cooling Protocol in Detail</h3>

<p>Given the centrality of rice to both cuisine and hazard, the cooling protocol warrants precise articulation:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Step</th>
      <th>Action</th>
      <th>Critical Parameter</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>1</td>
      <td>Remove rice from cooker immediately after cooking cycle completes</td>
      <td>Do not leave on "warm" setting for extended periods</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>2</td>
      <td>Decant into shallow containers</td>
      <td>Depth &lt;9 cm (3.5 inches)</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>3</td>
      <td>Cool within 90 minutes</td>
      <td>From ≥93°C to ≤20°C</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>4</td>
      <td>Refrigerate at ≤5°C</td>
      <td>Within a further 90 minutes (total cooling period &lt;3 hours)</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>5</td>
      <td>Label with production date</td>
      <td>Use within 24–48 hours</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>For operators who find this protocol impractical during service, the alternative is <strong>small-batch cooking</strong>: preparing rice at intervals throughout service rather than a single bulk cook in the morning.</p>

<h2>4. Verification: What the Auditor Looks For</h2>

<h3>4.1 The EHO Inspection Perspective</h3>

<p>When an environmental health officer inspects a Chinese restaurant or takeaway, they are trained to observe specific compliance indicators. Understanding this perspective allows the operator to prioritize resources effectively.</p>

<p><strong>High-priority visual indicators:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Rice storage: presence of bulk rice in deep containers in refrigeration suggests inadequate cooling practices</li>
  <li>Marinade handling: same container used for raw meat marinade and finished sauce</li>
  <li>Allergen management: absence of accessible allergen information</li>
  <li>Documentation: incomplete or "batch-completed" records</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>High-priority documentary indicators:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Gaps in temperature records corresponding to busy periods</li>
  <li>Absence of corrective action notations when deviations are recorded</li>
  <li>No verification of thermometer calibration</li>
</ul>

<h3>4.2 Internal Verification: Making the System Self-Sustaining</h3>

<p>Verification is HACCP Principle 6: "establishing procedures, which shall be carried out regularly, to verify that the measures...are working effectively". For a Chinese takeaway, practical verification includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Weekly walk-through</strong>: The proprietor or manager physically observes cooling practices, checks date labels, and verifies that allergen information is being communicated.</li>
  <li><strong>Monthly thermometer calibration</strong>: Using ice-point (0°C) and boiling-point (100°C) methods to confirm probe accuracy.</li>
  <li><strong>Quarterly documentation review</strong>: Confirming that records are complete and that corrective actions were documented when deviations occurred.</li>
</ul>

<p>These activities should themselves be recorded. The record of verification demonstrates to enforcement authorities that the system is actively managed, not merely filed.</p>

<h2>5. Post-Brexit Divergence: UK vs EU Requirements</h2>

<h3>5.1 Current Alignment</h3>

<p>As of 2026, the substantive hygiene requirements in Great Britain remain aligned with Regulation (EC) No 852/2004, which was retained in UK law as retained EU legislation. The requirement for HACCP-based procedures under Article 5 remains unchanged in both jurisdictions.</p>

<h3>5.2 Operational Differences</h3>

<p>The principal divergence is administrative rather than substantive:</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Aspect</th>
      <th>EU Position</th>
      <th>UK Position</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Enforcement body</td>
      <td>Member state competent authorities</td>
      <td>FSA / Local Authorities</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Guidance framework</td>
      <td>2022/C 355/01 Commission Notice</td>
      <td>SFBB (FSA-developed)</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Allergen legislation</td>
      <td>EU 1169/2011</td>
      <td>UK Food Information Regulations 2014 (as amended)</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>For the operator, the practical consequence is that compliance with SFBB satisfies UK enforcement expectations. An operator seeking to demonstrate compliance across both jurisdictions should maintain the SFBB framework while being aware that EU member state inspectors may be less familiar with the specific format—though the underlying principles are identical.</p>

<h3>5.3 Northern Ireland</h3>

<p>Under the Windsor Framework, Northern Ireland continues to apply EU food hygiene legislation directly. Operators in Northern Ireland must comply with Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 as applied in EU law, not as retained UK legislation. In practice, the SFBB Chinese cuisine pack remains acceptable as a documented food safety management system.</p>

<h2>6. Practical Implementation: From Theory to Daily Operation</h2>

<h3>6.1 Staff Training</h3>

<p>Article 4 and Annex II Chapter XII of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 require that food handlers be "supervised and instructed and/or trained in food hygiene matters commensurate with their work activity". For a Chinese kitchen, this means:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Wok chefs</strong>: Training specific to time-temperature control, rice cooling, and allergen awareness</li>
  <li><strong>Kitchen porters</strong>: Training on cleaning procedures for starch-heavy equipment</li>
  <li><strong>Front-of-house</strong>: Training on allergen communication and the legal obligation to provide accurate information</li>
</ul>

<p>The FSA provides a free introductory guide, "Working with food? What you need to know before you start", available in multiple languages including Chinese.</p>

<h3>6.2 Supplier Assurance</h3>

<p>Chinese restaurants rely heavily on specialist suppliers for ingredients such as fermented sauces, dried mushrooms, and specific vegetable varieties. The traceability requirement of Article 18 of Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 applies: operators must be able to identify from whom they purchased ingredients and to whom they supplied products.</p>

<p>For imported specialty ingredients, the operator should retain:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Supplier name and address</li>
  <li>Nature of products supplied</li>
  <li>Date of transaction/delivery</li>
  <li>Batch or lot codes where available</li>
</ul>

<h3>6.3 Takeaway-Specific Considerations</h3>

<p>The takeaway model introduces additional variables: extended hot-holding for delivery, packaging that may affect temperature, and the time interval between cooking and consumption.</p>

<p><strong>Hot-holding for delivery</strong>: Insulated bags should maintain food at ≥63°C. The SFBB pack includes checks for delivery vehicle and bag condition. Food that falls below 63°C during delivery should not be held for later use.</p>

<p><strong>Allergen communication for online orders</strong>: The legal obligation to provide allergen information extends to food sold online. Platforms should either display allergen information directly or provide clear signposting to obtain it from the restaurant.</p>
