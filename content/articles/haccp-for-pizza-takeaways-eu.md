<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>Many pizza takeaways fail to monitor the temperature of their make-line toppings, leading to unsafe food practices. This oversight can result in bacterial growth, jeopardizing customer safety and your business reputation. Tighten your procedures to avoid common pitfalls that could lead to audit failures.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always document the temperature of your make-line toppings every two hours, especially during peak hours. Use a probe thermometer to verify temperatures are consistently below 8°C.</blockquote>

<h4>What You'll Learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to manage dough safely to prevent Bacillus cereus contamination.</li>
    <li>Key temperature controls for toppings and delivery to ensure food safety.</li>
    <li>How to create an allergen matrix for your menu to protect customers.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Make-line temperature logs to ensure toppings are kept below 8°C.</li>
    <li>Storage practices, particularly raw chicken above ready-to-eat toppings.</li>
    <li>Time management for dough, ensuring it is portioned within 2 hours of mixing.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Pizza Takeaways</h3>

<h4>Biological Hazards</h4>
<p><strong>Key Pathogens:</strong> Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus cereus, Salmonella, Listeria monocytogenes, Clostridium perfringens.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday when staff are rushed, toppings may sit at ambient temperature for extended periods. This increases the risk of Staphylococcus aureus from hand contact and Bacillus cereus from dough balls held out too long.</p>
<p>Control measures: Keep toppings on the make-line at <8°C and ensure dough balls are portioned and refrigerated within 2 hours of mixing.</p>

<h4>Chemical Hazards</h4>
<p><strong>Potential Chemicals:</strong> Allergen cross-contamination (gluten, milk, eggs), cleaning agents.</p>
<p>On a busy Friday night when staff are multitasking, a pizza maker might inadvertently use the same utensils for raw chicken and a gluten-free pizza. This can lead to serious allergen exposure for sensitive customers.</p>
<p>Control measures: Implement an allergen matrix for menu items and train staff to wash hands and change utensils between handling raw and ready-to-eat foods.</p>

<h4>Physical Hazards</h4>
<p><strong>Common Physical Contaminants:</strong> Foreign objects from the kitchen (e.g., pieces of equipment, hair).</p>
<p>On a hectic Saturday when multiple orders are prepared simultaneously, a pizza cutter may accidentally drop a piece of plastic into a pizza. This poses a choking hazard.</p>
<p>Control measures: Regularly inspect the kitchen for loose items and ensure staff are trained to maintain a clean workspace, checking for foreign objects before serving.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>Friday and Saturday nights bring peak demand, which leads to higher make-line temperatures and potential for staff to skip handwashing between tasks. Dough balls and sides like garlic bread may be left out too long, increasing the risk of Bacillus cereus.</p>
<p>In summer, longer delivery times due to traffic can result in lukewarm food. Use insulated bags and limit delivery radius to ensure temperatures remain above 63°C upon delivery.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Pizza Oven Temperature Verification</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Centre temperature of the pizza must reach at least 75°C.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Use a calibrated food probe; check random pizzas during peak service (every 10 pizzas).</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If the centre temperature is below 75°C, continue cooking until the target is reached before serving.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Dough Management</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Bulk dough must be portioned and refrigerated within 2 hours of mixing.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Use a timer to track dough mixing; check logs every shift.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If dough exceeds 2 hours at ambient, discard it immediately.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Topping Bar Temperature</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Toppings (pre-sliced meats, cheese, vegetables) must remain below 8°C.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Use a digital thermometer; check every 2 hours during service.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If toppings exceed 8°C, discard those items and replace them with fresh stock.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Delivery Holding Temperature</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Boxed pizzas must be delivered at a minimum temperature of 63°C.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Use a probe thermometer on the first pizza out of the oven and every 5th pizza after.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If below 63°C, reheat the pizza to meet the temperature requirement before delivery.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Mistake: Topping bar temperature not monitored → Fix: Implement a log system to check and record temperatures every 2 hours.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Raw chicken stored above ready-to-eat toppings → Fix: Rearrange storage to ensure raw chicken is always below ready-to-eat items in the fridge.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Dough balls left out for hours without time logging → Fix: Use timers to ensure dough is portioned and refrigerated within 2 hours of mixing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management is critical for maintaining food safety in your pizza takeaway. Regular checks on incoming products help prevent contamination and ensure quality. Establish clear criteria for receiving checks to uphold your standards.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Pre-sliced meats (pepperoni, ham, salami): delivery temperature <5°C.</li>
    <li>Opened packages of pre-sliced meats must be used within 48 hours.</li>
    <li>Cheese (mozzarella): delivery temperature <5°C; check for bloating or gas in packaging.</li>
    <li>Raw chicken: delivery temperature <4°C; sourced from an approved supplier with Salmonella testing.</li>
    <li>Flour and dry goods: verify sealed packaging and check for pest evidence.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature of pre-sliced meats exceeds 5°C upon delivery.</li>
    <li>Bloating or gas present in cheese packaging indicates spoilage.</li>
    <li>Raw chicken delivered above 4°C or lacks proper Salmonella testing documentation.</li>
    <li>Visible pest evidence in flour or dry goods packaging.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always have a thermometer ready at the receiving area. If a delivery doesn't meet your temperature requirements, document it and inform your supplier immediately.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Check topping bar temperature: <strong>must be < 8°C</strong>.</li>
    <li>Probe random pizzas during rush: <strong>ensure centre reaches > 75°C</strong> when loaded with raw toppings.</li>
    <li>Monitor dough management: <strong>portion and refrigerate within 2 hours</strong> of mixing.</li>
    <li>Log ambient holding times for dough balls and sides: <strong>must not exceed 4 hours</strong>.</li>
    <li>Verify delivery holding: <strong>customer receives food > 63°C</strong> upon delivery.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature logs for topping bar and pizza oven.</li>
    <li>Dough management records: time of mixing and refrigeration.</li>
    <li>Delivery temperature checks: records of food temperature upon dispatch.</li>
    <li>Allergen matrix for menu items, especially for custom pizzas.</li>
    <li>Supplier delivery checks: temperature at receipt and use-by dates for opened packages.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Pizza makers: wash hands between topping changes, especially after handling raw chicken.</li>
    <li>Probe random pizza centres during busy periods to ensure safe cooking temperatures.</li>
    <li>Delivery drivers: use insulated bags, deliver within <strong>30 minutes</strong> of boxing, and report any returned orders for discard.</li>
    <li>All staff: must understand allergens present in the menu-gluten, milk, egg, possible fish (anchovies), celery (some sauces), and mustard.</li>
    <li>New starters: focus on dough time-management; portion within <strong>2 hours</strong> and avoid leaving bulk dough in a warm kitchen.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>

<p>Understanding and implementing key regulatory requirements is vital for pizza takeaways. Maintain topping bar temperatures below 8°C (EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(5)), ensure food delivery vehicles keep food above 63°C (EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IV(2)), and provide allergen information for all made-to-order pizzas (Regulation 1169/2011 Art.</p><p>44). Regular monitoring and documentation will help mitigate food safety risks.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Check and log topping bar temperatures during peak hours this week.</li>
    <li>Implement a strict handwashing schedule for pizza makers between topping changes.</li>
    <li>Verify supplier delivery temperatures for pre-sliced meats and chicken.</li>
    <li>Create and display an allergen matrix for your menu items.</li>
    <li>Train staff on dough management-portion within 2 hours of mixing.</li>
</ol>
