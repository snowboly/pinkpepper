<h3>Introduction</h3>
<p>Imagine a busy lunch hour where children eagerly queue for their meals, but a single infected staff member unknowingly spreads Norovirus, leading to a widespread outbreak. This scenario is all too common in school canteens, where the stakes are high, and the risks are often underestimated.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always log temperatures for hot-held items, especially during second and third sittings. If a child gets sick, your records will be your best defense.</blockquote>

<h4>What You'll Learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to effectively manage allergens in compliance with Natasha's Law.</li>
    <li>Critical control points for cooking, holding, and cooling food safely.</li>
    <li>Staff training requirements and best practices for allergen awareness.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Allergen labeling compliance, particularly for grab-and-go items.</li>
    <li>Temperature logs for hot-held items during service periods.</li>
    <li>Documented proof of staff allergen training, especially for supply and agency staff.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to School Canteens</h3>

<p><strong>Biological Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Salmonella:</strong> Commonly found in undercooked chicken and eggs. On a busy Monday when chicken curry is prepared in bulk, if the thickest part of the chicken is only cooked to 70°C for 1 minute, Salmonella can survive.</li>
    <li><strong>Bacillus cereus:</strong> Often associated with rice and pasta dishes. On a hectic lunch service, if rice is cooked at 8 am and held at ambient temperature until 1 pm, Bacillus cereus spores can multiply, leading to foodborne illness.</li>
    <li><strong>Clostridium perfringens:</strong> Typically found in large batch stews. If a casserole is cooled from 60°C to 20°C in 3 hours instead of the required 90 minutes, this organism can proliferate, posing a risk for students.</li>
    <li><strong>Norovirus:</strong> Easily transmitted by infected staff. If a lunch supervisor with symptoms handles food without proper hygiene, they can infect hundreds of children in a single sitting.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Chemical Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Allergens:</strong> Common allergens include peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, gluten, and sesame. If a supply/agency staff member serves a nut-containing dish without checking the allergen register, it can lead to severe anaphylactic reactions.</li>
    <li><strong>Cleaning agents:</strong> Residual chemicals from cleaning products can contaminate food. If a surface is wiped with a chemical solution not properly rinsed, it can lead to chemical contamination of food items.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Physical Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Foreign objects:</strong> Items like metal fragments from equipment can find their way into food. On a busy Friday when equipment is being hastily cleaned, a metal shard can fall into a salad, posing a choking hazard.</li>
    <li><strong>Improperly stored utensils:</strong> Utensils left in food storage can introduce contaminants. If a ladle used for serving soup is left in the pot, it can lead to cross-contamination.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>

<p>During the autumn term, Norovirus outbreaks peak. Enforce rigorous handwashing protocols and exclude symptomatic staff to prevent widespread transmission.</p>

<p>In the summer term, warm kitchens and limited air conditioning can push food through the danger zone more rapidly. Keep prepared food below 5°C or above 63°C to mitigate risks associated with higher ambient temperatures.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Core Cooking Temperature</strong>
        <p>Measure the core temperature of all meat dishes, aiming for a minimum of 75°C for at least 2 seconds.</p>
        <p>Use a calibrated digital probe thermometer to check the thickest part of each batch, logging the temperature for every dish.</p>
        <p>If the temperature is below 75°C, continue cooking until the correct temperature is reached, then recheck before serving.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Hot Holding During Service</strong>
        <p>Maintain hot-held food at a temperature greater than 63°C throughout service.</p>
        <p>Use an infrared thermometer to monitor food in the bain-marie every 30 minutes, logging the temperatures.</p>
        <p>If the temperature drops below 63°C, reheat the food to 75°C before serving.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Cooling Large Batches</strong>
        <p>Cool food from 63°C to below 5°C within 90 minutes.</p>
        <p>Partition food into shallow containers (less than 50mm depth) and use a blast chiller for rapid cooling, monitoring with a probe thermometer.</p>
        <p>If the food does not reach below 5°C within 90 minutes, discard it to prevent bacterial growth.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Allergen Management</strong>
        <p>Ensure all prepacked-for-direct-sale items are individually labelled with complete ingredient and allergen information.</p>
        <p>Check labels upon receipt from suppliers and before serving, keeping a documented record of compliance.</p>
        <p>If any item is found without proper labelling, remove it from service and contact the supplier for compliant products.</p>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> No individual allergen labels on grab-and-go items (sandwiches, wraps, salad pots). 
        <br><strong>Fix:</strong> Implement Natasha's Law compliance by ensuring every item has a full ingredient and allergen list before service.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Missing temperature logs for hot-held items during second/third sitting. 
        <br><strong>Fix:</strong> Assign a staff member to take and log temperatures every 30 minutes during service.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Rice cooked in bulk at 8am, served at 1pm with no time or temperature monitoring in between. 
        <br><strong>Fix:</strong> Monitor and log time and temperature at regular intervals, ensuring it is kept above 63°C or cooled correctly after cooking.
    </li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management and receiving checks play a critical role in maintaining food safety in school canteens. Properly assessing deliveries ensures that the food served to children meets safety and allergen standards. Follow the guidelines below to mitigate risks associated with foodborne illnesses and allergen exposure.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Pre-made items (e.g., sandwiches, salad pots): Verify Natasha's Law compliant labelling from the supplier.</li>
    <li>Allergen declarations: Obtain and file written allergen specifications for every ingredient, updating when suppliers change.</li>
    <li>Meat/poultry: Ensure delivery temperature is <5°C upon receipt; maintain an approved supplier list reviewed annually.</li>
    <li>Milk and dairy: Check pasteurisation status and ensure delivery temperature is <5°C.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature of meat/poultry exceeds 5°C upon delivery.</li>
    <li>Pre-packed items lack individual allergen labels under Natasha's Law.</li>
    <li>Milk and dairy products show signs of spoilage or improper pasteurisation.</li>
    <li>Missing or incomplete allergen specifications for any ingredient.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always keep a temperature log for deliveries; this documentation will support your decisions during audits.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Check core cooking temperature: 75°C for 2 seconds, probe the thickest part of every batch.</li>
    <li>Hot holding temperature: maintain above 63°C during service.</li>
    <li>Cooling large batches: ensure cooling from 63°C to <5°C within 90 minutes using shallow containers.</li>
    <li>Verify individual allergen labels on all grab-and-go items (sandwiches, wraps, salad pots).</li>
    <li>Monitor handwashing compliance among staff, especially during peak Norovirus season.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature logs for hot-held items during all service sittings.</li>
    <li>Cooling records for large batches, including time and temperature checks.</li>
    <li>Documentation of allergen training for all kitchen staff, including agency staff.</li>
    <li>Supplier verification records for pre-made items, ensuring compliance with Natasha's Law.</li>
    <li>Daily allergen checks against the menu, signed off by the head cook.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<p>All kitchen staff must complete allergen awareness training (Level 2 minimum) and attend annual refreshers. Document attendance for all training sessions.</p>
<p>Supply and agency staff require an induction covering allergen protocols before they handle any food. Obtain a sign-off for their completion.</p>
<p>Lunch supervisors must be trained to recognize anaphylaxis symptoms, know the location of EpiPens, and understand emergency protocols. The head cook is responsible for conducting daily allergen checks against the menu and cross-referencing with the pupil allergy register.</p>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>

<p>School canteens must comply with key regulations, including temperature control requirements outlined in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(5), allergen labelling as specified in Regulation 1169/2011 Art. 44 and UK Natasha's Law, and staff training mandates detailed in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch.</p><p>XII. Every kitchen should also adhere to the School Food Standards (England) 2014 and Healthy Eating in Schools (Wales) 2013 for nutritional guidelines. Regular audits and proper documentation are non-negotiable for maintaining food safety and protecting children's health.</p>
