<div class="pp-article-callout"><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> If your team cannot walk the process in the same order as the diagram, the flow diagram is not finished yet.</div>

<p>A HACCP process flow diagram should do one job well: show every meaningful step in your operation clearly enough that you can identify hazards, set controls, and explain the process to an auditor without guessing. Many diagrams fail because they are copied from a template, miss rework loops, or stop at broad headings such as “prepare”, “cook”, and “serve”.</p>

<p>This guide gives you a practical method for building a process flow diagram that works for small food businesses, manufacturers, catering teams, and multi-step kitchens. If you still need the wider plan structure, start with our <a href="/resources/haccp-plan-template">HACCP plan template</a> or use the <a href="/features/haccp-plan-generator">HACCP plan generator</a> once the process map is clear.</p>

<h2>What a HACCP process flow diagram needs to show</h2>

<p>A useful flow diagram follows the product from incoming goods to dispatch or service. It should cover the real route the food takes, not the route management thinks it takes.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Receipt of ingredients and packaging</li>
  <li>Storage, including chilled, frozen, ambient, or segregated allergen storage</li>
  <li>Preparation stages such as thawing, washing, mixing, trimming, or portioning</li>
  <li>Heat treatment, cooling, reheating, hot holding, or cold display where relevant</li>
  <li>Packaging, labelling, dispatch, service, or customer handover</li>
  <li>Any rework, return, decanting, or temporary holding steps</li>
</ul>

<p>If a step changes food safety risk, time, temperature, allergen status, traceability, or product identity, it belongs on the diagram.</p>

<h2>Who should help create it</h2>

<p>Do not leave the diagram to one compliance person working from memory. The best diagrams are built with the people who run the process every day.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Production or kitchen staff who know the actual work sequence</li>
  <li>Quality or food safety staff who understand hazards and records</li>
  <li>Supervisors who know changeovers, staffing shortcuts, and busy-period workarounds</li>
  <li>Engineering or maintenance staff if equipment creates holding, timing, or calibration dependencies</li>
</ul>

<p>This matters because auditors often spot weak diagrams when the paperwork says one thing but the line or kitchen behaves differently.</p>

<h2>How to build the diagram step by step</h2>

<h3>1. Pick one product family or process at a time</h3>

<p>Do not try to map your whole site in one chart. Start with a product or product family that genuinely shares the same route and controls. If your sandwiches, soups, and bakery products move differently, they need separate diagrams.</p>

<h3>2. Walk the process physically</h3>

<p>Stand in the actual production or service area and trace the route from receipt to release. Note each handoff, wait point, equipment step, and storage point. This is usually where missing steps appear.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Ingredients left on a trolley before refrigeration</li>
  <li>Cooked food moved to a cooling rack before blast chilling</li>
  <li>Temporary allergen substitutions during service</li>
  <li>Part-packed products waiting for labels or sleeves</li>
</ul>

<h3>3. Use process language, not audit jargon</h3>

<p>Write steps so an operator would recognise them instantly. “Receive chilled chicken”, “slice tomatoes”, “grill burger patties”, and “apply PPDS label” are more useful than generic headings such as “control point” or “processing”.</p>

<h3>4. Show decision points and loops</h3>

<p>Not every process is linear. If products can be cooled and reheated, sent for rework, held pending QA release, or diverted into a different pack format, show that clearly. Hidden loops are a common reason hazard analysis becomes weak later.</p>

<h3>5. Validate the diagram on site</h3>

<p>Once drafted, walk it again with the team. Confirm the order, the real timing, and the actual controls used. Date the review so you can show when the diagram was last validated.</p>

<h2>What auditors and inspectors check</h2>

<p>A flow diagram is rarely judged in isolation. It is usually checked against the rest of the food safety system.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Does the diagram match what happens on site?</li>
  <li>Do hazards in the HACCP study align with the mapped steps?</li>
  <li>Are CCPs or other process controls linked to real stages on the line?</li>
  <li>Do records exist for the steps that need monitoring?</li>
  <li>Has the diagram been reviewed after process, recipe, equipment, or layout changes?</li>
</ul>

<p>If the diagram says “cool” but you cannot explain where, for how long, by which method, and which record proves it, the document is not doing its job.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes that weaken a flow diagram</h2>

<h3>Leaving out waiting and storage stages</h3>

<p>Food safety failures often happen between major steps, not only during them. A tray of cooked food waiting for space in the blast chiller or a PPDS sandwich waiting for the correct label can be more important than the obvious production stage.</p>

<h3>Using one diagram for products that behave differently</h3>

<p>If one product is fully cooked, another is assembled from ready-to-eat ingredients, and another is cooled overnight for next-day service, one generic chart usually creates weak hazard analysis.</p>

<h3>Failing to update after changes</h3>

<p>New equipment, recipe changes, new allergens, new packaging, and layout changes all affect the process map. If the diagram stays static while the site changes, the rest of the HACCP study drifts out of date too.</p>

<h3>Building the diagram from a template only</h3>

<p>Templates are useful starting points, but they are not evidence. A diagram that still reads like a generic training example will not help you explain real hazards or justify monitoring records.</p>

<h2>A simple structure you can use</h2>

<p>For many operations, a process flow diagram can start with a sequence like this:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Receive ingredients and packaging</li>
  <li>Check delivery condition and store appropriately</li>
  <li>Prepare ingredients</li>
  <li>Cook or otherwise process</li>
  <li>Cool, hold, or assemble as relevant</li>
  <li>Pack and label</li>
  <li>Release, dispatch, display, or serve</li>
</ol>

<p>That is only a starting structure. Your actual version must show the specific extra stages that create risk in your business.</p>

<h2>When to review the diagram</h2>

<p>Review it whenever the process changes in a way that could affect hazard control or product identity.</p>

<ul>
  <li>New recipes or new allergens</li>
  <li>New equipment or layout changes</li>
  <li>New packaging or labelling steps</li>
  <li>Changes to chilled holding, cooling, reheating, or dispatch</li>
  <li>Non-conformities, complaints, or audit findings showing the process map is incomplete</li>
</ul>

<h2>Final takeaway</h2>

<p>A good HACCP process flow diagram is not decorative. It is the backbone of the hazard analysis, the reference point for your monitoring records, and one of the easiest places for an auditor to test whether the documented system matches reality. If you make it specific, validate it on site, and keep it current, the rest of the HACCP study becomes far easier to defend.</p>
