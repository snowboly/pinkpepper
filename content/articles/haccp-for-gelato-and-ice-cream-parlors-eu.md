<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>One of the most common pitfalls in gelato and ice cream parlors is the failure to maintain proper temperature controls, especially within display cabinets. When an EHO probes your gelato and finds it warmer than -12°C, you're risking not only a failed audit but also your customers' health.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always check that your display cabinet thermometer is measuring the product core temperature, not just the air temperature. An accurate reading can save you from penalties.</blockquote>

<h4>What you'll learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to implement effective temperature control measures to prevent pathogen growth.</li>
    <li>The importance of thorough documentation for pasteurisation and cooling processes.</li>
    <li>How to train your staff effectively to reduce cross-contamination risks.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Batch-level pasteurisation logs: Ensure you log temperature and time for every batch.</li>
    <li>Display cabinet core temperature: An EHO will probe the gelato, not just rely on the cabinet thermometer.</li>
    <li>Scoop sanitation practices: Stagnant water can be a breeding ground for pathogens-keep your scoops in running water or sanitiser.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Gelato and Ice Cream Parlors</h3>

<h4>Biological Hazards</h4>
<p><strong>Key Pathogens:</strong> Listeria monocytogenes, Salmonella, Staphylococcus aureus, E. coli.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday when staff are scooping rapidly and not changing gloves, hand contact can lead to Staphylococcus aureus contamination. This can occur if a scoop is used for multiple flavours without proper sanitation.</p>
<p>Control measure: Use a clean scoop for each flavour and store scoops in running water above 43°C or in a sanitiser solution to prevent cross-contact.</p>

<h4>Chemical Hazards</h4>
<p><strong>Key Substances:</strong> Unpasteurised milk, raw eggs, allergens.</p>
<p>On a delivery day, if pasteurisation certificates for the dairy base are not checked, you risk using unpasteurised milk that can lead to E. coli contamination in your gelato. This oversight can happen when suppliers are busy and staff are distracted.</p>
<p>Control measure: Verify that dairy deliveries are below 5°C and check pasteurisation certificates for all dairy products used.</p>

<h4>Physical Hazards</h4>
<p><strong>Key Issues:</strong> Foreign objects, equipment malfunctions.</p>
<p>On a hot day when the soft-serve machine is working overtime, a broken part can lead to foreign objects contaminating the gelato. Staff may not notice until a customer complains.</p>
<p>Control measure: Conduct daily inspections of all equipment and ensure that all components are intact and functioning properly before service.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>During summer, when ambient temperatures exceed 30°C, display cabinets work harder. Reduce the quantity of gelato displayed and rotate flavours more frequently to maintain product core temperatures below -12°C.</p>
<p>When serving outdoors, ensure gelato in cups or cones is served immediately to prevent Listeria growth. Never re-freeze gelato that has been exposed to ambient conditions.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>CCP 1: Pasteurisation of Mix</strong>
        <p>Measure the temperature and time of the pasteurisation process. The mix must reach 85°C for 15 seconds or 69°C for 30 minutes.</p>
        <p>Monitor using a calibrated thermometer and log every batch with date, time, and temperature. Check logs before the start of each new batch.</p>
        <p>If the temperature or time limit is breached, discard the batch and re-pasteurise a new mix, documenting the incident.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>CCP 2: Blast Freezing</strong>
        <p>Measure the core temperature of the pasteurised mix after blast freezing. It must cool to <4°C within 90 minutes.</p>
        <p>Use a probe thermometer to check the core temperature. Monitor this every time you complete a batch.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, discard the batch and start a new cooling process, ensuring to log the incident.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>CCP 3: Display Cabinet Holding</strong>
        <p>Measure the core temperature of the gelato in the display cabinet. It must remain below -12°C.</p>
        <p>Use a calibrated probe thermometer to check the core temperature of the gelato at least every 4 hours.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, adjust the cabinet temperature immediately and check for proper airflow. Discard any product that has been above -12°C for more than 2 hours.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>CCP 4: Scoop Sanitation</strong>
        <p>Ensure scoops are stored in running water >43°C or in sanitiser solution. Monitor the temperature of the water or concentration of the sanitiser.</p>
        <p>Check the water temperature or sanitiser concentration every hour. Use a thermometer or test strips for sanitiser concentration.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, immediately replace the water or sanitiser and re-sanitize all scoops before use.</p>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Mistake: No batch-level pasteurisation logs. → Fix: Log every batch with temperature, time, and date.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Display cabinet thermometer reads air temperature, not product core. → Fix: Use a probe thermometer to check the core temperature of the gelato.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Scoops stored in stagnant cold water. → Fix: Store scoops in running water >43°C or in sanitiser between flavours.</li>
</ul>
<p>Managing suppliers and conducting thorough receiving checks is vital for maintaining food safety in your gelato or ice cream parlor. Each delivery must meet strict criteria to prevent contamination and ensure quality. Implementing a robust supplier management system protects your business and your customers.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Dairy base: Ensure delivery temperature is <5°C and check pasteurisation certificate.</li>
    <li>Egg supply: Prefer pasteurised liquid egg over shell eggs for mixes.</li>
    <li>Fruit/nut inclusions: Verify allergen cross-contact declarations are provided.</li>
    <li>Waffle cones/toppings: Confirm sealed packaging, within best-before dates, and stored dry.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>If dairy products are delivered above 5°C.</li>
    <li>Missing pasteurisation certificates for dairy or egg products.</li>
    <li>Damaged or open packaging on waffle cones or toppings.</li>
    <li>Out-of-date fruit/nut inclusions or products with allergen risks not declared.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always inspect deliveries immediately upon arrival. A quick check can save you from using compromised ingredients that could jeopardize food safety.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Check pasteurisation temperature: 85°C for 15 seconds or 69°C for 30 minutes.</li>
    <li>Blast freeze pasteurised mix to <4°C within 90 minutes.</li>
    <li>Verify core temperature of display cabinet: must stay < -12°C.</li>
    <li>Inspect scoops stored in running water >43°C or sanitiser between flavours.</li>
    <li>Disassemble soft-serve dispensing nozzles and sanitise with 200ppm chlorinated solution.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Batch-level pasteurisation logs: document temperature and time for each batch.</li>
    <li>Blast freezing records: log core temperatures of the mix post-pasteurisation.</li>
    <li>Daily equipment cleaning records: detail disassembly and sanitisation of machines.</li>
    <li>Display cabinet temperature logs: include core temperatures, not just air readings.</li>
    <li>Staff training records: confirm completion of allergen training and machine operation procedures.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<p>New scoopers must undergo a 30-minute induction covering allergen cross-contact via shared scoops, handwashing triggers, and the 'taste safely' protocol (never re-use tasting spoons).</p>
<p>Machine operators need training on daily disassembly and cleaning-in-place (CIP) of soft-serve and batch freezer nozzles to prevent Listeria biofilm.</p>
<p>All staff must understand that melted-and-refrozen gelato poses a Listeria risk; never re-freeze any product.</p>
<p>To operate a compliant gelato and ice cream parlor, adhere to temperature controls outlined in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(5), ensure proper processing of raw milk and dairy as per EC 853/2004 Annex III Section IX, and monitor Listeria limits according to EC 2073/2005. Maintain accurate records for pasteurisation logs and display cabinet temperatures.</p><p>Regularly train staff on hygiene practices and allergen management to mitigate risks.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Review and update your pasteurisation logs to include temperature and time for each batch.</li>
    <li>Check display cabinet thermometers; ensure they measure product core temperatures, not just air temperatures.</li>
    <li>Implement a daily cleaning schedule for soft-serve dispensing nozzles, using 200ppm chlorinated sanitiser.</li>
    <li>Conduct a staff training session on allergen cross-contact and 'taste safely' protocols.</li>
    <li>Inspect all deliveries this week to ensure dairy is <5°C and check pasteurisation certificates.</li>
</ol>
