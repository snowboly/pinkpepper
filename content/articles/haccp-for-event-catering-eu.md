<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>One of the most common issues in event catering is the transport of hot food that arrives at the venue below 63°C. This lapse not only puts guests at risk but also violates the strict temperature control regulations outlined in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IV Section 7.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always use a probe thermometer to check food temperatures immediately upon unloading at the venue. Document these temperatures in a temperature log to avoid disputes later.</blockquote>

<h4>What You'll Learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to establish and maintain critical control points (CCPs) during transportation.</li>
    <li>The importance of proper allergen management for large events and how to implement it.</li>
    <li>Best practices for training staff to ensure food safety compliance at every event.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature logs during transit to confirm food arrived at safe temperatures.</li>
    <li>Allergen management practices, specifically whether meals are individually sealed and labeled.</li>
    <li>Risk assessments for each venue, including checks for adequate facilities and equipment.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Event Catering</h3>

<p><strong>Biological Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li><strong>Clostridium perfringens:</strong> Common in large batch meats or stews cooked at the base kitchen. On a busy Saturday when you prepare 50 kg of beef stew, if it's transported warm and held too long at the venue, this pathogen can proliferate.</li>
 <li><strong>Staphylococcus aureus:</strong> Frequently found in foods handled by many people. During a buffet setup for 300 guests, if your staff handles hundreds of portions without proper hand hygiene, the risk of contamination increases significantly.</li>
 <li><strong>Bacillus cereus:</strong> Often associated with rice and grain dishes. If you transport 20 kg of rice for a wedding and it sits at 20°C for 2 hours, the risk of this pathogen growing is high.</li>
 <li><strong>Salmonella:</strong> Particularly a risk with undercooked poultry. On a busy event day, cooking 200 chicken breasts can lead to uneven cooking, especially if the internal temperature does not reach at least 75°C.</li>
 <li><strong>Norovirus:</strong> Can spread from an ill food handler to food. On a crowded event day, if one staff member is symptomatic but still works, they can potentially infect hundreds of guests.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Control Measures:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Cook poultry to a minimum of <strong>75°C</strong> and verify with a probe thermometer.</li>
 <li>Transport hot food at >63°C and verify at both load and unload.</li>
 <li>Hold rice below <strong>5°C</strong> during transit; discard any held at ambient for over 2 hours.</li>
 <li>Ensure all food handlers practice proper hygiene, especially after handling raw foods.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Chemical Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Cleaning agents can contaminate food if not rinsed properly. On a busy Saturday when staff are cleaning equipment, if they don't allow sufficient time for surfaces to dry, residues can contaminate food.</li>
 <li>Allergens must be managed to avoid cross-contact. If a team member preparing nut-free meals uses the same utensils as those used for dishes containing nuts, the potential for cross-contact is significant.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Control Measures:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Ensure cleaning agents are rinsed thoroughly with potable water before food contact.</li>
 <li>Label and store allergen meals separately and ensure utensils are dedicated to specific allergens.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Physical Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Foreign objects like glass or plastic can end up in food. On a busy Saturday when you're setting up, if a glass bowl shatters near food prep areas, shards can contaminate the food.</li>
 <li>Improperly stored utensils can lead to injuries or contamination. If staff improperly stack knives near food, this poses a physical risk to both staff and guests.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Control Measures:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Conduct a thorough inspection of the area before food prep starts to remove any potential hazards.</li>
 <li>Store utensils in a safe manner, away from food preparation areas.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>In summer, outdoor events in 25-30°C ambient temperatures can halve display times for cold canapés. Use insulated transport and monitor food temperature closely.</p>
<p>During the December party season, when volume peaks, ensure casual staff are trained properly. Untrained agency staff can lead to corners being cut, increasing risks.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Transport Temperature for Hot Food</strong>
        <p>Measure hot food temperature at departure and arrival: must be >63°C.</p>
        <p>Use a calibrated probe thermometer at load and unload; check every event.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, discard food that falls below 63°C and document the incident.</p>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Transport Temperature for Cold Food</strong>
        <p>Measure cold food temperature throughout transit: must remain <5°C.</p>
        <p>Use data loggers in cool boxes; check temperature upon arrival.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, discard food that exceeds 5°C and document the incident.</p>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Venue Hot Holding Temperature</strong>
        <p>Measure holding temperature: food must remain >63°C if served within 2 hours.</p>
        <p>Use a probe thermometer to check every 30 minutes during service.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, reheat food to >75°C or discard it if it has been below 63°C for over 2 hours.</p>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Time Control for Ambient Holding</strong>
        <p>Document time food is held at ambient temperature: maximum 2 hours.</p>
        <p>Use a timer or log sheet to track time from cooking.</p>
        <p>If time exceeds 2 hours, discard the food and document the decision.</p>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Allergen Meal Management</strong>
        <p>Ensure allergen meals are individually sealed and labelled.</p>
        <p>Check every meal before loading; use a checklist for each guest's requirements.</p>
        <p>If meals are not sealed or labelled correctly, repackage meals before serving or discard them.</p>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> No temperature log between leaving base kitchen and arriving at venue. 
        <strong>Fix:</strong> Implement a temperature log for every event, recording temperatures at load and unload.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Hot food arrives at venue below 63°C but is served anyway. 
        <strong>Fix:</strong> Discard any food below 63°C upon arrival and do not serve it.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Allergen meals not individually sealed, risking cross-contact. 
        <strong>Fix:</strong> Ensure all allergen meals are sealed, labelled, and trackable to specific guests before transport.
    </li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management and receiving checks are critical in event catering. The quality and safety of your food start with what you receive. Implement a robust system to verify that all equipment and ingredients meet your standards before they enter your kitchen.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Check hired equipment (bain-maries, hot cupboards, fridges) to ensure they maintain required temperatures.</li>
    <li>Test temperature of food upon arrival; hot food must be >63°C and cold food <5°C.</li>
    <li>Verify power supply (amps) and water access for hired equipment and venue facilities.</li>
    <li>Inspect transport vehicles; if not refrigerated, ensure cool boxes with data loggers for cold food.</li>
    <li>Confirm that casual/agency staff have valid food hygiene certificates (minimum Level 2).</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Hot food arrives below 63°C; do not accept it.</li>
    <li>Cold food is >5°C; refuse delivery immediately.</li>
    <li>Equipment fails temperature tests; return it before use.</li>
    <li>Casual staff cannot provide their food hygiene certificate; do not allow them to handle food.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always conduct a pre-visit to the venue to confirm the setup requirements; this avoids surprises on the event day.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Check transport temperature of hot food: >63°C at load and unload</li>
    <li>Check transport temperature of cold food: <5°C throughout transit</li>
    <li>Verify hot holding temperature at venue: >63°C within 30-45 minutes</li>
    <li>Document time for hot food held at ambient: max 2 hours</li>
    <li>Inspect allergen meal packaging: individually sealed and labeled</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature logs for hot and cold food during transport</li>
    <li>Hot holding temperature records at the venue</li>
    <li>Documented risk assessment for each venue, including facilities check</li>
    <li>Allergen management records, tracking meals to specific guests/tables</li>
    <li>Staff training certificates for all casual/agency staff</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<p>All event staff must be trained in probe thermometer usage at the venue. Hot food must be >63°C and cold food <5°C; check these temperatures on arrival and before service.</p>
<p>Each event needs a nominated 'food safety person' responsible for temperature logs and allergen management. Team leaders should ensure compliance and address any issues immediately.</p>
<p>Casual staff require a 10-minute briefing at the event start covering handwash locations, allergen specials, and service temperature rules. Drivers must understand their responsibility for food temperatures during transport and unload checks.</p>
<p>Compliance in event catering hinges on strict adherence to temperature control, allergen management, and risk assessment as outlined in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IV Section 7, Annex II Ch. III, and Regulation 1169/2011 Art. 44. Understand that transport temperatures must be maintained above 63°C for hot food and below 5°C for cold food, while allergen meals need to be individually sealed and trackable.</p><p>Each venue requires a documented risk assessment to address specific facilities and requirements.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Conduct a risk assessment for each venue you cater at this week.</li>
    <li>Implement a temperature log system for food during transit.</li>
    <li>Verify all hired equipment maintains correct temperatures before your next event.</li>
    <li>Train staff on probe thermometer use and allergen management.</li>
    <li>Ensure all allergen meals are individually sealed and labeled for tracking.</li>
</ol>
