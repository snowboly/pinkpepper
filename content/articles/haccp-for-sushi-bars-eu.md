<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>One of the most common problems in sushi bars is failing to maintain proper freezing protocols for fish intended for raw consumption. A missing freezer log can lead to immediate enforcement actions, jeopardizing your business and the safety of your customers.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always keep an up-to-date freezer log that tracks temperatures and times for all fish undergoing parasite freezing. This is your first line of defense against audit failures.</blockquote>

<h4>What you'll learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to effectively manage parasite freezing protocols for raw fish.</li>
    <li>Best practices for handling sushi rice to prevent contamination.</li>
    <li>Key supplier checks to ensure the safety of your ingredients.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Freezer logs for parasite freezing compliance - missing logs are a top failure point.</li>
    <li>Sushi rice pH levels and time logs - improper management leads to potential B. cereus growth.</li>
    <li>Temperature control for sashimi and raw ingredients - auditors will probe fish core temperatures during service.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Sushi Bars</h3>

<h4>Biological Hazards</h4>
<p>Key pathogens include Anisakis simplex, Histamine, Listeria monocytogenes, Vibrio parahaemolyticus, and Staphylococcus aureus.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday when raw fish is prepared while waiting for a delivery, you may not have time to verify the parasite freeze. Ensure all fish for raw consumption is frozen at -20°C for 24 hours or -35°C for 15 hours before serving (EC 853/2004 Annex III Section VIII Ch. 3(D)).</p>
<p>Check the core temperature of sashimi regularly; it must remain below 5°C at all times during service.</p>

<h4>Chemical Hazards</h4>
<p>Histamine formation is a significant risk, especially in tuna and mackerel. If fish is displayed above 5°C during peak hours, histamine can form irreversibly.</p>
<p>On a hot summer day when the display case struggles to maintain low temperatures, you need to limit the amount of fish on display. Keep the core temperature below 5°C at all times to prevent histamine levels exceeding 200mg/kg (EC 2073/2005 Annex I Ch. 1 Row 1.27).</p>
<p>Verify supplier temperature logs upon delivery; reject any tuna delivered above 5°C.</p>

<h4>Physical Hazards</h4>
<p>Physical hazards can include foreign objects such as bones, shells, or even utensils. If staff are hurriedly preparing sushi, they may overlook checking for small bones in fish.</p>
<p>On a busy night, when multiple orders come in at once, ensure all staff are trained to check fish thoroughly before serving. Use a designated knife for each species to prevent cross-contamination and physical hazards.</p>
<p>Implement a visual inspection checklist for staff to follow during prep to catch any potential hazards.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>During summer, high ambient temperatures can accelerate histamine formation in displayed fish. Reduce the quantity of fish displayed to maintain a safe temperature below 5°C.</p>
<p>During festive periods, increased demand for sushi can lead to bulk rice preparation. Enforce a strict 4-hour discard rule for sushi rice held at ambient temperature to minimize the risk of B. cereus growth.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Parasite Freezing</strong>
        <p>All fish for raw consumption must be frozen at -20°C for 24h or -35°C for 15h.</p>
        <p>Monitor with a calibrated freezer thermometer, recording temperatures and times in a freezer log daily.</p>
        <p>If the limit is breached, discard the fish and document the reason for non-compliance.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Sushi Rice pH</strong>
        <p>Vinegared rice must reach a pH of <4.6 to inhibit B. cereus growth.</p>
        <p>Use pH strips to test each batch of rice immediately after preparation.</p>
        <p>If the pH exceeds 4.6, discard the rice and prepare a new batch, ensuring proper acidification.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Rice Holding Time</strong>
        <p>Sushi rice held at ambient temperature must be discarded after 4 hours.</p>
        <p>Log the batch times when rice is prepared and use a timer to monitor holding time.</p>
        <p>If the 4-hour limit is reached, discard the rice and prepare a fresh batch as needed.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Cold Chain for Sashimi</strong>
        <p>Display and prep temperature must remain below 5°C at all times.</p>
        <p>Probe the core temperature of fish hourly during service using a calibrated food thermometer.</p>
        <p>If temperatures exceed 5°C, discard the fish and adjust refrigeration settings immediately.</p>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> No freezer log proving parasite freeze for raw fish.<br />
        <strong>Fix:</strong> Implement a daily log system to track freezing times and temperatures.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Sushi rice left at ambient with no time log or pH verification.<br />
        <strong>Fix:</strong> Establish a strict logging process for batch times and conduct pH testing for every rice preparation.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Thawing fish at room temperature or under running warm water.<br />
        <strong>Fix:</strong> Always thaw fish in the refrigerator at temperatures <5°C to maintain cold chain integrity.
    </li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management is critical in a sushi bar to ensure the safety and quality of your ingredients. Each delivery must be thoroughly checked against specific criteria to mitigate risks associated with raw fish and other ingredients. Implementing these checks will help maintain compliance and protect your customers.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Fish for raw consumption: Confirm that the supplier has frozen fish at -20°C for 24h or -35°C for 15h.</li>
    <li>Tuna: Verify delivery temperature is <2°C; reject if above 5°C to prevent histamine formation.</li>
    <li>Nori, soy, and wasabi: Check for allergen declarations (soy, sesame, wheat - all Top 14 allergens).</li>
    <li>Shellfish: Ensure all deliveries include harvester registration and zone classification documents.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Fish not accompanied by a parasite-freezing confirmation or wild-caught origin documentation.</li>
    <li>Tuna delivered at a temperature >5°C.</li>
    <li>Nori, soy, or wasabi lacking allergen declarations.</li>
    <li>Shellfish without proper harvester registration or zone classification.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Check the ice in the delivery box; if it's melted or insufficient, it's a sign that the cold chain has been broken. Discard that delivery immediately.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Verify parasite freeze log for all raw fish: -20°C for 24h or -35°C for 15h.</li>
    <li>Check sushi rice pH with strips; must be <4.6.</li>
    <li>Log sushi rice batch times; discard if held >4h at ambient.</li>
    <li>Probe fish core temperature hourly; ensure <5°C for sashimi.</li>
    <li>Inspect handwashing practices; ensure staff wash hands before touching RTE foods.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Parasite freeze log detailing fish type, freezing date, and temperature.</li>
    <li>Sushi rice pH testing records with date, time, and pH values.</li>
    <li>Sushi rice preparation and discard time logs.</li>
    <li>Temperature logs for sashimi, including core temperature readings.</li>
    <li>Supplier confirmations for fish origin and freezing status.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Sushi chefs must understand parasite freeze log responsibilities and rice pH testing procedures.</li>
    <li>Knife sanitisation protocols between different fish species must be followed.</li>
    <li>All prep staff are required to adhere to handwashing protocols before handling RTE foods, especially after raw fish.</li>
    <li>Front-of-house staff must be knowledgeable about allergens in sushi, including fish, crustaceans, molluscs, soy, sesame, and wheat.</li>
</ul>
<p>To operate a compliant sushi bar, adhere to the parasite freezing requirements outlined in EC 853/2004 Annex III Section VIII Ch. 3(D), maintain sushi rice pH below 4.6 as per EC 2073/2005 Annex I Ch. 1 Row 1.27, and ensure temperature control for ready-to-eat foods according to EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch.</p><p>IX(5). Regularly monitor key control points to prevent hazards like histamine formation and cross-contamination. Document all processes diligently to avoid common audit failures.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
 <li>Review and update your parasite freezing log to ensure compliance with EC 853/2004.</li>
 <li>Test sushi rice pH using strips and log results for each batch.</li>
 <li>Implement a strict 4-hour discard rule for sushi rice held at ambient temperatures.</li>
 <li>Train staff on handwashing protocols and knife sanitisation procedures.</li>
 <li>Check supplier documentation for fish deliveries, ensuring compliance with allergen declarations and temperature controls.</li>
</ol>
