<div class="pp-article-callout"><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Build your plan around your real process flow, not a copied template. Inspectors quickly spot generic plans that do not match what actually happens on site.</div>

<p>Under EC 852/2004, most food businesses in the EU and UK are legally required to operate food safety management procedures based on HACCP principles. The point of a HACCP plan is not to produce paperwork — it is to identify where things can go wrong and put reliable controls in place before they do.</p>

<p>This guide covers the full sequence: from assembling your team to the records that keep your plan defensible during an inspection.</p>

<h2>Step 1: Assemble your HACCP team</h2>

<p>HACCP is not a document one person writes and files. It requires input from people who know the product, the process, and the hazards involved. For a small business, that might be just two or three people. For larger operations, it typically includes production, quality, sanitation, and procurement.</p>

<p>Document each team member's name, role, and specific responsibility within the plan. This establishes accountability and makes it clear who owns each section when an auditor asks.</p>

<h2>Step 2: Describe the product and its intended use</h2>

<p>Write a full profile of every product the plan covers. This should include ingredients (and any allergens), water activity or pH if relevant, processing method, packaging type, storage conditions, shelf life, and intended consumer.</p>

<p>The intended use matters. A ready-to-eat product eaten without further cooking carries different risks than a frozen product the consumer cooks at home. If the product is intended for a vulnerable group — infants, elderly people, or immunocompromised individuals — the hazard analysis needs to reflect that.</p>

<h2>Step 3: Build and verify the process flow diagram</h2>

<p>Map every step the product moves through, from intake of raw materials to dispatch. Include storage, preparation, processing, packaging, rework loops, and waste handling. Do not rely on memory or written procedures alone.</p>

<p>Once the diagram is drafted, walk the actual process on site and check it against what happens in practice. Operators often hold product longer than the procedure states, or cleaning steps occur at different points than documented. The flow diagram must reflect the real operation, not the intended one. Adjust anything that does not match.</p>

<h2>Step 4: Conduct the hazard analysis</h2>

<p>At each step in the flow diagram, identify what could go wrong. Hazards fall into three categories:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Biological:</strong> pathogens such as <em>Salmonella</em>, <em>Listeria monocytogenes</em>, <em>E. coli</em>, spoilage organisms, and viruses</li>
  <li><strong>Chemical:</strong> allergen cross-contact, cleaning chemical residues, mycotoxins, pesticide carryover</li>
  <li><strong>Physical:</strong> metal fragments, bone, glass, hard plastic, stones</li>
</ul>

<p>For each hazard, assess whether it is significant — meaning it is reasonably likely to occur and would cause serious harm if not controlled. Hazards that are minor or highly unlikely are addressed through prerequisite programmes such as good hygiene practice (GHP) and standard operating procedures, not through the HACCP plan itself.</p>

<h2>Step 5: Identify your Critical Control Points</h2>

<p>A Critical Control Point (CCP) is a step where a control measure can be applied to prevent, eliminate, or reduce a significant hazard to an acceptable level. Not every step in the process is a CCP, and treating too many steps as CCPs makes the plan unmanageable.</p>

<p>Use a decision tree to assess each step systematically. Common CCPs include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Thermal processing steps designed to destroy pathogens (ovens, pasteurisers, fryers)</li>
  <li>Metal detection or X-ray screening for physical contaminants</li>
  <li>Filtration or sieving steps that remove bone or foreign material</li>
  <li>Chilling steps where temperature must reach a safe level within a defined time</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 6: Set critical limits</h2>

<p>Every CCP needs a measurable boundary that defines safe from unsafe. Critical limits must be specific and quantifiable. Vague language does not work here.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Not acceptable:</strong> "Cook until hot enough"</li>
  <li><strong>Acceptable:</strong> "Internal temperature reaches 75°C and holds for at least 30 seconds"</li>
</ul>

<p>Base your critical limits on regulatory requirements, Codex Alimentarius guidance, or documented scientific evidence. If an auditor asks why your cook time is what it is, you need a source — not an assumption.</p>

<h2>Step 7: Define monitoring, corrective actions, and verification</h2>

<h3>Monitoring</h3>

<p>For each CCP, define what is being measured, how it is measured, how often, and by whom. A calibrated probe thermometer, a flow meter, or a metal detector test piece are all examples of monitoring tools. The frequency must be sufficient to catch a deviation before unsafe product moves forward in the process.</p>

<h3>Corrective actions</h3>

<p>When a monitoring check shows a deviation, your plan must specify two things: what happens to the affected product, and how the process is corrected. Hold and segregate any product made during the deviation. Determine whether it can be reworked, diverted, or must be destroyed. Then address the cause — do not just re-run the check and move on.</p>

<h3>Verification</h3>

<p>Verification is how you confirm the plan is working as intended. It includes calibrating monitoring equipment, reviewing monitoring records for completeness, and periodic product testing where appropriate. Validation — the scientific evidence that your controls can actually eliminate the hazard — should be in place before the plan goes live, not after.</p>

<h2>Records your plan must include</h2>

<p>If it is not documented, it did not happen. An auditor reviewing your plan will expect to see:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The signed and dated HACCP plan with version history</li>
  <li>CCP monitoring logs (temperature charts, detection records)</li>
  <li>Corrective action reports describing each deviation and what was done</li>
  <li>Validation evidence supporting your critical limits</li>
  <li>Equipment calibration records</li>
  <li>Training records for staff responsible for CCP monitoring</li>
</ul>

<h2>Keep the plan current</h2>

<p>A HACCP plan is a working document. It should be reviewed whenever something material changes: a new raw material or supplier, new processing equipment, a reformulated product, or repeated deviations at a CCP. At minimum, schedule a full reassessment annually. Plans that have not been touched in years are a common audit finding.</p>

<h2>Common mistakes</h2>

<h3>Making everything a CCP</h3>

<p>Prerequisite programmes such as cleaning schedules, pest control, and personal hygiene handle background risks. If routine sanitation steps are listed as CCPs, the plan becomes bloated and harder to manage without making the operation any safer.</p>

<h3>Vague critical limits</h3>

<p>Words like "adequate," "sufficient," or "properly cooked" are not critical limits. Every CCP needs a number or a defined measurable condition. If your team cannot check it with a tool and record a result, it is not a workable limit.</p>

<h3>Flow diagrams that do not match site reality</h3>

<p>This is one of the most common findings during inspections. If the diagram shows a step that does not happen, or misses a step that does, the hazard analysis built on top of it is unreliable. The on-site verification walk is not optional.</p>

<h2>Final takeaway</h2>

<p>A HACCP plan that works is built around your actual process, uses measurable controls, and is maintained by people who understand what they are monitoring and why. The paperwork is the evidence — but the real protection comes from controls that are practical enough to run consistently under normal operating pressure. If your plan only works when things are quiet and everyone is following procedure perfectly, it will not hold up when something goes wrong.</p>
