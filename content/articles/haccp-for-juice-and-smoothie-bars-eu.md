<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>Every day, juice and smoothie bars face the risk of serious food safety violations. A common problem is the lack of a documented produce wash and sanitisation step, leading to potential E. coli contamination from unwashed leafy greens. This oversight can not only harm customers but also jeopardise your business reputation.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always document your produce wash and sanitisation procedures, including time, concentration of sanitiser, and rinse steps. A simple 'we rinse everything' won't protect you during an audit.</blockquote>

<h4>What you'll learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to implement effective washing and sanitisation protocols for all produce.</li>
    <li>Key temperature controls for freshly pressed juice and pre-made juice storage.</li>
    <li>Best practices for preventing allergen cross-contact in shared equipment.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Documentation of produce washing and sanitisation procedures.</li>
    <li>Temperature logs for pre-made juices, ensuring they are stored <5°C and consumed within 24 hours.</li>
    <li>Allergen information matrix, confirming that it is updated and accurate for daily smoothie menus.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Juice and Smoothie Bars</h3>

<h4>Biological Hazards</h4>
<p>Key pathogens include E. coli O157, Salmonella, Norovirus, and Listeria monocytogenes. E. coli O157 can survive on unwashed leafy greens, while Salmonella can be transferred from contaminated fruit surfaces during extraction.</p>
<p>On a busy Saturday when you're pressing juices rapidly, unwashed leafy greens may slip through the cracks. Always wash all produce in potable water, and for leafy greens, sanitise using a 50-100 ppm chlorine rinse followed by a potable water rinse.</p>

<h4>Chemical Hazards</h4>
<p>Allergens such as milk, tree nuts, and celery can cause serious reactions. Cross-contact can occur through shared blenders if not cleaned properly between uses.</p>
<p>When preparing multiple smoothies in quick succession, if you blend a nut-based smoothie first, residual nut protein can contaminate a fruit-only smoothie served next. Rinse blenders thoroughly between allergen groups to prevent cross-contact; a quick splash won't suffice.</p>

<h4>Physical Hazards</h4>
<p>Physical hazards can include foreign objects like fruit seeds, stems, or even equipment fragments. These can pose choking risks or cause injury to consumers.</p>
<p>On a hectic day, if staff are rushing and not inspecting produce properly, a piece of equipment may inadvertently end up in a smoothie. Implement a strict visual inspection protocol for all produce before use to catch any potential hazards.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>During summer, the demand for juices peaks, leading to faster bacterial growth. Press smaller batches throughout the day and serve immediately; discard any juice left at ambient temperature for more than 4 hours.</p>
<p>In January, with a surge in green juice demand, ensure that leafy greens are carefully washed and sanitised to combat E. coli risks. Don't shortcut sanitisation under pressure; maintain the 50-100 ppm chlorine rinse procedure strictly.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Fruit/Vegetable Washing</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> All produce must be washed in potable water; leafy greens must be sanitised with a 50-100 ppm chlorine rinse followed by a potable water rinse.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Use a chlorine test kit to verify concentration every time you prepare leafy greens. Document the washing procedure in a logbook.</li>
            <li><strong>What to do when the limit is breached:</strong> If the chlorine concentration is below 50 ppm, re-sanitise the greens immediately. If they have already been processed, discard them.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Juice Holding Time</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Juice must be held at <5°C and consumed within 24 hours.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Use a calibrated fridge thermometer to check temperature at least every 4 hours. Maintain a log of juice production times and temperatures.</li>
            <li><strong>What to do when the limit is breached:</strong> Discard any juice held for over 24 hours or above 5°C. Document the batch and dispose of it immediately.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Blender Cross-Contact</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Ensure blenders are cleaned between allergen groups, specifically after nut-based smoothies before making fruit-only smoothies.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Establish a cleaning protocol and visually inspect blenders between uses. Document cleaning times and methods in a log.</li>
            <li><strong>What to do when the limit is breached:</strong> If a blender is used for multiple allergen groups without proper cleaning, stop all production immediately, clean the blender, and retrain staff on cross-contact protocols.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Raw Sprouts Handling</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Ensure raw sprouts are sourced from an approved supplier with pathogen testing certification.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Request and keep a copy of the supplier's pathogen testing certificate for each batch received. Check for freshness upon receipt.</li>
            <li><strong>What to do when the limit is breached:</strong> If sprouts are received without certification, do not use them. Discard any existing sprouts in the kitchen.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Mistake: No produce wash/sanitise step documented → Fix: Implement a verified washing protocol and document it in your standard operating procedures.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Pre-made juices held >24h without use-by label or temperature log → Fix: Label all juice with press time and 24h use-by date, and maintain a temperature log.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Shared blender for nut-based and nut-free smoothies without adequate cleaning → Fix: Establish a strict cleaning protocol between allergen groups and train staff accordingly.</li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management is critical in maintaining the safety and quality of your juice and smoothie bar products. Thorough checks upon receiving deliveries can prevent contamination and ensure compliance with food safety regulations. Establish a routine to assess every delivery for potential hazards.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Inspect fresh produce for damage, soil contamination, and pest evidence.</li>
    <li>Wash all items, regardless of 'pre-washed' label, in potable water.</li>
    <li>Check allergen declarations on protein powders and supplements for milk, soy, and egg.</li>
    <li>Store nut milks and butters at <5°C after opening, checking for recalls.</li>
    <li>Request pathogen testing certificates for raw sprouts from your supplier.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Produce shows signs of spoilage, mold, or excessive bruising.</li>
    <li>Containers of protein powders are opened or damaged, risking contamination.</li>
    <li>Nut milks or butters are not stored at <5°C or lack proper recall information.</li>
    <li>Sprouts arrive without a pathogen testing certificate or show signs of decay.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>When inspecting deliveries, always have a thermometer on hand to check the temperature of chilled items-anything above <5°C is a red flag.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Produce wash protocol followed: all fruit and vegetables washed in potable water.</li>
    <li>Leafy greens sanitised with 50-100ppm chlorine, rinsed with potable water.</li>
    <li>Juice holding temperature checked: <5°C for any pre-made juice.</li>
    <li>Juices discarded if held for >24 hours without use-by label.</li>
    <li>Blenders cleaned between allergen groups: after nut-based to fruit-only smoothies.</li>
    <li>Hand hygiene maintained: staff washed hands before handling any produce.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Produce wash/sanitisation logs: document procedures and times for each batch.</li>
    <li>Juice holding temperature logs: track temperatures for any pre-made juices.</li>
    <li>Blender cleaning records: indicate when each blender was cleaned and sanitised.</li>
    <li>Allergen matrix: updated list of all allergens in smoothies, including daily changes.</li>
    <li>Supplier checks: records of inspections for fresh produce and pathogen testing for sprouts.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<ul>
    <li>All staff trained on the produce wash protocol: demonstrate correct wash/sanitise/rinse procedure.</li>
    <li>Blender operators trained in allergen cross-contact awareness: clean blenders between allergen groups.</li>
    <li>Juice bottlers trained to label all juices with press time and 24-hour use-by date.</li>
    <li>Hand hygiene training: wash hands before handling any produce to mitigate Norovirus risk.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>

<p>To comply with EU regulations, juice and smoothie bars must wash produce in potable water (EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(3)), maintain juice holding temperatures below 5°C and label with 24-hour use-by dates (EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(5)), and ensure allergen information is clearly provided for all made-to-order items (Regulation 1169/2011 Art.</p><p>44). Failure to adhere to these requirements can lead to serious health risks and potential legal consequences. Regular staff training and strict adherence to hygiene practices are non-negotiable.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Document and implement a produce washing and sanitisation protocol for all items.</li>
    <li>Set up a log to monitor and record juice holding temperatures daily.</li>
    <li>Establish a cleaning schedule for blenders and train staff on allergen cross-contact procedures.</li>
    <li>Review your supplier lists and ensure all produce, nut milks, and protein powders have current allergen declarations.</li>
    <li>Create and display an allergen matrix that is updated weekly according to your smoothie menu.</li>
</ol>
