<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>Temperature logging failures are a common issue in hospital catering. Many facilities log temperatures only at kitchen dispatch, neglecting to monitor them during transit. This oversight can lead to food arriving at the ward outside safe temperature ranges, risking patient safety.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always log temperatures at the ward level. Implement a system where each trolley load is probed and logged before serving patients.</blockquote>

<h4>What You'll Learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>The unique CCPs specific to hospital catering, including cook-chill and regeneration processes.</li>
    <li>How to maintain compliance with temperature control regulations and avoid common audit failures.</li>
    <li>Best practices for staff training in handling vulnerable patient populations and specific dietary needs.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature logs at the ward level - verifying they reflect actual serving conditions.</li>
    <li>Labelling of modified-texture meals - ensuring each tray is correctly matched to patient dietary requirements.</li>
    <li>Calibration of regeneration ovens - checking that core temperatures reach >75°C for safe reheating.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Hospital Catering</h3>

<p><strong>Biological Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li><strong>Listeria monocytogenes:</strong> This pathogen has a high fatality rate in immunocompromised patients, reaching 20-30%. On a busy Saturday when pre-prepared meals are being dispatched, a failure to chill meals from 70°C to <3°C within 90 minutes can lead to Listeria growth.</li>
 <li><strong>Clostridium difficile:</strong> While not foodborne, it poses a risk through inadequate kitchen hygiene. If staff fail to change gloves and aprons between the kitchen and clinical areas, cross-contamination can occur.</li>
 <li><strong>Bacillus cereus:</strong> Particularly associated with rice and pasta, its spores can survive cooking. On a busy evening service, if food is cooled too slowly, spores may germinate, leading to foodborne illness.</li>
 <li><strong>Salmonella:</strong> Commonly linked to undercooked eggs and poultry. If a kitchen staff member undercooks chicken on a high-demand day, it can result in prolonged, severe illness for patients.</li>
 <li><strong>Norovirus:</strong> Outbreaks can quickly spread from food service staff to patients. If a staff member shows symptoms but continues to work, it can lead to rapid ward outbreaks.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Control Measures:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Chill cooked foods from 70°C to <3°C within 90 minutes (EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(5)).</li>
 <li>Change gloves and aprons when transitioning from kitchen to clinical areas.</li>
 <li>Ensure rice and pasta cool from >60°C to <8°C within 90 minutes.</li>
 <li>Probe poultry to ensure it reaches a core temperature of >75°C.</li>
 <li>Exclude staff exhibiting Norovirus symptoms for 48 hours post-symptom cessation.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Chemical Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li><strong>Allergens:</strong> Cross-contact can occur if allergen-free items are not produced on dedicated lines. On a busy service day, if a gluten-free item is prepared on the same surface as wheat products, it risks patient safety.</li>
 <li><strong>Cleaning Chemicals:</strong> Inappropriate use can contaminate food surfaces. If staff use a strong disinfectant solution without proper rinsing, it can lead to chemical residues on food prep areas.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Control Measures:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Verify supplier evidence for dedicated production lines for allergen-free items.</li>
 <li>Utilize cleaning chemicals as per manufacturer guidelines, ensuring thorough rinsing.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Physical Hazards:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li><strong>Foreign Objects:</strong> Items like broken glass or metal shavings can enter food. On a busy weekend, if a staff member drops a utensil into a food container, it can create a serious risk for patients.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Control Measures:</strong></p>
<ul>
 <li>Implement strict policies for utensil handling and regular inspections of food areas for foreign objects.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>
<p>During winter, Norovirus outbreaks are common. Implement immediate kitchen-to-ward barrier protocols and exclude symptomatic staff for 48 hours post-symptom cessation.</p>
<p>In summer, be aware that trolley transit through non-air-conditioned corridors can lead to faster temperature drops. Consider using insulated trolleys or reducing transit distance to maintain food safety.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Cook-Chill Production:</strong>
        <p>Measure the temperature during cooling from 70°C to <3°C within 90 minutes.</p>
        <p>Use data loggers to continuously monitor batch temperatures, recording readings every 5 minutes.</p>
        <p>If the temperature exceeds 3°C or cooling time exceeds 90 minutes, discard the batch and investigate the cause.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Regeneration (Reheating):</strong>
        <p>The core temperature must reach >75°C for every trolley load served.</p>
        <p>Probe each trolley load with a calibrated thermometer at the point of service, checking every meal.</p>
        <p>If the temperature is ≤75°C, return the food to the oven until the required temperature is achieved.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Diet-Coded Meals:</strong>
        <p>Each meal tray must match the patient dietary code accurately.</p>
        <p>Verify the patient name, ward, and diet code during tray assembly, conducting checks for every tray.</p>
        <p>If a tray is assembled incorrectly, immediately correct it and retrain staff on the verification process.</p>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Ward-Level Holding:</strong>
        <p>Food must arrive at the patient >63°C (hot) or <8°C (cold).</p>
        <p>Measure food temperatures using a probe thermometer upon arrival at the ward, checking every trolley.</p>
        <p>If food is not at the required temperature, serve immediately or reheat/cool as necessary and document the incident.</p>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Temperature not logged at ward level; only at kitchen dispatch. 
        <br><strong>Fix:</strong> Implement logging at both points to track temperature changes during transit.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Modified-texture meals not individually labelled with patient name and IDDSI level.
        <br><strong>Fix:</strong> Ensure every modified-texture meal is labelled before tray assembly.
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Mistake:</strong> Regeneration oven not calibrated; readings show 80°C but food core only reaches 65°C.
        <br><strong>Fix:</strong> Calibrate the oven regularly and verify with a probe thermometer to ensure compliance.
    </li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management and receiving checks are critical to maintaining food safety in hospital catering. Your suppliers must provide documentation demonstrating adherence to HACCP standards and ensure that products meet the specific needs of vulnerable patients. Implementing strict checks at delivery helps prevent foodborne illness and ensures compliance with relevant regulations.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Verify full HACCP documentation from the supplier, including chill curve data per batch for cook-chill items.</li>
    <li>Ensure modified-texture products have an IDDSI compliance certificate from the manufacturer.</li>
    <li>Check for allergen-free and special diet items with dedicated production line evidence to prevent cross-contact.</li>
    <li>Confirm that supplements and enteral feeds are within their use-by dates and stored per manufacturer requirements.</li>
    <li>Inspect packaging for signs of damage or contamination before acceptance.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Missing or incomplete HACCP documentation, including chill curve data.</li>
    <li>Modified-texture meals lacking individual labels with patient name and IDDSI level.</li>
    <li>Temperature readings above 8°C for cold items or above 5°C for frozen items upon delivery.</li>
    <li>Any evidence of cross-contact with allergens in packaging or product.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always document any delivery issues in your receiving log. This creates a clear record for audits and helps identify recurring supplier problems.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Check and log cook-chill batch temperatures from 70°C to <3°C within 90 min.</li>
    <li>Probe and log core temperatures of each trolley load at >75°C during regeneration.</li>
    <li>Verify that each meal tray matches patient dietary code, including modified texture and IDDSI levels.</li>
    <li>Ensure food arrives at patient >63°C (hot) or <8°C (cold) upon service.</li>
    <li>Monitor and log the temperature of food during transit, especially in non-air-conditioned corridors.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Cook-chill documentation, including chill curve data for every batch from the supplier.</li>
    <li>Temperature logs for each meal during preparation, transit, and service.</li>
    <li>Diet-coded meal assembly records, showing patient name, ward, and diet code verification.</li>
    <li>Calibration records for regeneration ovens, demonstrating consistent temperature accuracy.</li>
    <li>Incident reports for any breaches in protocol, with corrective actions taken.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<ul>
    <li>All catering staff must understand that hospital patients are 'vulnerable persons' - zero tolerance for temperature breaches.</li>
    <li>Tray assembly team must complete diet code verification training to match patient name, ward, and diet code on every tray.</li>
    <li>Ward hostesses need training on probe thermometer use at the point of service, ensuring food is >63°C at patient delivery.</li>
    <li>Infection control links must attend regular updates, understanding protocols for bay closures and outbreak meal procedures.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>

<p>Hospital catering must comply with EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(5) for temperature control at the point of consumption, ensuring food is served at >63°C for hot meals and <8°C for cold meals. Staff training must align with EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. XII, focusing on the unique needs of vulnerable patients.</p><p>Suppliers must provide full HACCP documentation as stipulated in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. IX(1) to verify compliance with food safety standards.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Review and update your temperature logging procedures at both kitchen and ward levels.</li>
    <li>Ensure all modified-texture meals are individually labelled with patient names and IDDSI levels.</li>
    <li>Calibrate your regeneration oven to guarantee food core temperatures exceed 75°C.</li>
    <li>Conduct a training session for staff on diet code verification and patient safety protocols.</li>
    <li>Check supplier documentation for HACCP compliance and allergen declarations.</li>
</ol>
