<p>When food safety conversations turn to hazards, microbiological pathogens like <em>Salmonella</em> and <em>Listeria</em> dominate the discussion. Yet physical hazards—foreign objects that should not be in food—remain a persistent and highly visible cause of consumer complaints, retailer rejections, and costly recalls.</p>

<p>Unlike a pathogen you cannot see, a physical hazard is undeniable when discovered. A shard of glass in a ready meal, a stone in a bag of dried fruit, or a metal bolt in a sausage creates immediate consumer alarm and triggers mandatory recall reporting to the Food Standards Agency (FSA) or local authorities.</p>

<p>Under EU Regulation (EC) 852/2004 and UK food safety law, food business operators have a legal obligation to ensure that food is not placed on the market if it is unsafe. Physical contamination renders food <strong>unsafe</strong> and <strong>unfit for consumption</strong>. This article provides a structured approach to identifying, assessing, and controlling physical hazards within your HACCP plan.</p>

<h2>Part 1: Defining Physical Hazards</h2>

<p>A <strong>physical hazard</strong> is any extraneous object or foreign matter in food that may cause illness, injury, or distress to the consumer. The key distinction from quality defects is the potential for <strong>harm</strong>.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Category</th>
      <th>Definition</th>
      <th>Risk Level</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Physical Hazard</strong></td>
      <td>Object capable of causing injury (choking, laceration, dental damage)</td>
      <td><strong>Food Safety Risk</strong></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Quality Defect</strong></td>
      <td>Object that is undesirable but unlikely to cause harm (e.g., a single peppercorn in a peppercorn sauce)</td>
      <td><strong>Quality Complaint</strong></td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p>The Codex Alimentarius General Principles of Food Hygiene classify physical hazards as one of the three primary hazard categories requiring analysis in every HACCP plan, alongside biological and chemical hazards.</p>

<h2>Part 3: Hazard Analysis for Physical Contaminants</h2>

<p>Within your HACCP plan, you must evaluate each process step for physical hazards and determine significance using the Codex decision framework.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Identification</h3>

<p>List every potential physical hazard associated with each ingredient and process step. Be specific—<em>"Metal from grinder plate wear"</em> is actionable; <em>"Foreign material"</em> is not.</p>

<h3>Step 2: Risk Assessment (Likelihood × Severity)</h3>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Severity Level</th>
      <th>Description</th>
      <th>Example Outcome</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>High</strong></td>
      <td>Life-threatening injury (choking, internal perforation, severe laceration)</td>
      <td>Glass shard in infant food; metal fragment in ready meal</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Medium</strong></td>
      <td>Injury requiring medical attention (dental damage, minor laceration)</td>
      <td>Stone in dried fruit breaking tooth</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Low</strong></td>
      <td>Discomfort or minor complaint, no medical intervention</td>
      <td>Soft plastic film piece easily detected before consumption</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>Step 3: Determine Significance</h3>

<p>A physical hazard is <strong>significant</strong> and requires control through a CCP if:</p>
<ul>
  <li>It is <strong>reasonably likely to occur</strong> in your specific operation, <strong>and</strong></li>
  <li>It would cause <strong>serious harm</strong> to the consumer.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote><strong>Justification Example (Significant):</strong> "Metal fragments from grinder plate wear. Hazard is significant due to high severity (laceration/perforation risk) and moderate likelihood based on equipment maintenance history. Controlled at CCP-3 (Metal Detection)."</blockquote>

<blockquote><strong>Justification Example (Not Significant):</strong> "Stones in raw potatoes. Hazard severity is medium (dental damage). Likelihood is low due to supplier HACCP certification and incoming washing/inspection step. Controlled by Supplier Approval (PRP) and visual inspection at preparation."</blockquote>

<h2>Part 4: Control Strategies for Physical Hazards</h2>

<p>Physical hazards are controlled through a layered approach combining <strong>Prerequisite Programs (PRPs)</strong> and <strong>Critical Control Points (CCPs)</strong>.</p>

<h3>Tier 1: Prerequisite Programs (Prevention)</h3>

<p>Effective PRPs reduce the likelihood of physical hazards entering the process, minimising reliance on downstream detection equipment.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>PRP Category</th>
      <th>Control Measures</th>
      <th>Expected Documentation</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Supplier Management</strong></td>
      <td>Require suppliers to have HACCP plans addressing physical hazards; obtain Certificates of Analysis (COA) for foreign material screening</td>
      <td>Approved Supplier List; Supplier audit reports; Raw material specifications</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Facility Design &amp; Maintenance</strong></td>
      <td>Shatterproof lighting with protective covers in all production areas; no glass permitted on production floor; glass and hard plastic register</td>
      <td>Glass &amp; Hard Plastic Register; Monthly integrity audit records</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Equipment Maintenance</strong></td>
      <td>Preventative maintenance schedule for all food contact equipment; documented blade and wear-part replacement; metal detectable or X-ray visible materials where possible</td>
      <td>Maintenance logs; Work orders; Blade replacement records</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Personnel Hygiene</strong></td>
      <td>Strict policy: no jewellery (including wedding bands), no false nails, no loose buttons; use of metal-detectable plasters (blue, contrasting colour)</td>
      <td>Personnel GMP training records; Line supervisor checks</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Tool and Utensil Control</strong></td>
      <td>Only approved, food-grade, intact utensils permitted; colour-coded; no temporary fixes (e.g., tape, wire, cardboard)</td>
      <td>Tool inventory; Pre-shift equipment check log</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Waste and Wood Control</strong></td>
      <td>No wooden pallets in open product areas; no corrugated cardboard in high-care zones; immediate removal of damaged containers</td>
      <td>Hygiene inspection records; Waste removal schedule</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Inspection and Sorting</strong></td>
      <td>Incoming raw material inspection; optical sorters, air classifiers, or sieves for dry ingredients; visual inspection tables for produce</td>
      <td>Inspection logs; Sifter tailings analysis</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>Tier 2: Critical Control Points (Detection and Removal)</h3>

<p>When PRPs cannot guarantee prevention, a CCP is required to detect and remove physical hazards before product leaves the facility. The CCP is typically placed <strong>as late in the process as possible</strong>—ideally after final packaging.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>CCP Technology</th>
      <th>Application</th>
      <th>Detection Capability</th>
      <th>Limitations</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Metal Detection</strong></td>
      <td>Most processed foods; dry, wet, frozen, packaged products</td>
      <td>Ferrous, non-ferrous, and stainless steel (depends on calibration)</td>
      <td>Cannot detect non-metallic hazards (glass, stone, bone, plastic); product effect (e.g., high salt/moisture) can interfere</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>X-Ray Inspection</strong></td>
      <td>Wide range; excellent for dense contaminants</td>
      <td>Metal, glass, stone, calcified bone, some high-density plastics and rubber</td>
      <td>Higher cost; cannot detect low-density materials (wood, insects, hair); density of product matrix matters</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Magnets</strong></td>
      <td>Dry, free-flowing powders and granules (flour, sugar, spices)</td>
      <td>Ferrous metal fragments only</td>
      <td>No protection against stainless steel, non-ferrous metals, or other hazards</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Sieves and Screens</strong></td>
      <td>Liquid and dry ingredient handling</td>
      <td>Oversized particles including stones, lumps, packaging debris</td>
      <td>Mesh size determines effectiveness; requires integrity checks</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Filters and Strainers</strong></td>
      <td>Liquid products (milk, juice, sauces, oils)</td>
      <td>Particles above micron rating</td>
      <td>Filter integrity and change frequency critical</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h2>Part 5: Managing a Physical Hazard CCP (Example: Metal Detection)</h2>

<p>This is the most common physical hazard CCP in food manufacturing. The following protocol outlines compliant management.</p>

<h3>Critical Limits</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Ferrous Test Piece:</strong> Detected and rejected</li>
  <li><strong>Non-Ferrous Test Piece:</strong> Detected and rejected</li>
  <li><strong>Stainless Steel Test Piece:</strong> Detected and rejected (size appropriate to product and equipment capability)</li>
  <li><strong>Reject Mechanism:</strong> Confirmed functional; product successfully diverted to reject bin</li>
</ul>

<h3>Monitoring Procedures</h3>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Parameter</th>
      <th>Frequency</th>
      <th>Method</th>
      <th>Responsibility</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Detector Sensitivity Check</strong></td>
      <td>Start-up, every hour during production, product changeovers, end of batch</td>
      <td>Pass test pieces (ferrous, non-ferrous, stainless steel) through detector aperture; verify detection and reject activation</td>
      <td>Trained Line Operator</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Reject Mechanism Function</strong></td>
      <td>Each test piece check</td>
      <td>Confirm product is physically diverted; fail-safe mechanisms activate if reject bin full or airflow blocked</td>
      <td>Trained Line Operator</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Belt Stop / Alarm Function</strong></td>
      <td>Each test piece check</td>
      <td>Verify alarm sounds or line stops upon detection</td>
      <td>Trained Line Operator</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>Corrective Actions (When Metal Detection Fails)</h3>

<p>A metal detector rejection or failed test piece check requires <strong>immediate and documented action</strong>.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Scenario</th>
      <th>Required Corrective Action</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Test piece not detected</strong></td>
      <td><strong>Stop production immediately.</strong> Quarantine all product produced since the <strong>last successful test piece check</strong>. Investigate cause (calibration drift, product effect, equipment fault). Re-validate detector before restarting. Document all product disposition.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Product rejected by detector</strong></td>
      <td>Investigate cause of rejection. Record item found if possible. Re-screen rejected product through detector three times to confirm. If metal is confirmed, <strong>quarantine and destroy</strong> affected product. Investigate source of metal (equipment wear, ingredient contamination).</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Reject bin full / mechanism jammed</strong></td>
      <td><strong>Stop production.</strong> Clear jam and empty bin. Quarantine product produced since condition occurred. Re-validate detector function before restart.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>Verification Activities</h3>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Activity</th>
      <th>Frequency</th>
      <th>Responsibility</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Record Review</strong></td>
      <td>Daily / Per Shift</td>
      <td>QA Technician / Shift Supervisor</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Detector Calibration</strong></td>
      <td>Annually (minimum) or per manufacturer specification</td>
      <td>External Service Provider / Maintenance</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Challenge Testing</strong></td>
      <td>Annually or after major maintenance</td>
      <td>QA Manager</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Product Disposition Review</strong></td>
      <td>Weekly review of all reject events</td>
      <td>HACCP Team</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h2>Part 6: Glass and Brittle Plastic Control</h2>

<p>Glass is the most hazardous physical contaminant due to its sharp edges and radiolucency (invisibility to standard metal detectors). BRCGS Issue 9 and IFS Food Version 8 mandate strict glass and brittle plastic management.</p>

<h3>Required Controls for Glass</h3>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Requirement</th>
      <th>Implementation</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Register</strong></td>
      <td>Maintain an up-to-date register of all glass and hard plastic items in production areas (lights, windows, dials, clock faces, control panels)</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Inspection Frequency</strong></td>
      <td>Inspect all registered items at a defined frequency (e.g., monthly) and record condition</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Protective Measures</strong></td>
      <td>All glass items must be shielded, shatterproof, or located where breakage cannot contaminate product</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Breakage Procedure</strong></td>
      <td>Documented procedure for managing glass breakage, including immediate line stop, area isolation, product quarantine, and documented clearance</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>Prohibited Items</strong></td>
      <td>No glass permitted on production floor (drinking vessels, personal items, mobile phone screens)</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>Metal-Detectable and X-Ray Visible Materials</h3>

<p>Where possible, use materials that can be detected by downstream CCPs:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Metal-detectable pens, scoops, scrapers, and plasters</strong></li>
  <li><strong>X-ray visible rubber seals and gaskets</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Colour-coded, contrasting materials</strong> (e.g., blue plasters and gloves) for visual detection</li>
</ul>

<h2>Part 7: Common Audit Findings and How to Avoid Them</h2>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Finding</th>
      <th>Root Cause</th>
      <th>Prevention</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>

<h2>Part 8: Physical Hazards and Food Fraud</h2>

<p>Physical hazards can also signal intentional adulteration or food fraud. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Stones added to increase weight of spices or grains</li>
  <li>Substitution of ingredients with inferior, contaminated alternatives</li>
</ul>

<p>Your Food Defence Plan (TACCP/VACCP) should consider physical hazards as potential indicators of deliberate contamination. Any unusual pattern of physical contaminants—especially stones or extraneous material inconsistent with normal agricultural debris—should trigger a food fraud investigation.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaways for Your HACCP Plan</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Physical hazards are safety issues</strong>, not just quality complaints. They require systematic analysis under Codex Principle 1.</li>
  <li><strong>Prevention through PRPs is the first line of defence</strong>—supplier approval, maintenance, GMPs, and facility controls.</li>
  <li><strong>Metal detection and X-ray are the primary CCPs</strong> for physical hazard control in manufacturing.</li>
  <li><strong>Glass requires a dedicated management program</strong> including a register, routine inspection, and breakage procedures.</li>
  <li><strong>Corrective action must address product disposition</strong>—quarantine, re-screen, or destroy affected product.</li>
  <li><strong>Verification is essential</strong>—test piece checks prove the CCP is working; record reviews prove the system is being followed.</li>
  <li><strong>Document everything.</strong> In a consumer complaint or regulatory investigation, your records are your only defence.</li>
</ol>

<p>By treating physical hazards with the same rigour as microbiological risks, you protect your consumers from injury and your business from the consequences of a preventable recall.</p>
