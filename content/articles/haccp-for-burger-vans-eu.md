<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>Many burger vans fail their audits because they don't have a probe thermometer on site. Relying on visual checks like "it looks done" can lead to serving undercooked patties, risking foodborne illnesses like E. coli O157:H7.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always keep a calibrated probe thermometer on hand and check the thickest part of the patty. It's your best defense against pathogens.</blockquote>

<h4>What you'll learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>How to implement effective temperature checks for burger patties and other high-risk items.</li>
    <li>The importance of raw and cooked food separation in your van.</li>
    <li>Best practices for maintaining hand hygiene and cleaning schedules.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Presence and calibration of a probe thermometer.</li>
    <li>Condition and accessibility of the handwash station.</li>
    <li>Storage practices, especially raw patties stored above ready-to-eat items.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Burger Vans</h3>

<p><strong>Biological Hazards:</strong> The primary pathogens of concern in burger vans include E. coli O157:H7, Campylobacter, Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus cereus, and Salmonella.</p>

<p>On a busy Saturday when you're flipping burgers at peak hour, the risk of cross-contamination increases. If raw burger patties are stored above cooked buns or salad, you can easily transfer E. coli to ready-to-eat items.</p>

<p>Control this by storing raw patties below cooked items in the fridge and using separate, colour-coded utensils for raw and cooked foods. Always cook burger patties to a minimum core temperature of 75°C (or 70°C held for 2 minutes).</p>

<p><strong>Chemical Hazards:</strong> Homemade sauces, especially those containing raw eggs, can lead to Salmonella contamination if not handled properly. Additionally, cleaning chemicals can pose risks if residues are left on food contact surfaces.</p>

<p>On a busy Saturday when you're preparing homemade aioli, ensure you use pasteurised eggs to mitigate Salmonella risk. Store all cleaning chemicals away from food preparation areas to prevent cross-contamination.</p>

<p>Regularly verify that cleaning solutions are correctly diluted as per manufacturer instructions, typically between 100-200 ppm for sanitising solutions. Rinse surfaces thoroughly after cleaning before food preparation.</p>

<p><strong>Physical Hazards:</strong> Physical hazards include foreign objects such as metal shards from equipment or plastic pieces from packaging. These can cause serious injury if ingested.</p>

<p>On a busy Saturday when you're rushing to serve customers, check for any loose items in your workspace. Ensure that all equipment is in good condition and that you regularly inspect food items for foreign debris.</p>

<p>Implement a routine check for equipment integrity and keep a dedicated area for packaging materials away from food. Perform a visual inspection before service begins to catch any potential hazards.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>

<p>During summer festivals, you'll face extended hours in high heat with high volume. Pre-prepare only what you can sell in 2 hours to avoid holding cooked items below 63°C.</p>

<p>In winter markets, gas regulator issues can arise due to cold weather. Always test your gas connections and generator reliability before each event to ensure consistent cooking temperatures.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points for Burger Vans</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Cooking Temperature:</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Core temperature of burger patties.</li>
            <li><strong>Limit:</strong> Minimum 75°C (or 70°C held for 2 minutes).</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Use a calibrated probe thermometer. Check every batch of burgers.</li>
            <li><strong>Corrective action:</strong> If below limit, continue cooking until the required temperature is achieved. Re-test before serving.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Raw/Cooked Separation:</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Storage arrangement of food items.</li>
            <li><strong>Limit:</strong> Raw patties must be stored below cooked buns and salad.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Check fridge layout at the start of each service. Use colour-coded tongs and utensils.</li>
            <li><strong>Corrective action:</strong> Rearrange items immediately if raw meat is above cooked food. Document the change.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Holding Temperature:</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Temperature of cooked items in warming trays.</li>
            <li><strong>Limit:</strong> Must stay above 63°C.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Use a probe thermometer to check every 30 minutes during service.</li>
            <li><strong>Corrective action:</strong> If below limit, reheat items to above 75°C before serving. Discard if they cannot be reheated.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    <li>
        <strong>Handwash Station:</strong>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>What to measure:</strong> Functionality of the handwash station.</li>
            <li><strong>Limit:</strong> Must have hot water, soap, and paper towels available.</li>
            <li><strong>How to monitor:</strong> Check at the start of each service; ensure it's unobstructed.</li>
            <li><strong>Corrective action:</strong> If not functional, cease operations until it is repaired and fully stocked.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Mistake: No probe thermometer on site → Fix: Always have a calibrated probe thermometer ready and check each batch of burgers.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Handwash basin blocked by equipment → Fix: Keep the handwash station clear and accessible at all times.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Raw burger patties stored above salad/buns → Fix: Store raw meat below cooked items and rearrange immediately if found otherwise.</li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management is vital to maintaining food safety in your burger van. Proper checks during delivery help prevent contamination and ensure your ingredients are safe to serve. Follow these guidelines to keep your operations compliant and your customers safe.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
 <li>Check that burger patties are delivered at <5°C to your commissary.</li>
 <li>Transport burger patties to the van in a cool box with a temperature log.</li>
 <li>Verify use-by dates on buns and salad; refuse any past the date.</li>
 <li>Store buns and salad separately from raw meat in the fridge.</li>
 <li>If using homemade sauces with raw egg, ensure pasteurised egg is used; check allergen labels on bought sauces.</li>
 <li>Inspect LP gas connections, regulator, and hose condition before each service.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
 <li>Raw burger patties delivered above <5°C.</li>
 <li>Expired buns or salad with use-by dates that have passed.</li>
 <li>Damaged packaging that could compromise food safety.</li>
 <li>Homemade sauces lacking pasteurised egg when raw egg is used.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Always keep a temperature log for your cool boxes; it's a simple way to prove compliance if questioned by an EHO.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Probe thermometer calibrated and ready for use</li>
    <li>Cooked burger patties reach a minimum core temperature of 75°C</li>
    <li>Holding temperatures for cooked items >63°C</li>
    <li>Handwash station clear, stocked with hot water, soap, and paper towels</li>
    <li>Raw burger patties stored below cooked items in the fridge</li>
    <li>Cooking surfaces and utensils cleaned and sanitized</li>
    <li>Check gas connections and regulator before service</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature logs for burger patties upon delivery and during cooking</li>
    <li>Cleaning schedule for griddle, surfaces, and utensils</li>
    <li>Handwashing records, especially after handling raw meat or money</li>
    <li>Supplier delivery records, including temperatures and use-by dates for buns and salad</li>
    <li>Gas safety checks and maintenance records for equipment</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<ul>
    <li>All staff must demonstrate proper use of the probe thermometer on every batch of burgers</li>
    <li>Train staff on hand hygiene: wash hands after handling raw patties, money, and before assembling buns</li>
    <li>Solo operators must have a cancellation plan if ill (vomiting/diarrhea) and cannot trade</li>
    <li>Conduct regular refreshers on food safety practices, especially during peak seasons</li>
</ul>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>
<p>Comply with EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. III by maintaining a dedicated handwash station with hot water, soap, and paper towels. Ensure probe thermometers are used to check core temperatures of cooked burgers at 75°C minimum (or 70°C for 2 minutes) as per EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. I. Store raw meat below ready-to-eat foods and keep cooked items above 63°C, as outlined in EC 852/2004 Annex II Ch. VII.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Purchase a probe thermometer if you don't have one.</li>
    <li>Set up a dedicated handwash station with hot water and soap.</li>
    <li>Review and update your cleaning schedule for all surfaces and utensils.</li>
    <li>Organize your fridge to store raw patties below buns and salad.</li>
    <li>Train all staff on hand hygiene and probe thermometer use this week.</li>
</ol>
