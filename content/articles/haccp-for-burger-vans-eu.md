<div class="pp-article-callout"><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> In a burger van, the question is not whether the food safety rules are simpler because the space is smaller. The question is whether you can still prove control when the service is fast and cramped.</div>

<p>Burger vans combine several high-risk pressures in one small space: raw meat, ready-to-eat salad, hot holding, limited storage, limited handwashing access, and service periods where one operator may be doing everything at once. That is why a burger van HACCP plan has to be practical. If the controls are too vague, staff default to speed and shortcuts.</p>

<p>This guide focuses on the controls that matter most for mobile burger operations in the EU and UK: cooking, raw-to-ready separation, chilled storage, hand hygiene, allergen communication, and the evidence EHOs usually expect to see.</p>

<h2>Where burger van risk really sits</h2>

<h3>Undercooked burgers</h3>

<p>Core temperature control remains one of the main risks. Visual checks are not enough. A burger that “looks done” can still be undercooked in the centre, especially during peak service when batches are moving quickly.</p>

<h3>Cross-contamination in a tight workspace</h3>

<p>Raw patties, buns, cheese, onions, sauces, salad, and wrapping materials often move through the same small area. Without deliberate separation and hand hygiene, raw meat juices can transfer easily to ready-to-eat ingredients.</p>

<h3>Temperature control before and during service</h3>

<p>Mobile units rely on cool boxes, under-counter fridges, or temporary holding during events. Deliveries, staging, and refilling the service line all create opportunities for chilled food to drift out of control.</p>

<h3>Allergen communication under service pressure</h3>

<p>Burger vans frequently customise orders. Sauce changes, cheese additions, gluten-containing buns, and supplier substitutions all make allergen control harder if there is no current ingredient and recipe reference on site.</p>

<h2>What a burger van HACCP plan should cover clearly</h2>

<h3>Receipt and pre-service setup</h3>

<p>Your HACCP study should not begin at the grill. It should cover how ingredients arrive, how they are checked, and how the van is prepared before opening.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Receipt of meat, buns, salad, sauces, dairy, packaging, and cleaning materials</li>
  <li>Chilled transport or transfer into the van</li>
  <li>Pre-service storage layout and raw-to-ready separation</li>
  <li>Probe availability and verification</li>
  <li>Handwash station set-up and stocking</li>
</ul>

<h3>Cooking and hot holding</h3>

<p>For burgers, the HACCP study should define the cooking standard, how it is checked, what record is kept if you record batch checks, and what happens if a burger fails the standard. If hot holding is used for any cooked item, that also needs a clear control and corrective action.</p>

<h3>Assembly and service</h3>

<p>Assembly is where raw-to-ready separation, utensil control, glove misuse, and money handling often cause the real problem. The plan should spell out which utensils are dedicated, when hands are washed, and how ready-to-eat ingredients are protected during service.</p>

<h3>Allergens and menu changes</h3>

<p>If customers can add cheese, switch buns, remove sauces, or choose specials, the allergen controls must keep up. That means current ingredient information on site, staff who know where to check it, and a rule that no one guesses.</p>

<h2>Controls EHOs often focus on first</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Whether a probe thermometer is present, usable, and understood by staff</li>
  <li>Whether raw meat is separated from buns, salad, cheese, and sauces</li>
  <li>Whether the handwash station is accessible, stocked, and actually used</li>
  <li>Whether chilled foods are stored safely before and during service</li>
  <li>Whether staff can answer how allergens are checked</li>
</ul>

<p>If a burger van cannot show control in those areas, inspectors usually lose confidence quickly in the rest of the food safety management system.</p>

<h2>Common burger van mistakes</h2>

<h3>Using one pair of tongs for everything</h3>

<p>This is a classic cross-contamination failure. The van may have a written separation rule, but the live service setup undermines it. Dedicated utensils are one of the easiest controls to observe and verify.</p>

<h3>Depending on memory for cooking checks</h3>

<p>Busy operators often say they can tell by touch, colour, or time on the grill. That is not a strong control if the product changes thickness, the grill load changes, or a new staff member takes over.</p>

<h3>Handwashing blocked by workflow</h3>

<p>A handwash sink that exists but is blocked by stock, empty of paper towels, or ignored during service does not count as effective control.</p>

<h3>Out-of-date allergen information</h3>

<p>Supplier substitutions for buns, sauces, cheese, or toppings are common in mobile catering. If the ingredient file is old or only one person knows the change, allergen answers become unreliable.</p>

<h2>A realistic record set for a burger van</h2>

<p>You do not need a huge paperwork stack, but you do need records that reflect the highest-risk stages.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Opening checks for handwash facilities, temperatures, and equipment readiness</li>
  <li>Any routine temperature checks used for chilled storage or hot holding</li>
  <li>Probe verification or calibration checks appropriate to your system</li>
  <li>Cleaning records for food contact areas and service equipment</li>
  <li>Current allergen and supplier information for sauces, buns, cheese, and toppings</li>
</ul>

<p>For newer operators, our <a href="/articles/haccp-checklist-for-new-food-businesses">HACCP checklist for new food businesses</a> is a useful companion to this guide.</p>

<h2>How to make the system workable during peak service</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Set the van layout so raw and ready-to-eat items do not compete for the same space</li>
  <li>Use clearly separated utensils and easy-to-reach sanitising steps</li>
  <li>Keep current allergen information on site and easy to reference fast</li>
  <li>Make cooking and holding checks simple enough to use when busy</li>
  <li>Brief staff on what to do when one control fails, not just what “good” looks like</li>
</ul>

<p>A system that only works when the van is quiet is not a working HACCP system.</p>

<h2>Final takeaway</h2>

<p>Good burger van HACCP is mostly about controlling the obvious risks consistently in a difficult environment: safe cooking, raw-to-ready separation, accessible handwashing, chilled storage, and reliable allergen information. If the controls are practical and visible in service, the paperwork becomes easier to defend. If the paperwork looks better than the van actually runs, inspectors will usually spot the gap quickly.</p>
