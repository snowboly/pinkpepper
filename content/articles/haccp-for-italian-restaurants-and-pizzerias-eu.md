<p><em>Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 • Commission Notice 2022/C 355/01 • SFBB Adaptation for Italian Cuisine</em></p>

<h2>1. Regulatory Framework</h2>

<p>Article 5 of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires food business operators to "put in place, implement and maintain a permanent procedure based on the HACCP principles." The seven principles are: hazard identification, CCP determination, critical limits, monitoring, corrective actions, verification, and documentation commensurate with business size.</p>

<p>The 2022 Commission Notice (2022/C 355/01) provides authoritative guidance, formalising <strong>Operational Prerequisite Programmes (OPRPs)</strong>—essential controls not suited to binary critical limits. For Italian kitchens, dough fermentation control, batch-cooked sauce cooling, and pizza topping management are more appropriately OPRPs than CCPs.</p>

<p>The FSA's Safer Food Better Business (SFBB) framework organises compliance around the "Four Cs": Cross-contamination, Cleaning, Chilling, and Cooking. While no dedicated Italian cuisine SFBB pack exists, operators may adapt the general catering pack. SFBB constitutes documented compliance when completed and maintained.</p>

<h2>2. Cuisine-Specific Hazards</h2>

<h3>2.1 Pizza Production: Dough, Toppings, and Baking</h3>

<p>Research applying HACCP to pizza specialty restaurants identified three Critical Control Points in pizza production: <strong>receiving, topping, and baking</strong>.</p>

<p>The baking step provides a reliable kill step—provided temperature and time are adequate. The greater hazards lie in <strong>pre-baking cross-contamination</strong> (toppings held at ambient temperature during service) and <strong>post-baking handling</strong> (contaminated cutting wheels, serving boards).</p>

<h3>2.2 Sauce Cooling: Batch Preparation of Tomato and Ragù</h3>

<p>Italian restaurants prepare large batches of tomato sauce, ragù (meat sauce), and béchamel. These high-moisture, nutrient-rich products support pathogen growth if improperly cooled. Traditional deep-container cooling is inadequate.</p>

<p>Sauces must cool from ≥60°C to ≤20°C within 2 hours, then to ≤5°C within a further 2 hours. Shallow gastronorm containers (depth ≤5 cm) and blast chillers or ice-water baths are essential.</p>

<h3>2.3 Cured Meats and Cheese: Ready-to-Eat Hazards</h3>

<p>Italian cuisine relies heavily on RTE products: prosciutto, salami, mortadella, mozzarella, ricotta, and mascarpone. These items receive no kill step before service. The primary hazard is <strong>Listeria monocytogenes</strong> in RTE foods, with Regulation (EU) 2021/382 introducing stricter criteria for ready-to-eat products.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Refrigerated storage at ≤5°C (strict cold chain)</li>
  <li>Segregation from raw meat and unwashed produce</li>
  <li>Dedicated slicing equipment, sanitised between different products</li>
  <li>Date-marking of opened products; use within manufacturer guidance</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.4 Pasta Preparation: Fresh Egg Pasta Risks</h3>

<p>Fresh pasta containing egg presents Salmonella risk if undercooked or cross-contaminated. Dried pasta presents lower microbiological risk but requires protection from physical and chemical hazards during storage.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Fresh pasta: refrigerated storage ≤5°C; use within 24–48 hours</li>
  <li>Egg pasta: thorough cooking (core temperature ≥75°C)</li>
  <li>Dried pasta: sealed storage away from chemicals; pest monitoring</li>
</ul>

<h3>2.5 Salad Items: The Antipasto Challenge</h3>

<p>Research on salad items in pizza restaurants found all procedures performed within the food safety danger zone (5–60°C), with particular concerns regarding separate use of knives and cutting boards, and hand washing habits.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Thorough washing of all salad vegetables in potable water</li>
  <li>Dedicated colour-coded boards (brown for unwashed vegetables, green for RTE salad)</li>
  <li>Preparation in small batches, held refrigerated until service</li>
  <li>Separate disposal of trimmings and leftovers</li>
</ul>

    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Baking</td>

<p><strong>Post-baking handling (OPRP):</strong> Research found pizza cutting knives and serving boards with elevated microbial counts. Control requires dedicated clean cutting wheels sanitised between uses, no bare-hand contact with baked pizza, and clean serving boards—single-use liners preferred.</p>

<h3>3.1 The Baking Step: Validating the Kill Step</h3>

<p>The baking step provides the primary microbiological control. Validation requires:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Oven temperature verified</li>
  <li>Baking time sufficient for core temperature to reach ≥75°C</li>
  <li>Visual indicators: cheese fully melted and bubbling; base evenly browned</li>
  <li>Periodic verification with probe thermometer</li>
</ul>

<h2>4. Dough Management: Fermentation and Proofing</h2>

<p>Pizza dough presents unique considerations. The fermentation process (8–72 hours) involves ambient or controlled-temperature holding. While the low water activity and competitive microflora of fermented dough inhibit pathogen growth, the dough surface may be exposed to environmental contamination.</p>

<p><strong>Control measures:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Covered containers during fermentation</li>
  <li>Refrigerated fermentation (≤5°C) for extended periods</li>
  <li>Clean, food-grade containers; dedicated dough trays</li>
  <li>Hand hygiene during dough handling</li>
</ul>

<h2>5. Documentation and Verification</h2>

<h3>5.1 Records That Attract Scrutiny</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sauce cooling logs:</strong> Time/temperature from cooking to ≤5°C</li>
  <li><strong>Refrigerator temperature records:</strong> Daily min/max ≤5°C for all units</li>
  <li><strong>Pizza topping refrigeration:</strong> Prep unit temperatures during service</li>
  <li><strong>Oven/baking verification:</strong> Periodic core temperature checks</li>
  <li><strong>Allergen matrix:</strong> Current and verified against supplier specifications</li>
  <li><strong>Cleaning records:</strong> Cutting wheels, serving boards, dough equipment</li>
</ul>

<h3>5.2 Internal Verification</h3>

<p>Verification (HACCP Principle 6) requires periodic evaluation:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Weekly management walk-through observing sauce cooling, topping refrigeration, and post-baking handling</li>
  <li>Monthly thermometer calibration (ice-point and boiling-point methods)</li>
  <li>Quarterly documentation review</li>
  <li>Annual HACCP plan review triggered by menu changes or new suppliers</li>
</ul>

<p>These activities should be recorded, demonstrating active management to enforcement.</p>

<h2>6. Food Safety Culture</h2>

<p>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 amended Annex II of 852/2004 to require evidence of food safety culture: management commitment, employee awareness, open communication, and sufficient resources. For Italian restaurants, this means:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Documented staff training on dough handling, topping safety, and allergen awareness</li>
  <li>Pizzaiolo training on post-baking contamination risks</li>
  <li>Clear procedures for slicer cleaning between cured meat and cheese products</li>
  <li>A process for employees to raise concerns without fear of reprisal</li>
</ul>
