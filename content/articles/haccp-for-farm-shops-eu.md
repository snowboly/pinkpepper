<h3>Introduction</h3>

<p>Many farm shops struggle with compliance, especially when it comes to raw milk sales. A common failure is selling raw milk above 5°C, which not only violates regulations but also puts customers at risk of E. coli O157 contamination.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Always check that raw milk is stored at or below 5°C and that the required warning signage is prominently displayed.</blockquote>

<h4>What you'll learn</h4>
<ul>
    <li>Specific temperature controls and legal requirements for raw milk and other perishables.</li>
    <li>How to maintain effective separation between farm environments and food retail areas.</li>
    <li>Key supplier checks and staff training requirements for compliance.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Auditors Check First</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Temperature records for raw milk and other chilled items.</li>
    <li>Separation practices between animal areas and food retail, including boot wash stations.</li>
    <li>Compliance with allergen labeling, especially for homemade products.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Hazards Specific to Farm Shops</h3>

<p><strong>Biological Hazards:</strong> Key pathogens include E. coli O157, Listeria monocytogenes, Campylobacter, Cryptosporidium, and Salmonella.</p>

<p>On a busy Saturday when customers are allowed to pet lambs before entering the shop, there's a risk of E. coli O157 or Cryptosporidium contamination. This can happen if visitors do not wash their hands properly after animal contact.</p>

<p>Control Measure: Display extra handwash signage and ensure handwashing stations are stocked with soap and disposable towels. Remind staff to monitor and enforce hand hygiene compliance.</p>

<p><strong>Chemical Hazards:</strong> Chemical risks mainly arise from improper handling of allergens and homemade products without proper validation.</p>

<p>For instance, if a staff member prepares homemade chutney without checking the pH level, there's a risk of botulism due to low-acid conditions. The chutney could be sold without proper labelling or allergen information.</p>

<p>Control Measure: Validate the pH of homemade preserves to ensure it is below 4.6. Maintain written records of pH/Aw levels for each batch made.</p>

<p><strong>Physical Hazards:</strong> These include contamination from foreign objects and cross-contamination from farm environments.</p>

<p>On a busy Saturday when customers are handling both raw and ready-to-eat products, there's a risk of cross-contamination. If a staff member uses the same utensils for raw poultry and pre-packaged salads, pathogens can transfer.</p>

<p>Control Measure: Implement strict utensil separation protocols for raw and ready-to-eat foods. Regularly train staff on using color-coded equipment and ensure regular monitoring.</p>

<h3>Seasonal Considerations</h3>

<p>During spring lambing, the risk of E. coli O157 and Cryptosporidium peaks. Extra handwash signage should be displayed for visitors who pet animals before entering the shop.</p>

<p>In summer, the influx of local produce leads to rapid stock turnover. Enforce strict use-by dates on all farm-made items to prevent spoilage.</p>

<p>During the Christmas season, with pre-assembled hampers containing mixed ambient and chilled items, ensure clear storage instructions are provided to prevent temperature abuse.</p>
<h3>Critical Control Points</h3>

<ul>
    <li>
        <strong>Raw Milk Sales</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Keep raw milk below 5°C at all times.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Use a calibrated fridge thermometer, check every 2 hours.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If temperature exceeds 5°C, discard milk that has been above this limit for more than 1 hour and document the incident.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Farm-to-Counter Traceability</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Every product must trace back to farm/field/animal.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Conduct weekly reviews of traceability records and ensure all products are logged correctly.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If a product lacks traceability, remove it from sale immediately and investigate the source.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Separation of Farm Environment from Retail</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Ensure a proper boot wash/change station is in place and used.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Check daily that the station is stocked with disinfectant and functioning properly.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If not in use, enforce immediate staff training on proper hygiene practices and restrict access to the retail area until compliance is achieved.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Temperature Control for Mixed Retail</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Chilled items must be kept at <strong>≤ 5°C</strong>, ambient items at <strong>≤ 20°C</strong>, and frozen items at <strong>≤ -18°C</strong>.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Use separate thermometers for each zone, check temperatures every 4 hours.</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If any item exceeds the temperature limit, segregate it immediately and assess for spoilage before deciding on disposal.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
    
    <li>
        <strong>Homemade Preserves</strong>
        <ul>
            <li>Measure: Validate pH and Aw (water activity) levels for safety.</li>
            <li>Monitor: Test every batch; maintain records of pH (≤ 4.6) and Aw (≤ 0.85).</li>
            <li>Corrective Action: If levels are unsafe, discard the batch and review production methods to ensure future compliance.</li>
        </ul>
    </li>
</ul>

<h3>Common Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Mistake: Raw milk sold above 5°C → Fix: Implement strict temperature monitoring and staff training on compliance.</li>
    <li>Mistake: No separation between farm yard and food retail area → Fix: Install and enforce use of boot wash/change stations.</li>
    <li>Mistake: Homemade preserves without validated pH/Aw records → Fix: Conduct regular testing and maintain records for all batches produced.</li>
</ul>
<p>Supplier management is critical for maintaining food safety and quality in farm shops. You are responsible for every product you sell, whether made on-site or sourced from local producers. Implementing strict receiving checks can prevent contamination and ensure that your customers receive safe, high-quality products.</p>

<h3>Delivery Acceptance Checklist</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Verify each supplier's food business registration and allergen specifications.</li>
    <li>Check raw milk for current quarterly TB test results and somatic cell count records.</li>
    <li>Confirm brucellosis-free status before accepting raw dairy products.</li>
    <li>Ensure game from local shoots is tagged with the kill date and that the dealer license is verified.</li>
    <li>Inspect homemade preserves for validated pH/Aw records to mitigate botulism risk.</li>
</ul>

<h3>When to Reject a Delivery</h3>
<ul>
    <li>Raw milk above 5°C or without required warning signage.</li>
    <li>Game that lacks proper tagging or documentation.</li>
    <li>Homemade preserves without validated pH/Aw records.</li>
    <li>Products from suppliers without valid food business registration.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote>Keep a binder for supplier documentation. When a delivery arrives, check it against this binder to streamline your acceptance process and maintain accountability.</blockquote>
<h3>Daily Monitoring Checklist</h3>
<ul>
 <li>Check raw milk temperature: <5°C.</li>
 <li>Verify raw milk warning signage is visible.</li>
 <li>Inspect that boot wash/change station is operational.</li>
 <li>Monitor all chilled storage areas for temperatures: <5°C.</li>
 <li>Ensure separation between farm animal areas and food retail is maintained.</li>
 <li>Confirm that homemade preserves have validated pH/Aw records.</li>
 <li>Check eggs are stored at <5°C if labelled 'keep refrigerated'.</li>
 <li>Review stock for use-by dates, especially on farm-made items.</li>
</ul>

<h3>What Records Auditors Expect</h3>
<ul>
 <li>Batch records for all on-farm products, including date, ingredients, and temperature logs.</li>
 <li>Quarterly TB test results for raw milk, along with cow somatic cell count records.</li>
 <li>Allergen specifications from local producers for any items sold.</li>
 <li>Game tagging records, including kill date and dealer license verification.</li>
 <li>Documentation of staff training sessions, including dates and topics covered.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Staff Training Requirements</h3>
<ul>
 <li>All staff must understand the 14 allergens and provide written allergen information for homemade products.</li>
 <li>Staff transitioning from animal areas to food handling must change outer clothing and wash hands thoroughly.</li>
 <li>Raw milk staff must be trained on legal requirements, including signage, temperature control, and TB records.</li>
 <li>Seasonal workers must complete a food hygiene induction before handling any food for sale.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Conclusion</h3>

<p>Farm shops must adhere to specific regulatory requirements to ensure food safety. Comply with EC 852/2004 Annex II (general hygiene), EC 853/2004 Annex III (products of animal origin), and the Raw Drinking Milk (England) Regulations 2006 (sale restrictions, labelling, TB testing). Additionally, understand allergen management as outlined in Regulation 1169/2011 Annex II, which applies to all food sold.</p>

<h3>Quick-Start Action Plan</h3>
<ol>
    <li>Review and update your food safety management system to align with EC 852/2004 Annex II requirements.</li>
    <li>Check all raw milk products for correct signage and ensure they are stored at <5°C.</li>
    <li>Conduct a thorough inspection of your retail area to ensure separation from animal contact zones.</li>
    <li>Verify that all homemade preserves have validated pH/Aw records to mitigate botulism risk.</li>
    <li>Train staff on allergen awareness and the proper procedures for handling raw milk products.</li>
</ol>
