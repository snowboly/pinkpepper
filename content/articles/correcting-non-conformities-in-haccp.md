<div class="pp-article-callout"><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> A non-conformity is not “closed” because the immediate problem stopped. It is closed when you can show what was contained, what was fixed, and why it is less likely to happen again.</div>

<p>Many HACCP systems describe corrective action in one line and then leave the team to improvise when something actually goes wrong. That is why non-conformities often repeat. The product may be moved, reheated, discarded, or relabelled, but the system rarely captures the full chain of decisions: what happened, what was done immediately, whether product was affected, what caused the failure, and what changed afterwards.</p>

<p>This guide focuses on practical corrective action for HACCP non-conformities. If you are looking at the wider consequences of poor control during inspection, use our related guide on <a href="/articles/failed-haccp-inspection-consequences-uk">incomplete HACCP plans and failed inspections</a>.</p>

<h2>What counts as a HACCP non-conformity</h2>

<p>A non-conformity is any failure to meet the control, procedure, or record requirement you rely on to keep food safe. That may include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>A critical limit breach such as a failed cooking or chilling result</li>
  <li>A missing monitoring check at a defined control step</li>
  <li>Incorrect or missing allergen or label information</li>
  <li>Use of the wrong packaging or an out-of-date recipe record</li>
  <li>Staff practice that does not match the documented procedure</li>
</ul>

<p>Some non-conformities are minor documentation gaps. Others create immediate product risk. Your response needs to reflect the actual severity.</p>

<h2>The four parts of a strong corrective action response</h2>

<h3>1. Contain the immediate problem</h3>

<p>First stop the issue getting worse. That may mean holding product, stopping packing, taking equipment out of use, removing a staff member from a task, or switching to a safe alternative process.</p>

<h3>2. Make a product decision</h3>

<p>Ask whether any food produced, stored, packed, or served during the failure may be unsafe or misdescribed. This is where many records stay too vague. “Issue corrected” is not the same as a product disposition decision.</p>

<h3>3. Investigate the cause</h3>

<p>Look beyond the visible failure. Was it training, staffing, equipment, supervision, recipe control, packaging control, record design, or workload pressure? Without root cause, the same failure usually returns.</p>

<h3>4. Put preventive action in place</h3>

<p>Preventive action might mean changing the form, the check frequency, the workflow, the equipment, the sign-off step, or the training. It should be specific enough that another person could verify the change later.</p>

<h2>What weak corrective action looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>“Staff reminded” with no record of retraining or process change</li>
  <li>“Food discarded” with no note of what lot or batch was affected</li>
  <li>“Temperature corrected” with no explanation of why the breach happened</li>
  <li>“Matrix updated” without checking labels, recipes, or release controls</li>
  <li>“Issue resolved” with no sign-off that the action was reviewed</li>
</ul>

<p>These responses may look tidy in the moment, but they do not build a defensible compliance record.</p>

<h2>Examples of better corrective action logic</h2>

<h3>Missed cooling check</h3>

<p>Containment might involve assessing the food currently in cooling, checking actual product temperature, and holding it pending decision. The record should then show whether product was used, reworked, or discarded, plus why the check was missed and how the monitoring process will be tightened.</p>

<h3>Undeclared allergen risk after recipe change</h3>

<p>Containment may involve stopping release, segregating old packaging, and reviewing affected stock. The preventive action may need to include stronger recipe change control, artwork approval, and line clearance, not just an updated allergen matrix.</p>

<h3>Repeated incomplete monitoring logs</h3>

<p>The right response is rarely “staff reminded”. You need to determine whether the issue is timing, ownership, form design, staffing, or management review. Repeat failures often point to a system problem rather than an individual mistake.</p>

<h2>What auditors expect to see in the record</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What the non-conformity was and when it was found</li>
  <li>Who identified it</li>
  <li>Which product, batch, area, or process was affected</li>
  <li>What immediate containment was applied</li>
  <li>What product decision was made</li>
  <li>What the root cause was judged to be</li>
  <li>What preventive action was assigned, by whom, and by when</li>
  <li>Who verified closure</li>
</ul>

<p>If any one of those steps is consistently missing, your corrective-action system is probably too weak.</p>

<h2>When a non-conformity should trigger wider review</h2>

<p>Some failures should not stay isolated to one record. Step back and review the wider system when:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The same failure has happened more than once</li>
  <li>The issue affects product release or allergen declaration</li>
  <li>Monitoring records were missing for more than one period</li>
  <li>Staff knowledge clearly did not match the documented procedure</li>
  <li>The problem exposed a recipe, supplier, or packaging change that was not controlled properly</li>
</ul>

<p>That is often the point where one corrective-action form becomes a management-control issue instead.</p>

<h2>Final takeaway</h2>

<p>Correcting a HACCP non-conformity means more than fixing the immediate symptom. Good corrective action shows containment, product decision, root cause, and prevention in one chain. When that chain is visible in your records, audits become easier and repeat failures become less likely. When it is missing, every non-conformity starts to look like a management weakness instead of a contained event.</p>
