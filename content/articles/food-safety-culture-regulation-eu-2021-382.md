<p>The EHO has finished reviewing your HACCP documentation. Temperatures check out. Allergen matrices are current. Cleaning schedules are signed. Then they walk the production floor. Within three minutes, they observe a line operative wipe a dropped spatula on their apron and return it to the filling line without sanitising. You correct it immediately, but the auditor's note is already written: "Positive food safety culture not demonstrated. Operator chose convenience over procedure when not observed." This scenario plays out daily since 2021. The regulatory landscape shifted from <em>what you document</em> to <em>how your people behave when nobody is watching</em>.</p>

<blockquote><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Auditors test culture by observing behaviours during unannounced walkthroughs, not by reviewing signed training records. A clean set of BRCGS culture KPIs will not compensate for a witnessed breach of personal hygiene protocol on the factory floor.</blockquote>

<h2>Part 1: Regulatory Foundation</h2>

<p>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 amends Annexes I and II of Regulation (EC) No 852/2004, effective 7 March 2021. Chapter XIa of Annex II now mandates that food business operators establish, maintain, and provide evidence of an <em>appropriate food safety culture</em> by complying with five specific requirements. This is not optional guidance. It is enforceable hygiene law equivalent to handwashing or pest control provisions.</p>

<p>Commission Notice 2022/C 355/01 clarifies that enforcement authorities should evaluate culture through observation, employee interviews, and review of leading indicators. The notice explicitly states that the presence of a policy does not constitute evidence of culture. Evidence of culture is behavioural.</p>

<p>For UK operators, Regulation (EU) 2021/382 was retained in domestic law post-Brexit. The Food Standards Agency expects local authority officers to assess culture during inspections under the Food Law Code of Practice. Non-compliance consequences range from improvement notices to escalated inspection frequency.</p>

<h2>Part 6: Practical Implementation Tools</h2>

<h3>Team Briefing Structure (5 minutes per shift)</h3>

<p><strong>Monday — Biological Hazard:</strong> One specific pathogen relevant to area. Example high-care: "<em>Listeria monocytogenes</em> grows at refrigeration temperatures. Control is dedicated footwear and handwashing." Verification question: "What temperature does Listeria grow at?" Expected: "Cold temperatures."</p>

<p><strong>Tuesday — Physical Hazard:</strong> Foreign body risk specific to line. Verification question on immediate action required.</p>

<p><strong>Wednesday — Allergen Focus:</strong> Specific allergen handled on that line and control measures.</p>

<p><strong>Thursday — CCP Review:</strong> Critical limit and required response to deviation.</p>

<p><strong>Friday — Near-Miss Review:</strong> Depersonalised issue from previous week with reminder of correct procedure.</p>

<p><strong>Documentation:</strong> Briefing log records topic, date, shift, and one example verification response. This is audit evidence for ongoing hazard awareness activity.</p>

<h3>Near-Miss and Concern Reporting System</h3>

<p><strong>Design Requirements:</strong> Anonymity (no name required), accessibility (QR code on noticeboards, canteen, toilet doors), simplicity (three fields maximum), amnesty policy (error not violation), feedback loop (monthly summary posted).</p>

<p><strong>Critical Audit Point:</strong> Zero reports over six months in a site with 20+ employees triggers a finding of "open communication not demonstrated."</p>

<h3>Resource Sufficiency Assessment</h3>

<p>One-page assessment required before any change affecting food safety resource. Sections: Change description, food safety impact assessment, mitigation measures, review date (max three months), Technical Manager sign-off.</p>

<h2>Part 7: Verification and Documentation</h2>

<p><strong>Method 1: Management Review of Culture Indicators.</strong> Monthly meeting agenda item covering near-miss volume, GMP audit behavioural trends, training comprehension verification. Annual review insufficient.</p>

<p><strong>Method 2: Employee Perception Survey.</strong> Minimum annually. Questions aligned to five pillars. Results trended year-on-year.</p>

<p><strong>Method 3: Behavioural Observation Audit.</strong> Separate from GMP audit. Conducted by independent internal resource.</p>

<p><strong>Method 4: Unannounced Drill.</strong> Simulated deviation (e.g., metal detector test piece "failure"). Conducted minimum quarterly.</p>

<p>Culture evidence is food safety documentation, not personnel documentation. Records must be available during food safety inspection.</p>

<h2>Part 10: Staff Training Requirements</h2>

<p>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 adds the requirement that training must result in demonstrable hazard awareness. Attendance records are not evidence of awareness. Comprehension verification is required.</p>

<p><strong>Training Content for All Food Handlers:</strong> Specific hazards relevant to their area, critical limits for any CCP they monitor, corrective action required, how to report a concern, amnesty policy.</p>

<p><strong>Comprehension Verification Methods:</strong> Scenario-based questioning, observation of task performance, peer verification. Initial verification at induction. Ongoing verification via team briefing questioning. Annual re-verification for all personnel in high-risk areas. Six-monthly for CCP monitoring personnel.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaways</h2>

<ol>
<li>Regulation (EU) 2021/382 is enforceable hygiene law. The five pillars are mandatory requirements under Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 Annex II.</li>
<li>Auditors assess culture through observation and interview, not through documented policy. Policy without operational practice is non-compliance.</li>
<li>Culture and compliance are distinct. A site can have perfect records and fail a culture assessment based on observed behaviours.</li>
<li>Evidence of culture must be generated through operational activity: team briefings with verification, near-miss reporting with feedback loops, management floor presence, resource assessments.</li>
<li>Zero near-miss reports indicates the reporting mechanism is unknown or untrusted. Competent authorities expect a baseline reporting rate.</li>
<li>Verification requires monthly management review, annual survey, quarterly behavioural observation, and unannounced drills.</li>
<li>Training attendance does not satisfy hazard awareness requirements. Comprehension verification is required and must be documented.</li>
</ol>
