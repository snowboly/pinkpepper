<div class="pp-article-callout"><strong>Audit Tip:</strong> Auditors scrutinise your "not significant" determinations as closely as your CCPs. Every hazard you decide not to control must have a written scientific justification in your hazard analysis worksheet.</div>

<p>Hazard analysis is often misunderstood as a simple brainstorming exercise, but it is actually the most critical scientific judgement in your entire food safety plan. If you fail to correctly identify a "reasonably likely" hazard here, the rest of your HACCP plan — no matter how well executed — will not protect your customer.</p>

<p>Here is your comprehensive guide to performing a hazard analysis correctly, covering the two distinct phases: <strong>Hazard Identification</strong> and <strong>Hazard Evaluation</strong>.</p>

<h2>Part 1: The two-phase approach to hazard analysis</h2>

<p>The correct approach is a methodical, two-step process that moves from broad identification to specific risk assessment.</p>

<h2>Part 2: Phase 1 — how to identify hazards correctly</h2>

<p>This phase is qualitative. You are brainstorming based on science and experience, not yet judging probability.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Map your inputs and steps:</strong> review your process flow diagram and assess every ingredient and step from receiving to distribution. A common mistake is overlooking hazards introduced by packaging materials or during maintenance and sanitation</li>
  <li><strong>Think across three categories:</strong> systematically consider all three hazard classes for every step:
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Biological:</strong> pathogens like <em>Salmonella</em>, <em>Listeria monocytogenes</em>, and <em>E. coli</em>; parasites; viruses (Hepatitis A, Norovirus); and spoilage organisms</li>
      <li><strong>Chemical:</strong> allergens, cleaning and sanitising residues, pesticides, mycotoxins (from mould), heavy metals, and unapproved food additives</li>
      <li><strong>Physical:</strong> hard foreign objects causing injury — metal, glass, stones, bone, hard plastic, wood splinters</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li><strong>Use multiple information sources:</strong> do not rely on guesswork. To ensure you do not miss emerging risks, consult published scientific texts, industry recalls, epidemiological outbreak data, and your own customer complaint records</li>
</ul>

<div class="pp-article-callout"><strong>Common Mistake:</strong> Vague descriptions like "contamination" are not sufficient. Be specific: <em>"Contamination with <strong>Listeria monocytogenes</strong> from the slicing environment"</em> or <em>"Introduction of <strong>metal fragments</strong> from the grinder plate."</em></div>

<h2>Part 3: Phase 2 — how to evaluate hazards correctly</h2>

<p>Once you have a list of potential hazards, you must filter them to find the "significant" ones. This is where many plans fail — either by ignoring real threats or by trying to make every minor issue a Critical Control Point (CCP).</p>

<p>The global standard is to use a <strong>risk assessment matrix</strong> based on two criteria:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Severity:</strong> if this hazard occurred, how serious would the health consequences be? (e.g., death, hospitalisation, minor injury)</li>
  <li><strong>Likelihood:</strong> in the <strong>absence</strong> of specific controls, what is the probability this will occur in <strong>your</strong> specific operation?</li>
</ul>

<h3>How to use the matrix</h3>

<p>A hazard is considered <strong>"Reasonably Likely to Occur"</strong> if it has both high severity and at least moderate probability.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Example (Significant):</strong> <em>Listeria monocytogenes</em> growth in a ready-to-eat deli salad. <strong>Severity:</strong> high (lethal in vulnerable populations). <strong>Likelihood:</strong> moderate to high if temperature abuse occurs during distribution. <strong>Conclusion: significant hazard</strong> requiring preventive controls.</li>
  <li><strong>Example (Not Significant):</strong> <em>Salmonella</em> in a raw chicken breast intended to be fully cooked by the consumer. <strong>Severity:</strong> high if not cooked. <strong>Likelihood:</strong> the hazard is present, but the intended use (cooking by the consumer) mitigates the severity. <strong>Conclusion:</strong> often handled by labelling and safe handling instructions rather than a CCP at the plant.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Justification is mandatory</h3>

<p>For <strong>every</strong> hazard you deem "Not Significant," you must write a scientific justification in the "Reason for Decision" column of your hazard analysis worksheet. Auditors will scrutinise this closely. For example: <em>"The hazard is not significant because the subsequent validated kill step (bake at 190°C for 20 minutes) achieves a 5-log reduction of <em>Salmonella</em> spp."</em></p>

<h2>Part 4: From hazard to control</h2>

<p>Once a hazard is deemed significant, the analysis dictates the required response.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Control measures:</strong> actions to prevent, eliminate, or reduce the hazard</li>
  <li><strong>CCP (Critical Control Point):</strong> a specific point in the process where control is applied to eliminate or reduce the hazard to an acceptable level — for example, metal detection or pasteurisation</li>
  <li><strong>PRP (Prerequisite Programme):</strong> general operational conditions maintaining a safe environment — for example, pest control, sanitation, and good manufacturing practices. You cannot make "cleaning the floor" a CCP</li>
</ul>

<h2>Part 5: Common pitfalls and pro tips</h2>

<h3>"One and done" mentality</h3>

<p>Hazard analysis is a <strong>living document</strong>. You must re-evaluate the plan when you change suppliers, change equipment, introduce a new product formulation, or learn of a new emerging pathogen.</p>

<h3>Group think</h3>

<p>Ensure the HACCP team includes diverse perspectives — maintenance, sanitation, quality, and production. A maintenance engineer knows about metal wear that a QA manager might miss.</p>

<h3>Pro tip: justify the "no"</h3>

<p>A thorough analysis explains <strong>why</strong> a hazard is <strong>not</strong> controlled by a CCP just as rigorously as why one <strong>is</strong>. Documenting the absence of risk — for example, "No physical hazard from glass because the facility uses shatterproof lighting and strictly prohibits glass containers" — protects you during audits.</p>

<p>By applying this structured, two-phase approach and documenting your scientific rationale, you ensure that your HACCP plan controls what truly matters: protecting the consumer and your brand.</p>
