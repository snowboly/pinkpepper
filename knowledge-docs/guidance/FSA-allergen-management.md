# Allergen Management — Practical Guidance

## Legal Basis

- **Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011** (FIC) — retained in UK as the Food Information Regulations 2014
- **Natasha's Law** (UK, October 2021) — mandatory PPDS labelling
- **EC 852/2004** — general hygiene duty (cross-contamination management)
- **Food Safety Act 1990** — offence to sell food injurious to health

---

## The 14 Major Allergens (Quick Reference)

1. Cereals containing gluten (wheat, rye, barley, oats)
2. Crustaceans
3. Eggs
4. Fish
5. Peanuts
6. Soybeans
7. Milk (including lactose)
8. Nuts (almonds, hazelnuts, walnuts, cashews, pecans, Brazil nuts, pistachios, macadamia)
9. Celery
10. Mustard
11. Sesame seeds
12. Sulphur dioxide and sulphites (>10 mg/kg or mg/litre)
13. Lupin
14. Molluscs

## Allergen Information — When and How

### Pre-Packed Food
- **Mandatory** allergen declaration in ingredients list, emphasised by typeset (bold, underline, italic, or background colour)
- Must be on the label permanently attached to or forming part of the packaging

### Food Pre-Packed for Direct Sale (PPDS) — Natasha's Law
Applies to food that is packaged at the place it is sold (e.g. shop, café, market stall):
- Must have **name of the food** and **full ingredients list with allergens emphasised**
- Even if the food never leaves the premises

### Non-Pre-Packed / Loose Food (Catering and Food Service)
- Allergen information must be available when requested
- Must be provided in a **written form** on the premises (e.g. on the menu, a chart, a folder, or on request sheets)
- Oral provision is permitted in addition, but cannot replace written documentation
- Staff must know where to direct customers and how to access allergen information

## Allergen Matrix / Chart

An **allergen matrix** (also called an allergen grid or chart) maps each menu item or product against all 14 allergens. Best practice format:

| Dish / Product | Gluten | Crustaceans | Eggs | Fish | Peanuts | Soy | Milk | Nuts | Celery | Mustard | Sesame | SO₂ | Lupin | Molluscs |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken soup | ✓ | — | — | — | — | — | — | — | ✓ | — | — | — | — | — |
| Caesar salad | ✓ | — | ✓ | ✓ | — | — | ✓ | — | — | ✓ | — | — | — | — |

- Review and update the matrix whenever a recipe or supplier changes
- Staff must know the matrix is not a definitive allergy guide — customers with severe allergies should always speak to a manager

## Allergen Control in Storage

- Store allergenic ingredients in **clearly labelled, sealed, dedicated containers**
- Keep allergens on **lower shelves** — prevents drip/spill contamination onto non-allergen foods above
- Designate separate shelves/areas for the most common allergens (particularly nuts, gluten, dairy)
- Use **colour-coded labels** to identify allergen-containing stock
- Separate dry allergen stores where production volume warrants it
- Check incoming deliveries — verify allergen status against supplier specifications

## Allergen Control in Preparation

### Segregation
- Designate **separate preparation areas** for allergen-free production where possible
- Use **colour-coded equipment** (cutting boards, utensils, containers, baking trays) — colour identifies allergen category
- Never use the same utensils, equipment, or surfaces for allergen and allergen-free food without an intervening clean and sanitise
- Consider allergen-free production scheduling: **allergen-free products first**, then allergen-containing products

### Cleaning Between Allergen and Allergen-Free Production
A validated cleaning procedure is required. "Clean" for allergen control does not equal "disinfected for microbiology" — the key concern is **allergen residue removal**:

1. Remove bulk food debris (dry clean, scrape)
2. Rinse with water
3. Apply detergent (alkaline or neutral — ensures protein breakdown)
4. Scrub contact surfaces
5. Rinse thoroughly with clean water
6. Verify: visual inspection, then allergen swab test (ATP testing does not confirm allergen removal)
7. Record the clean and verification result

### Cleaning Verification
- **Visual inspection** — minimum acceptable check; surfaces must appear clean
- **Allergen-specific lateral flow tests (rapid swab tests)** — available for gluten, peanut, milk, egg, soy; results in 5–10 minutes; should form part of cleaning validation
- **Protein swabs (biuret/colorimetric)** — indicates residual protein (non-specific)
- **ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay)** — laboratory method for quantitative allergen measurement; used for cleaning validation programmes

## Allergen Control in Cooking

- Use **separate cooking oil** for allergen-free foods (oil absorbs allergens from food)
- Replace oil in deep-fat fryers before allergen-free batches
- Use separate oven trays or line with fresh foil for allergen-free baking
- Cooking **does not destroy** food allergens — heat may reduce but not eliminate allergenicity
- Keep allergen-containing and allergen-free items physically separated in the oven, fryer, or cooking vessel

## Allergen Communication to Customers

### At Point of Order
- Menus should state where allergen information is available
- Signage: "Please inform a member of staff about any food allergies or intolerances before ordering"
- All front-of-house staff trained to direct customers to allergen information
- If unable to confirm allergen content: say so honestly — do not guess

### When a Customer Declares a Severe Allergy
1. Take the request seriously — always involve a manager
2. Review the allergen matrix with the customer
3. Assess whether allergen-free preparation is feasible (consider cross-contact risk)
4. If cross-contact cannot be controlled, inform the customer honestly
5. Never claim a dish is "allergen free" unless it has been verified through the full allergen-free procedure
6. Document the conversation where possible

## Precautionary Allergen Labelling (PAL)

"**May contain [allergen]**" or "**Not suitable for people with [allergen] allergy**" statements.

### When PAL Is Appropriate
Only after:
1. A **formal risk assessment** identifying an uncontrollable risk of cross-contact
2. All **reasonable technical and GMP controls** have been put in place and are insufficient
3. A genuine probability of cross-contact exists (not just a theoretical possibility)

### When PAL Is NOT Appropriate
- As a blanket precaution without risk assessment
- To avoid investing in proper allergen controls
- Where cross-contact can be controlled through GMP

PAL overuse is criticised by the FSA and allergic consumer groups because it restricts food choice unnecessarily. Enforcement authorities can challenge inappropriate PAL claims.

---

## Supplier Management for Allergens

- Obtain **allergen declarations / specifications** for all purchased ingredients
- Check for "may contain" statements in supplier specifications — these affect your own labelling/communication
- Conduct **supplier approval** including allergen information as part of specifications
- Re-check specifications on any supplier change or reformulation
- Retain copies of all specifications

## Training Requirements

| Staff Group | Training Required | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| All food handlers | Allergen awareness (14 allergens, cross-contact, labelling) | On induction; refresher every 2–3 years |
| Front-of-house / customer-facing | Customer communication on allergens; how to escalate | On induction; refresher as needed |
| Supervisors and managers | Full allergen management including risk assessment, PAL decisions, complaint handling | Level 3 equivalent; annual refresher |
| Kitchen leads | Cleaning validation for allergens; production scheduling | On appointment; when procedures change |

Training records retained for duration of employment + minimum 3 years.

## Allergen Incident Response

If a customer reports an allergic reaction following consumption of food from the premises:
1. Take the report seriously; express concern for their wellbeing
2. Obtain details: product consumed, symptoms, time of onset
3. Record the incident
4. Do not admit liability at this stage
5. Preserve any remaining product for testing (if applicable)
6. Review allergen records for the batch/service period
7. Report to the local authority / EHO as appropriate (serious incidents may be notifiable)
8. Review allergen controls and implement corrective actions
9. Cooperate with any investigation
