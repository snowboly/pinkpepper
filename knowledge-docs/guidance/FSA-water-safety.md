# Water Safety in Food Premises — FSA Guidance

## Legal Basis

- **EC 852/2004, Annex II, Chapter VII** — water supply requirements
- **Water Supply (Water Quality) Regulations 2016** (England) / equivalent for Scotland, Wales, NI — define potable water standards
- **Private Water Supplies Regulations 2016** — for premises using non-mains water
- **L8 ACOP (Legionnaires' Disease: The Control of Legionella Bacteria in Water Systems)** — HSE guidance, applies to premises with water systems
- **HSG274 Parts 1-3** — HSE technical guidance for evaporative cooling, hot/cold water systems, etc.

---

## Potable Water Requirement (EC 852/2004)

Food premises must have an **adequate supply of potable water** for:
- Food preparation and cooking
- Ice making (for food contact)
- Steam generation (in contact with food)
- Cleaning and disinfection of food contact surfaces and equipment
- Handwashing
- Personal hygiene

**Potable water** = water that meets the standards set by the Water Supply (Water Quality) Regulations — safe for human consumption.

### Key Potable Water Standards (England)

| Parameter | Standard |
|---|---|
| E. coli | 0 per 100 ml |
| Coliforms | 0 per 100 ml |
| Enterococci | 0 per 100 ml |
| Turbidity | ≤4 NTU at consumer's tap |
| pH | 6.5–9.5 |
| Nitrate | ≤50 mg/l |
| Nitrite | ≤0.5 mg/l at consumer's tap |
| Lead | ≤10 µg/l |
| Arsenic | ≤10 µg/l |

## Non-Potable Water

Non-potable water (e.g. recycled water, non-potable mains) may be used only for:
- Fire suppression
- Refrigeration (not in contact with food)
- Steam production where steam does not contact food
- Garden irrigation

### Separation Requirements
Non-potable systems must:
- Circulate in **separate, clearly identified pipework** (colour-coded: typically black or purple for non-potable)
- Have **no cross-connections** with potable water systems
- Be fitted with **backflow prevention devices** (check valves, air gaps, RPZ valves)

---

## Backflow Prevention

Backflow can contaminate drinking water supplies.

### Types of Backflow Prevention

| Device | Application |
|---|---|
| **Air gap** (AA, AB, AD types) | Highest protection; physical break between supply and vessel; e.g. tap above overflow rim |
| **Reduced pressure zone (RPZ) valve** | High-risk connections (e.g. chemical dosing systems); tested annually |
| **Double check valve** | Lower risk applications; e.g. garden hose connection |
| **Non-return valve** | Minimal protection; not suitable for high-risk applications |

Water companies can require RPZ valves on commercial food premises connections. Annual testing by a certified plumber required.

## Ice

- Ice must be made from **potable water**
- Ice makers: cleaned and sanitised per manufacturer schedule (typically monthly, or more often)
- Ice scoops: dedicated, stored hygienically (not in ice), cleaned after use
- Ice bins/storage: cleaned and disinfected weekly minimum; no bare-hand contact with ice
- If ice contacts food or is served in drinks — treat as a food safety hazard
- Ice from external suppliers: supplier must demonstrate potable water source; obtain supplier specifications

---

## Steam

Steam **in direct contact with food** (e.g. steam injection cooking, blanching, steam peelers) must:
- Be generated from potable water
- Not contain any chemical treatment substances unless food-grade approved
- Water treatment chemicals used in boiler systems (anti-scale, anti-corrosion) must be food-grade where steam contacts food; retain supplier MSDS/DoC

Steam not in contact with food (e.g. space heating, external cleaning) — no potable water requirement but Legionella risk management still applies.

## Private Water Supplies

Food businesses using a private water supply (borehole, well, spring, spring water from a surface source) must:
- **Notify their local authority** (regulatory duty)
- Have the supply **tested** by an accredited laboratory at minimum prescribed frequencies (E. coli, coliforms, turbidity, chemical parameters — frequency depends on supply size and type)
- Carry out a **risk assessment** of the supply (required under Private Water Supplies Regulations 2016)
- Treat the supply as necessary (UV, chlorination, filtration)
- Retain all test results

If a private supply fails potable water standards, the food business must:
- Not use it for food preparation, cleaning, or personal hygiene until remedied
- Use an alternative (tanked supply, bottled water) if necessary
- Notify the local authority and the EHO

## Legionella Risk Management

Legionella pneumophila thrives in water systems at 20–45°C, in stagnant water, and where biofilm, scale, or sediment accumulate. Legionnaires' disease is a potentially fatal pneumonia transmitted by inhalation of contaminated aerosols.

### Who Is At Risk?
Food premises with **cooling towers, evaporative condensers, hot/cold water systems, spa baths, humidifiers, or other aerosol-generating systems** must conduct a Legionella risk assessment.

Even premises with simple hot/cold water systems must consider Legionella risk under L8 ACOP.

### Key Control Measures

| System | Control Measure |
|---|---|
| **Hot water (calorifiers/cylinders)** | Store at ≥60°C; distribute at ≥50°C at all outlets within 1 minute of running; keep dead legs to minimum |
| **Cold water** | Store below 20°C; prevent stagnation |
| **Shower heads and hoses** | Clean and disinfect monthly; consider quarterly descaling |
| **Infrequently used outlets** | Flush weekly to prevent stagnation |
| **Cooling towers** | Clean and disinfect twice yearly minimum; dose with biocide; notifiable to HSE |
| **Calorifier cleaning** | Annual clean and inspect (or as risk assessment dictates) |

### Legionella Temperature Requirements

| Location | Target Temperature |
|---|---|
| Hot water storage | ≥60°C (kills Legionella within 2 minutes) |
| Hot water distribution | ≥50°C at all outlets within 60 seconds |
| Cold water distribution | <20°C at all outlets within 2 minutes |
| Risk zone | 20–45°C — control to avoid this range |

### Legal Duty
Under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) Regulations 2002, employers must manage Legionella risk:
- Written **Legionella risk assessment** (reviewed on system change or significant change in use)
- **Written scheme of control** (Water Safety Plan)
- **Responsible person** named for managing the scheme
- **Logbook** of all checks, monitoring, treatments, and maintenance retained (minimum 5 years)

## Post-Flood / Post-Mains Failure Procedures

If mains water supply has been interrupted or premises have been flooded:
1. Assume the water supply is contaminated until confirmed otherwise
2. Contact the water company for advice
3. Use bottled or tanked potable water for all food-contact uses
4. Flush the system thoroughly after restoration of supply (minimum 5 minutes per outlet)
5. Consider disinfection of the whole system if significant contamination is suspected
6. Do not resume food production until potable water is confirmed available
