# Cooking and Reheating Guidance for Food Businesses

## Introduction

Proper cooking and reheating are critical controls for destroying harmful bacteria and ensuring food safety. This guidance provides practical advice on achieving safe cooking temperatures and reheating previously cooked food.

---

## Safe Cooking Temperatures

### Core Temperature Requirement

**Food must reach a minimum core temperature of 75°C for 30 seconds** to ensure harmful bacteria are destroyed.

**Alternative validated processes:**
- 70°C for 2 minutes
- 80°C for 6 seconds
- Other time/temperature combinations validated through HACCP

**High-risk foods requiring particular attention:**
- Poultry (chicken, turkey, duck)
- Minced meat (burgers, meatballs, sausages)
- Rolled or stuffed meat joints
- Dishes containing eggs (omelettes, quiches)
- Reheated meals

---

## How to Check Core Temperature

### Equipment

- Use a **calibrated digital probe thermometer**
- Ensure probe is clean and sanitized before use

### Method

1. **Insert probe into thickest part** of the food
2. **Avoid touching bone or cooking vessel** (gives false reading)
3. **Wait for reading to stabilize** (typically 10–20 seconds)
4. **Ensure ≥75°C achieved for 30 seconds**
5. **Record temperature** on cooking log

### Multiple Items

- Check **each large piece** individually (e.g., whole chickens)
- For batches of smaller items (burgers, sausages), check **one item per batch minimum**

---

## Cooking Guidance by Food Type

### Poultry

- Whole chickens and turkeys: check thickest part of thigh (not touching bone)
- Chicken breasts: check centre of thickest piece
- Juices should run clear (visual check is not sufficient alone; always probe)
- **Target:** ≥75°C core temperature

### Beef, Lamb, Pork

**Whole cuts (steaks, chops):**
- Can be served rare (cooked on outside only) **if whole muscle meat from a known safe source**
- Surface bacteria destroyed by searing

**Minced, rolled, or mechanically tenderized meat:**
- Bacteria distributed throughout meat
- **Must reach ≥75°C throughout** (no rare burgers or rolled joints)

**Pork:**
- Traditionally cooked to higher temperatures (historically due to trichinella risk)
- Modern farmed pork: ≥75°C sufficient

### Fish

- Cook until flesh flakes easily and is opaque throughout
- **Target:** ≥75°C core (though fish often fully cooked at lower temperatures due to protein structure)

### Eggs

**Lightly cooked eggs (runny yolk):**
- Use only **Lion-stamped eggs** (UK) or pasteurized eggs for vulnerable groups (elderly, pregnant, young children, immunocompromised)
- Cook whites until solid

**Fully cooked eggs:**
- Cook until both white and yolk are solid (≥75°C)

**Egg dishes (quiches, omelettes, scrambled eggs):**
- Cook to ≥75°C throughout

## Cooking Methods and Considerations

### Ovens

- Preheat oven to required temperature before cooking
- Avoid overloading oven (restricts air circulation)
- Use oven thermometer to verify temperature accuracy
- Rotate trays if oven has hot spots

### Grilling and Frying

- Turn food regularly to ensure even cooking
- Probe thickest part to verify core temperature
- Beware of surface browning giving false impression of being cooked through

### Boiling and Steaming

- Food submerged in boiling water will reach 100°C (but check thick items reach temperature at core)
- Steaming: ensure sufficient steam and cooking time

### Microwave Cooking

- Microwaves heat unevenly (hot and cold spots)
- Stir or rotate food during cooking
- Allow standing time for heat distribution
- **Check temperature in multiple locations** (centre, edges, thickest part)

### Slow Cookers

- Ensure food reaches ≥75°C within 2 hours of starting to cook
- Do not use slow cookers for reheating (insufficient heating speed)

### Sous Vide

- Time/temperature combinations validated (e.g., 55°C for extended time for pasteurization)
- Requires specialist knowledge and HACCP validation
- Finished product often seared to destroy surface bacteria

## Reheating Previously Cooked Food

### Reheating Requirement

**Reheat food to a minimum core temperature of 75°C and serve immediately** (or transfer to hot holding ≥63°C).

### How to Reheat Safely

1. **Reheat food only once** (do not reheat multiple times)
2. Reheat quickly (do not leave food warming slowly)
3. Use high heat (oven, microwave, hob) rather than slow cooker
4. Stir or turn food to ensure even heating
5. Check core temperature reaches ≥75°C
6. Serve immediately or hold hot (≥63°C)

### Reheating Methods

**Oven:**
- Preheat oven to ≥180°C
- Cover food to retain moisture
- Reheat until core reaches ≥75°C (check with probe)

**Microwave:**
- Stir food halfway through reheating
- Allow standing time
- Check temperature in multiple places (microwaves heat unevenly)

**Hob (saucepan or frying pan):**
- Add small amount of liquid if needed to prevent sticking
- Stir frequently
- Bring to boiling point (100°C) and maintain for 1 minute

**Do not use slow cookers for reheating** (too slow; bacteria may multiply before safe temperature reached).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Relying on appearance alone** (colour, texture) — always probe
2. **Not allowing thermometer to stabilize** — wait for accurate reading
3. **Touching bone or cooking vessel with probe** — gives false high reading
4. **Cooking from frozen without adjusting time** — may not reach core temperature
5. **Reheating food multiple times** — increases contamination risk
6. **Using slow cooker for reheating** — insufficient heat
7. **Not stirring microwave-reheated food** — hot and cold spots

## Special Considerations

### Large Joints

- Use a meat thermometer or probe thermometer
- Allow resting time (temperature may continue to rise slightly)
- Thick joints may require longer cooking; verify core temperature

### Stuffed Poultry

- Bacteria from raw poultry can contaminate stuffing
- **Cook stuffing separately** (safer option)
- If stuffing cooked inside bird: ensure stuffing core reaches ≥75°C (check with probe)

### Batch Cooking

- Cook in batches small enough to ensure even heat distribution
- Do not overload pans or ovens
- Check temperature of multiple items per batch

---

## Record Keeping

- **Record cooking temperatures** for all high-risk foods
- Use a **cooking temperature log** (date, product, batch, core temperature, staff initials)
- Retain records for minimum **3 months**
- Supervisor reviews logs daily

---

## Training

Ensure all food handlers trained on:
- Importance of cooking to correct temperature
- How to use and clean probe thermometers
- Checking core temperature correctly
- Recording temperatures
- Corrective action if temperature not reached
