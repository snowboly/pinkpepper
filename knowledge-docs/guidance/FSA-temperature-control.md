# Temperature Control — FSA Guidance and Legal Requirements

## Legal Basis

- **EC 852/2004, Annex II, Chapter IX, Para 5** — foods supporting pathogen growth must be kept at temperatures not resulting in a health risk; cold chain must not be interrupted
- **Food Hygiene (England) Regulations 2006, Schedule 4** — specific UK temperature requirements for chilled and hot foods
- **Temperature Control Regulations (Northern Ireland)** — equivalent
- **Food Safety (Temperature Control) Regulations 1995** (Scotland) — still applies in Scotland

---

## Legal Temperature Requirements (UK)

| Requirement | Temperature | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Chilled food (general)** | ≤8°C | Schedule 4, Food Hygiene Regs 2006 |
| **Chilled food (specific products)** | ≤5°C or lower (see Annex) | Product-specific requirements |
| **Frozen food storage** | ≤-18°C | EC 853/2004 / Frozen Food Regs |
| **Soft whipped ice cream mix storage** | ≤5°C | Schedule 4 |
| **Pies, pastries, sandwiches with meat/fish/egg/dairy** | ≤8°C or chill-on-manufacture within 30 min | Schedule 4 |
| **Hot food holding** | ≥63°C | Schedule 4, Food Hygiene Regs 2006 |

> **Best practice**: Chill at ≤5°C (not ≤8°C) — reduces pathogen growth substantially; most reputable retailers and caterers use 5°C as their internal standard.

## Temperature Requirements by Stage

### Delivery / Receipt

| Product | Acceptance Temperature |
|---|---|
| Chilled foods | ≤8°C (≤5°C best practice) |
| Frozen foods | ≤-18°C (surface temperature) |
| Fresh raw meat / poultry | ≤7°C carcass; ≤3°C offal (EC 853) |
| Fresh fish on ice | Approaching 0°C in ice/meltwater |
| Fresh poultry | ≤4°C |
| Ambient ambient-stable products | Ambient (check for damage, pest signs) |

Reject delivery if temperature is outside acceptable limits (document the rejection; return to supplier).

### Cold Storage

| Storage Type | Target | Legal Maximum |
|---|---|---|
| Refrigeration (general) | ≤5°C | ≤8°C |
| Dairy/high-risk chilled | ≤4°C | ≤5°C (where specified) |
| Cold meat display | ≤4°C | ≤8°C |
| Blast chill post-cooking | Reduce from ≥63°C to ≤8°C within 90 minutes | ≤8°C when placed in chill store |
| Frozen storage | ≤-18°C | ≤-18°C |
| Defrost cycle | Brief excursion above -18°C acceptable during automated defrost cycle | Core of product must remain frozen |

### Cooking

| Food | Minimum Core Temperature |
|---|---|
| **All food (general UK recommendation)** | 75°C for 2 minutes (instantaneous) |
| **Scotland (legal requirement)** | 82°C (legal requirement under Scottish regs) |
| **Poultry (UK best practice)** | 75°C core; juices run clear; no pink flesh |
| **Pork, burgers, sausages, minced meat** | 75°C core (UK best practice — not served pink unless specific validated process) |
| **Whole beef / lamb (for rare serving)** | Surface-sear required; core may remain lower if validated process (HACCP-based) |
| **Sous vide / low-temperature cooking** | Requires validated time-temperature combinations (e.g. 55°C for 1 hour for beef) |
| **Reheated food** | 75°C core (England/Wales/NI); 82°C (Scotland) — only reheat once |
| **Rice** | Must be cooked thoroughly, cooled quickly, refrigerated, and reheated to ≥75°C once only |

> **Probe placement**: insert to the centre/thickest part of the product. Hold for 5 seconds to stabilise reading.

### Hot Holding

- Legal minimum: **≥63°C**
- Monitor minimum every 2 hours; discard if temperature drops below 63°C and has been held for >2 hours
- Or: serve within **2 hours** at any temperature (single-pass service only; discard remainder)
- Do not re-heat food that has been hot-held and has dropped below 63°C

### Cooling (Post-Cooking)

A two-stage cooling target:

| Stage | Target | Timeframe |
|---|---|---|
| Hot to intermediate | ≥63°C → ≤21°C | Within 2 hours |
| Intermediate to cold | ≤21°C → ≤5°C | Within a further 4 hours |

Total cooling time from hot to refrigerated: **6 hours maximum** (EC 852/2004 principle).

**Best practice**: Use a blast chiller — from ≥63°C to ≤3°C within 90 minutes (ideal).

Cooling methods:
- Blast chiller (most effective)
- Shallow trays / ice bath / stirring continuously
- Divide into small portions
- Never stack hot food in deep containers in the fridge — insulates and slows cooling

### Thawing / Defrosting

- Thaw in the refrigerator at ≤8°C overnight (preferred)
- Or: cold water bath (submerged, water ≤21°C, changed every 30 minutes) — immediate cooking required
- Or: microwave on defrost setting — cook immediately
- Never thaw at room temperature for extended periods
- Thaw drip/run-off must be caught and disposed of hygienically (cross-contamination risk)
- Once thawed: cook promptly; do not refreeze unless cooked first
- Log thawing date and time

## Temperature Monitoring Equipment

### Probe Thermometers

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Needle probe** | Core temperature of food items (meat, poultry, filled pastries) |
| **Surface probe** | Contact measurement of surfaces (delivery checks, worktops) |
| **Air probe** | Oven, refrigerator, blast chiller monitoring |
| **Penetration probe (thin needle)** | Small items, sauces, delicate food |

### Requirements
- Food-grade (food-safe probe materials)
- Accurate to ±0.5°C
- Cleaned and sanitised between uses (probe wipes or hot water wash)
- **Calibrated** at least annually (or more often if subject to impact) — ice point and/or boiling point method, or certified calibrator check

### Calibration Methods

| Method | Procedure | Expected Reading |
|---|---|---|
| **Ice point** | Insert probe into crushed ice slurry (50/50 ice/water) | 0°C (±0.5°C tolerance) |
| **Boiling point** | Insert probe into boiling water (adjust for altitude) | 100°C at sea level (±1°C) |
| **Reference thermometer** | Compare with UKAS-calibrated thermometer | Match within ±0.5°C |

If probe is out of calibration: quarantine the probe; reassess all temperatures checked with it since last calibration; remediate records.

### Data Loggers and Continuous Monitoring
- Refrigeration and freezer units should ideally have continuous temperature data loggers with alarm function
- Alarms set at: 8°C for refrigerators, -15°C for freezers (action before legal breach)
- Review and retain logs (minimum 3 months; longer where product shelf life warrants)

## Cold Chain Management

### Key Principles
1. **Never break the cold chain unnecessarily**
2. Minimise the time product is outside temperature control during handling
3. Rotate stock — FIFO (first in, first out): oldest product at front, new stock at rear
4. Check fridge/freezer temperatures at start of every shift and record
5. Refrigerators: do not overload (restricts air circulation); never store hot food directly in (raises air temperature)
6. Check door seals regularly — replace if damaged

### Delivery Vehicles
- Refrigerated vehicles: pre-chill before loading
- Monitor vehicle temperature during journey (data logger required for long hauls)
- Insulated boxes / cool boxes for short-haul/catering: validate holding time at correct temperature
- Document delivery vehicle temperatures on delivery records

## Temperature Records

| Record Type | Minimum Frequency | Retention |
|---|---|---|
| Refrigerator/freezer temperature checks | At least once daily (opening/closing checks) | Minimum 3 months |
| Cooking temperature checks | Every batch | Duration of product shelf life + 1 month |
| Cooling records | Every cooling event | Duration of product shelf life + 1 month |
| Hot-hold temperature checks | Every 2 hours during service | Per day / service period |
| Delivery temperature checks | Every delivery | Minimum 3 months |
| Probe calibration records | Per calibration event | Minimum 2 years |

---

## Exemptions

EC 852/2004 permits **limited periods outside temperature control** to accommodate the practicalities of handling during:
- Preparation (e.g. dough making, assembling)
- Transport (e.g. catering delivery)
- Storage (display for short periods)
- Service

The exemption applies where the risk to health is not increased — typically interpreted as a maximum of **2 hours** cumulative out-of-refrigeration time. This should be risk assessed and documented in the HACCP plan.
