# Commission Regulation (EU) 2024/3190 — Bisphenol A (BPA) and Bisphenols Ban in Food Contact Materials

## Overview
Commission Regulation (EU) 2024/3190 of 19 December 2024 restricts the use of bisphenol A (BPA) and a range of other bisphenols and hazardous bisphenol derivatives in materials and articles intended to come into contact with food. It amends Annex I to Regulation (EU) No 10/2011 (plastics FCM) and adds bisphenol restrictions to Regulation (EC) No 1935/2004 (framework FCM regulation). This is one of the most significant food contact materials regulatory changes in a decade.

*Note: Commission Regulation (EU) 2026/250 corrects certain technical errors in this regulation — see that regulation for minor corrigenda.*

- **CELEX:** 32024R3190

## Why It Matters
Bisphenol A (BPA) is an endocrine-disrupting chemical (EDC) used as a monomer and crosslinking agent in polycarbonate plastics and epoxy resins. It was previously authorised as a food contact material substance with a specific migration limit (SML) of 0.05 mg/kg. Following EFSA's 2023 opinion, which concluded BPA poses a health concern at exposure levels well below the former tolerable daily intake, the Commission moved to substantially restrict or ban BPA across most food contact applications.

**EFSA 2023 key finding**: The tolerable daily intake (TDI) for BPA was revised downwards by a factor of approximately 20,000 compared to the previous TDI — from 4 μg/kg bw/day to 0.2 ng/kg bw/day. This means there is effectively no safe level for BPA in food contact applications when dietary exposure is considered.

## Scope of the Regulation

### BPA (Bisphenol A) — CAS 80-05-7
**Prohibited from use** in:
- Polycarbonate plastics in contact with food (water bottles, baby bottles, food containers, kitchenware, storage containers, packaging)
- Epoxy resin coatings for food cans, beverage cans, drum liners, and metal closures (lids, caps)
- Varnishes, lacquers, and protective coatings on food packaging
- Thermal paper used as food packaging or product labelling in contact with food

**Specific Migration Limit (SML)**: Reduced to **non-detectable** (< LOQ of 0.05 μg/kg food) for categories still in phased transition

### Other Restricted Bisphenols
The regulation also restricts:
- **Bisphenol B (BPB)** — prohibited in plastics FCM
- **Bisphenol F (BPF)** — restricted (SML reduced)
- **Bisphenol S (BPS)** — new restrictions added
- **Bisphenol AF (BPAF)** — prohibited
- **Tetramethyl BPA (TMBPA)** — restricted

The regulation addresses concerns about substitution — i.e., manufacturers switching from BPA to structurally similar bisphenols with comparable endocrine-disrupting properties.

## Implementation Timeline

| Application | Prohibition Date |
|---|---|
| Baby bottles, sippy cups, feeding equipment | 1 January 2025 (already in effect) |
| Water bottles, reusable food containers (polycarbonate) | 1 September 2025 |
| Single-use food packaging (polycarbonate) | 1 January 2026 |
| Epoxy resin coatings on cans (beverages) | 1 January 2026 |
| Epoxy resin coatings on cans (food, room temp) | 1 September 2026 |
| Epoxy resin coatings on cans (food, retorted/sterilised) | 1 January 2027 (derogation possible) |
| Varnishes and lacquers on metal closures | 1 January 2026 |

*Transitional periods allow products placed on market before the respective dates to remain in circulation until their minimum durability date.*

## Who Is Affected

### Food Manufacturers and Packagers
- Products in BPA-containing cans (tinned tomatoes, fruits, vegetables, fish) must transition to BPA-free can coatings by the applicable date
- Review all food packaging specifications — polycarbonate must be replaced
- Epoxy-lined cans remain the area with the most complex transitional timeline; retorted products (high temperature processing) have the longest derogation

### Food Contact Material Manufacturers (Plastics, Packaging)
- Cannot place BPA-containing materials on the market for direct food contact from the applicable dates
- Must reformulate coatings, liners, and polymer compounds
- Declaration of compliance (DoC) under EU 10/2011 must be updated to confirm BPA-free status

### Catering and Food Service Operators
- Polycarbonate water jugs, storage containers, and serving equipment must be replaced with BPA-free alternatives by September 2025
- Check equipment supplier compliance declarations

### Importers
Imported food products in canned/coated packaging entering the EU must comply with the above dates. Border inspections may include food contact material testing.

---

## Testing and Compliance
- **Migration testing**: SML for BPA is now effectively zero (non-detectable at LOQ); testing must use validated LC-MS/MS methods capable of quantifying at 0.05 μg/kg food level
- **Declaration of compliance (DoC)**: FBOs must request updated DoCs from FCM suppliers confirming BPA-free composition from applicable dates
- **Derogation for retorted cans**: Subject to technical feasibility review; operators in this sector should monitor Commission guidance on the derogation timeline

---

## Practical Guidance for HACCP/Food Safety Plans
- BPA migration is a chemical hazard under HACCP. From the applicable dates:
  - Chemical hazard identification should include BPA if legacy packaging is still in use
  - Corrective action: transition to BPA-free packaging; do not use prohibited materials after cut-off dates
  - Supplier verification: require updated declarations of compliance confirming BPA-free status

## Interaction with Other Regulations
- **EU No 10/2011**: primary plastics FCM regulation (directly amended)
- **EC 1935/2004**: framework FCM regulation (general principles apply)
- **EU 2025/351**: 2025 amendment to EU 10/2011 covering recycled plastics (separate but related)
- **EU 2026/245 and 2026/250**: further 2026 amendments/corrections to plastics FCM and bisphenol provisions
- **REACH Regulation (EC) No 1907/2006**: BPA is also listed as a substance of very high concern (SVHC) under REACH
