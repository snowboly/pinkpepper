# Regulation (EC) No 1069/2009 - Animal By-Products and Derived Products

## Overview
Regulation (EC) No 1069/2009 sets the main EU rules for animal by-products (ABPs) and derived products not intended for human consumption. It matters for food businesses because once certain materials, waste streams, or returned goods fall outside the human-food chain, they may move into the ABP regime rather than ordinary food-law disposal rules.

- **CELEX:** 32009R1069
- **Official source:** https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32009R1069
- **OJ:** L 300, 14.11.2009, pp. 1-33

---

## Why It Matters
This regulation is central for questions about:
- catering waste
- meat, fish, dairy, and egg by-products
- disposal of food of animal origin
- returns, recalls, and withdrawals involving animal-origin material
- separation of food, waste, and ABP streams

For PinkPepper, it is an important boundary-setting source: not every discarded food item is handled only under HACCP or ordinary waste controls.

---

## Core Regulatory Function
The regulation:
- classifies animal by-products by risk category
- restricts how they may be collected, transported, stored, used, processed, or disposed of
- aims to control risks to public health and animal health

It works alongside more detailed implementing measures, but this is the main legal anchor.

---

## Key Practical Themes

### 1. Scope Beyond Food for Sale
The regulation becomes relevant when animal-origin material is no longer intended for human consumption, including certain waste, side-streams, and returned or condemned products.

### 2. Category-Based Controls
ABPs are divided into categories according to risk. Higher-risk material faces tighter restrictions on handling and disposal.

### 3. Segregation and Identification
Operators need clear separation between:
- food intended for human consumption
- food removed from sale but still potentially recoverable under food law
- material that must be treated as ABP

### 4. Storage, Transport, and Disposal
Containers, storage conditions, movement controls, and disposal routes matter. Mixed handling can create compliance and contamination risks.

### 5. Interaction with Hygiene and Traceability
ABP issues often overlap with hygiene, cleaning, pest control, documentation, and traceability, especially in mixed-use food premises.

## PinkPepper Retrieval Notes
This source should be surfaced when users ask about:
- disposal of meat, fish, dairy, or egg waste
- catering waste rules
- recalled or rejected animal-origin product handling
- whether a material can stay in the food chain
- separation of waste and edible stock

This source should usually be paired with:
- EC 178/2002 for withdrawal/recall and operator responsibilities
- EC 852/2004 for hygiene controls
- EC 853/2004 where animal-origin product rules are relevant

---

## Limits
This is not the main source for:
- ordinary consumer food labelling
- general HACCP structure
- non-animal-origin food waste in general
