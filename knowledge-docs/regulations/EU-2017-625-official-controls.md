# Regulation (EU) 2017/625 — Official Controls Regulation

## Overview
Regulation (EU) 2017/625 is the main EU framework for official controls and other official activities carried out by competent authorities to verify compliance with food and feed law and related rules. For food businesses, this is the enforcement-side regulation that sits behind inspections, sampling, import checks, and action on non-compliance.

- **CELEX:** 32017R0625
- **Official source:** https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2017/625/oj/eng
- **Current consolidated version surfaced by EUR-Lex:** 5 January 2025
- **OJ:** L 95, 7.4.2017, pp. 1-142

---

## Why It Matters
If EC 852/2004 and related food laws tell operators what they must do, EU 2017/625 helps determine how authorities check, verify, and enforce those duties.

This is central for questions about:
- inspections
- official sampling
- documentary checks
- border and import controls
- what authorities can require when they find non-compliance

---

## Core Regulatory Function
The regulation provides a unified framework for official controls performed to ensure application of:
- food law
- feed law
- certain animal health and welfare rules
- plant health and related controls

For PinkPepper, the food-law side is the main use case.

---

## Key Practical Themes

### 1. Risk-Based Official Controls
Competent authorities must organise official controls on a risk basis rather than using a purely random approach.

In practice, higher-risk businesses, products, and processes are more likely to receive:
- more frequent inspection
- more intensive verification
- more focused sampling or documentary review

### 2. Operator Cooperation
Food business operators are expected to cooperate with competent authorities during official controls.

That can include:
- giving access to premises
- producing records and documents
- facilitating sampling, audit, or inspection activity

### 3. Official Sampling, Analysis, and Methods
The regulation supports the use of documented methods, laboratories, and official procedures when authorities verify compliance.

This matters for:
- microbiological testing
- contaminant controls
- import verification
- challenge or dispute situations involving official findings

### 4. Measures on Non-Compliance
Where competent authorities identify non-compliance, they may require corrective action and can impose measures proportionate to the risk.

Typical consequences can include:
- requiring remediation within a set timeframe
- restricting or prohibiting placing food on the market
- ordering treatment, withdrawal, recall, or destruction
- increasing official controls on the business or product

### 5. Border and Import Controls
The regulation is also a major part of the import-control framework for food entering the EU from third countries, especially for higher-risk products.

This is relevant when discussing:
- official import checks
- documentary, identity, and physical controls
- designated control points
- interaction with implementing acts on high-risk imports

## Interaction with Other Food-Safety Laws

- **EC 178/2002:** creates the general food law framework; 2017/625 helps enforce it
- **EC 852/2004 / EC 853/2004:** define hygiene obligations; 2017/625 informs how authorities verify compliance
- **EC 2073/2005:** official controls may include microbiological checks against those criteria
- **EC 1069/2009:** explicitly amended within the wider official-controls framework
