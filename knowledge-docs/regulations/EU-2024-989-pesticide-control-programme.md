# Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/989 — Coordinated Pesticide Control Programme 2025–2027

## Overview
Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/989 of 2 April 2024 establishes a coordinated multiannual control programme for pesticide residues in food for the years 2025, 2026, and 2027. It specifies which foods Member States must test, which pesticides must be monitored, and the minimum number of samples required. This triennial programme is the primary mechanism through which EU food safety authorities enforce Regulation (EC) No 396/2005 on maximum residue levels (MRLs) of pesticides.

- **CELEX:** 32024R0989
- **Based on:** Regulation (EC) No 396/2005, Article 29
- **Published:** 3 April 2024
- **Programme period:** 2025, 2026, 2027
- **Official source:** https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg_impl/2024/989/oj

---

## Why It Matters
The EU coordinated programme is distinct from national monitoring plans — Member States must conduct this programme **in addition to** their own national programmes. Results are reported to EFSA, which publishes an annual EU Pesticide Monitoring Report. Non-compliances found during the programme trigger enforcement action and may affect import conditions for third-country suppliers.

For food businesses, understanding which commodities are under intensive EU-wide scrutiny each year is essential for:
- Supplier risk management
- Frequency of incoming goods testing
- Priority commodities to include in own-check testing programmes

---

## Commodities Under Coordinated Monitoring (2025–2027)

The programme rotates commodities across the triennial cycle. For 2025–2027, key food categories under coordinated testing include:

### Fresh Produce (Fruits and Vegetables)
- Apples, pears, peaches, nectarines
- Strawberries, grapes (table), kiwifruit
- Tomatoes, sweet peppers, courgettes, aubergines
- Lettuce, spinach, basil, chives (herbs)
- Carrots, sweet potatoes, leeks
- Citrus fruit (oranges, mandarins, lemons)

### Cereals and Pulses
- Wheat grain, wheat flour
- Oats, rye, barley
- Dry pulses (lentils, chickpeas, beans)
- Maize (corn)

### Animal-Origin Products
- Eggs (hen)
- Bovine meat (muscle) and bovine liver
- Milk and dairy products
- Honey

### Processed Foods
- Baby foods (fruit- and vegetable-based, cereal-based)
- Infant formula and follow-on formula
- Wine (grape-based, only for certain residues)

### Specific Focus Areas 2025–2027
- **Residues of banned pesticides**: The programme specifically targets substances whose authorisation has lapsed or been withdrawn but which persist in global supply chains (e.g. chlorpyrifos, thiamethoxam in commodities from certain third countries)
- **Highly polar pesticides**: New analytical challenge — polar compounds (e.g. fosetyl-aluminium, glyphosate, chlorate) require different extraction methods and are the subject of mandatory monitoring
- **Neonicotinoids**: Following EU restrictions on outdoor use (thiacloprid banned 2020, imidacloprid/clothianidin/thiamethoxam restricted), monitoring confirms compliance with import conditions

## Pesticides Under Coordinated Monitoring

The programme lists specific pesticides per commodity. Key substances under enhanced scrutiny in 2025–2027 include:

### Priority Residues — Widespread Monitoring Required
- Chlorpyrifos and chlorpyrifos-methyl (banned in EU, still found in imports)
- Glyphosate (MRL set at LOQ 0.10 mg/kg for most crops not authorised for desiccation)
- Fosetyl-aluminium (complex MRL — sum of fosetyl + phosphonate expressed as H₃PO₃)
- Neonicotinoids (acetamiprid, clothianidin, imidacloprid, thiamethoxam, thiacloprid)
- Mancozeb and dithiocarbamates (MRL expressed as CS₂)
- Spinosad (authorised in organic production; monitoring for exceedances)

### New Additions for 2025–2027
- **Flupyradifurone** (systemic insecticide; increased EU use following neonicotinoid restrictions)
- **Sulfoxaflor** (sulfoximine insecticide; authorised for some uses in EU but concerns remain)
- **Spirotetramat** (ketoenol insecticide used in fruit and vegetables)

## Sampling Requirements

### Minimum Sample Numbers
Each Member State must analyse at least:
- **10 samples** per commodity per year from the coordinated list
- Additional samples for commodities where non-compliance was found in the previous EFSA annual report
- At least **50% of samples** must be of domestic origin (EU-produced) where available; the remainder may be imported

### Origin Requirements
A defined proportion of samples must come from **third-country (non-EU) imports** — particularly for commodities where EU import volumes are high and where surveillance data shows elevated non-compliance rates (e.g. herbs from Asia, tropical fruits from South America and Africa).

---

## Implications for Food Business Operators

### Importers and Traders
- Commodities on the coordinated programme are subject to **more frequent official sampling at borders**. This is not a reason to delay or refuse shipments, but operators should:
  1. Maintain analytical certificates of analysis (CoAs) from producing-country laboratories for priority commodities
  2. Brief suppliers on EU MRL requirements and changes to authorised pesticides
  3. Document supplier approval processes including evidence of pesticide management

### Manufacturers Using Listed Raw Materials
- If using fresh herbs, dried pulses, cereals, or fresh produce from the list as ingredients, consider increasing incoming goods testing frequency for the period 2025–2027
- Baby food manufacturers: infant formula and baby foods are on the coordinated programme — residue testing of raw materials and finished products is expected

### Supermarkets and Importers of Exotics
- Tropical fruits (mangoes, avocados, papaya, pineapple) sometimes appear in national programmes but less frequently in the coordinated programme; check current national monitoring plans
- Herbs (fresh basil, mint, coriander) have historically high non-compliance rates; testing is strongly recommended even where not legally required for self-monitoring

---

## Where Non-Compliance Leads

When official samples under the coordinated programme exceed MRLs:
1. **Immediate enforcement**: Product may be withdrawn, detained, or destroyed
2. **Rapid Alert System (RASFF)**: Serious exceedances are notified through RASFF and may trigger enhanced border controls for all consignments from the same origin
3. **Third-country notifications**: EFSA annual reports inform the Commission's decisions on import conditions; persistent non-compliance can lead to emergency measures under EU 2019/1793
4. **Operator traceability**: FBOs must cooperate with competent authorities; failure to trace origin is itself a violation of EC 178/2002

---

## Interaction with Other Regulations
- **EC 396/2005**: Sets the MRLs that the programme enforces
- **EU 2019/1793**: Emergency import controls (increased official controls and emergency measures)
- **EU 2024/1441 and EU 2026/194**: Recent amendments to EU 2019/1793 imposing enhanced controls on specific products from specific countries, often triggered by coordinated programme findings
- **EC 178/2002**: Traceability obligations apply to all non-compliant product
