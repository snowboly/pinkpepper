# COMMISSION REGULATION (EU) 2023/915 — Maximum Levels for Certain Contaminants in Food

## Overview
Commission Regulation (EU) 2023/915 of 25 April 2023 consolidates and replaces Regulation (EC) No 1881/2006, setting maximum levels for certain contaminants in food. It also incorporates the amendments previously introduced by Commission Regulation (EU) 2023/465 (arsenic limits). In force from 25 May 2023.

**CELEX:** 32023R0915
**Legal basis:** Council Regulation (EEC) No 315/93, Article 2(3)
**Replaces:** Regulation (EC) No 1881/2006 and all its amendments, including Regulation (EU) 2023/465

---

## Regulation (EU) 2023/465 — Arsenic (Now Incorporated into 2023/915)

Commission Regulation (EU) 2023/465 of 3 March 2023 amended Regulation (EC) No 1881/2006 as regards maximum levels of **inorganic arsenic** in certain foods. It was implicitly repealed on 25 May 2023 by Regulation (EU) 2023/915, which now contains the updated arsenic limits.

Key provisions from 2023/465 (now in 2023/915):
- Lowered maximum levels for inorganic arsenic in rice and rice-based products
- Introduced new maximum levels for additional food categories (e.g. baby food, cereals)
- Based on EFSA CONTAM Panel opinion that the former PTWI of 15 μg/kg bw was no longer appropriate
- Recognised that high consumers of rice (including ethnic groups and children under 3) face 2–3× higher exposure than adults

## Key Contaminant Categories Covered

### 1. Heavy Metals
- **Lead (Pb):** Maximum levels in meat, offal, fish, cereals, vegetables, fruit, milk, infant food
- **Cadmium (Cd):** Maximum levels in meat, offal, fish, cereals, vegetables, cocoa, infant food
- **Mercury (Hg):** Maximum levels in fish and fishery products (methylmercury)
- **Inorganic arsenic (iAs):** Maximum levels in rice, rice-based products, infant food, cereals, fruit juices, water

### 2. Mycotoxins
- **Aflatoxins (B1, total, M1):** Maximum levels in nuts, dried fruit, cereals, spices, milk, infant food
- **Ochratoxin A:** Maximum levels in cereals, wine, dried fruit, coffee, cocoa, spices, liquorice
- **Deoxynivalenol (DON):** Maximum levels in cereals and cereal products
- **Zearalenone:** Maximum levels in cereals and cereal products
- **Fumonisins (B1+B2):** Maximum levels in maize, maize-based food
- **Citrinin:** Maximum levels in food supplements from red yeast rice
- **Ergot alkaloids:** Maximum levels in cereal milling products

### 3. Process Contaminants
- **Benzo(a)pyrene and PAH4:** Maximum levels in oils/fats, smoked meat/fish, cocoa, infant food
- **3-MCPD and glycidyl esters:** Maximum levels in vegetable oils/fats, infant formula
- **Acrylamide:** Benchmark levels in fried potatoes, bread, cereals, coffee, biscuits, infant food

### 4. Environmental and Industrial Contaminants
- **Dioxins and PCBs (dl-PCBs, ndl-PCBs):** Maximum levels in meat, fish, milk, eggs, oils
- **Perchlorate:** Maximum levels in fruit, vegetables, infant food
- **Melamine:** Maximum level of 2.5 mg/kg in food; 0.15 mg/kg in infant formula

### 5. Plant Toxins
- **Tropane alkaloids:** Maximum levels in cereal-based infant food, herbal infusions
- **Pyrrolizidine alkaloids:** Maximum levels in tea, herbal infusions, food supplements, honey
- **Hydrocyanic acid:** Maximum levels in stone fruit spirits, marzipan
- **Opium alkaloids:** Maximum levels in poppy seeds and derived products
- **Delta-9-THC:** Maximum levels in hemp-derived food products

## Key Provisions

### Article 1 — Scope
Maximum levels set out in the Annex apply to food placed on the market. Levels apply to the edible part unless otherwise specified.

### Article 2 — Dried, Diluted, Processed and Multi-Ingredient Foods
- For dried/concentrated products: apply concentration/dilution factor
- For processed products: apply processing factors where available
- For multi-ingredient products: take into account proportions of ingredients

### Article 3 — Sorting and Physical Treatment
- Groundnuts, nuts, dried fruit and cereals may be sorted or physically treated to reduce contamination before final consumer sale
- Maximum levels for aflatoxins after sorting are more stringent

### Article 4 — Specific Provisions for Infant and Young Child Food
- Stricter limits apply to infant formulae, follow-on formulae, processed cereal-based food and baby food
- For contaminants not specifically regulated in baby food, levels must be as low as reasonably achievable (ALARA)

### Article 9 — Repeal
Regulation (EC) No 1881/2006 is repealed with effect from 25 May 2023. References to 1881/2006 shall be read as references to this Regulation.

---

## Sampling and Analysis
Sampling and analysis for official controls shall be performed in accordance with:
- Commission Regulation (EC) No 401/2006 (mycotoxins)
- Commission Regulation (EC) No 1882/2006 (nitrates)
- Commission Regulation (EU) 2017/644 (dioxins and PCBs)
- Commission Regulation (EC) No 333/2007 (metals and other contaminants)

---

## Food Business Operator Obligations
- Must not place on the market food exceeding maximum levels
- Must not mix compliant with non-compliant lots
- Must apply HACCP principles and good manufacturing practice to keep contaminants as low as reasonably achievable
- Must maintain traceability records for contaminant testing and results

## 2024–2025 Amendments — IMPORTANT

Regulation (EU) 2023/915 has been progressively amended since publication. The standalone 2023/915 text is **no longer current**. The following subsequent regulations modify specific maximum levels and must be read alongside this regulation:

| Regulation | Subject | Applies From |
|---|---|---|
| **EU 2024/1022** | Deoxynivalenol (DON) and acetylated forms in cereals and cereal products; halved limits for infant/baby cereals | 1 July 2024 |
| **EU 2024/1756** | Corrections and amendments: lead in leafy vegetables/baby foods, cadmium in cocoa/oilseeds, PAH in smoked fish/dried herbs, ochratoxin A, fumonisin corrections | 15 July 2024 |
| **EU 2025/1891** | Heavy metals in fish and seafood: inorganic arsenic (fish, shellfish, algae), methylmercury (predatory fish, tuna, freshwater species), cadmium (cephalopods, crab) | 2025 |

**Practical guidance:** Always use the EUR-Lex **consolidated version** of EU 2023/915 (CELEX: 32023R0915) which incorporates all amendments automatically. Do not rely on the standalone 2023 text when checking specific maximum levels — it is outdated.

The old EC 1881/2006 is fully repealed. Any reference to "Regulation 1881/2006" in supplier documentation or specifications should be updated to EU 2023/915 (as amended).

### Note on Inorganic Arsenic in Fish (post-EU 2025/1891)
The 2025/1891 amendment substantially revised arsenic limits for fish and seafood based on EFSA's 2024 opinion. Key points:
- Maximum levels now differentiated by species group (flatfish, pelagic fish, crustaceans, bivalves, cephalopods) rather than applying a single blanket limit
- Algae and seaweed products have stricter inorganic arsenic limits
- The distinction between **total arsenic** (mostly organic, non-toxic) and **inorganic arsenic** (toxic) is critical — regulations target only the inorganic fraction
- Testing must use species-specific validated methods for inorganic arsenic (not total arsenic)
