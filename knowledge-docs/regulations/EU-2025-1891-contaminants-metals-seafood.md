# Commission Regulation (EU) 2025/1891 — Maximum Levels for Metals in Fish and Seafood

## Overview
Commission Regulation (EU) 2025/1891 amends Regulation (EU) 2023/915 as regards maximum levels for certain heavy metals — in particular inorganic arsenic, mercury (methylmercury), cadmium, and lead — in fish and fishery products. It implements updated EFSA scientific opinions published in 2024 on dietary exposure to these metals through seafood consumption, and introduces revised maximum levels that better reflect current consumption patterns and toxicological data.

- **CELEX:** 32025R1891
- **Amends:** Regulation (EU) 2023/915 (Annex I, Section 3 — metals)
- **Published:** Autumn 2025
- **Official source:** https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2025/1891/oj
- **Note:** For the definitive and most current text, verify on EUR-Lex using the CELEX reference above.

---

## Why It Matters
Fish and seafood are the dominant dietary sources of inorganic arsenic and methylmercury for most European consumers. EFSA's 2024 scientific opinion on arsenic re-evaluated previous tolerable weekly intake estimates, concluding that:
1. Exposure to inorganic arsenic in certain seafood categories (particularly certain fish species and shellfish) may exceed health-based guidance values for regular consumers
2. Existing maximum levels for inorganic arsenic in fish (set or consolidated in EU 2023/915) did not sufficiently reflect interspecies variation or bioavailability differences between marine and freshwater fish

For methylmercury, EFSA's review of fish consumption data identified specific consumer groups — pregnant women, women of childbearing age, and young children — where dietary exposure through regular consumption of predatory fish (tuna, swordfish, shark, pike, perch) may exceed the tolerable weekly intake (TWI).

## Key Changes

### Inorganic Arsenic in Fish and Fishery Products
- **Revised limits for specific fish species** with high arsenic bioaccumulation: limits adjusted downward for flatfish and certain marine fish species
- **New category: crustaceans and molluscs (shellfish)**: differentiated limits introduced for shrimp, prawns, crab, lobster, oysters, mussels, clams to replace a single blanket limit
- **Algae and seaweed-based products** used as food or food ingredients: stricter limits based on evidence that certain macroalgae (hijiki, nori, wakame) accumulate significantly higher levels of inorganic arsenic
- **Fish-based baby foods and infant products**: limit reduced in line with EFSA's assessment that infants have lower tolerable intakes per kg body weight

### Methylmercury in Fish
- **Predatory fish** (shark/dogfish, swordfish, marlin, tuna, pike): limits reviewed; no substantive increase beyond EU 2023/915 values, but method of expression clarified (total mercury vs methylmercury fraction)
- **Tuna in cans**: maximum level confirmed; new differentiation between skipjack/light tuna and albacore/white tuna reflecting bioaccumulation differences
- **Freshwater predatory fish** (pike, perch, pike-perch): separate limits introduced for inland fisheries product distinct from marine species limits

### Cadmium in Cephalopods and Crustaceans
- **Cephalopods (squid, octopus, cuttlefish)**: revised limits following evidence of high cadmium bioaccumulation in digestive organs; limit applies to the whole edible portion (muscle + viscera)
- **Crab hepatopancreas**: updated recommendation against consuming the brown meat of crab given naturally elevated cadmium content

### Lead in Fish and Seafood
Minor corrections only — limits largely consistent with EU 2024/1756 corrections; no substantive change.

## Species-Specific Guidance

### Fish to Monitor Closely Under New Limits
| Species/Category | Key Contaminant | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Flatfish (plaice, flounder, sole) | Inorganic arsenic | Limit revised |
| Shellfish (mussels, oysters, clams) | Inorganic arsenic, cadmium | Differentiated limits |
| Shrimp and prawns | Inorganic arsenic | New specific limit |
| Crab (whole) | Cadmium | Tighter for viscera |
| Tuna (albacore) | Methylmercury | Confirmed/clarified |
| Algae/seaweed ingredients | Inorganic arsenic | Stricter limit |
| Freshwater predatory fish | Methylmercury | New specific category |

## Operator Obligations

### Fish Processors, Importers, and Wholesalers
1. **Review incoming goods specifications**: update CoA requirements for affected species and products
2. **Country-of-origin due diligence**: arsenic levels in fish vary considerably by geographic origin (Pacific vs Atlantic vs inland); supplier risk assessments should reflect this
3. **Species substitution risk**: verify that product labelling correctly identifies species — mislabelling of cheaper flatfish as premium species is a documented fraud that can inadvertently transfer compliance risk
4. **Canned fish**: verify tuna speciation (skipjack vs albacore) for correct limit application

### Manufacturers Using Seafood Ingredients
- If using dried or powdered seafood, shrimp meal, fish bone, or algae extracts as ingredients, test incoming materials under the new limits
- Baby food manufacturers: any fish-containing infant product must comply with infant-specific limits

### Retail and Catering
- Purchasing specifications for fresh fish and shellfish should reference the updated limits
- Labelling of species must be accurate (EU 1169/2011 requires species name for fish sold prepacked)

## Consumer Advice (for chatbot context)
When users ask about fish safety:
- **Pregnant women and young children**: advised to limit consumption of predatory fish (shark, swordfish, tuna, pike) due to methylmercury; current FSA/EFSA guidance recommends max 2 portions of oily fish per week, 1 portion per week for pregnant women
- **Seaweed and algae supplements**: high inorganic arsenic content is a genuine risk; some products (particularly hijiki) were advised against for regular consumption even before this regulation
- **Shellfish from bivalve growing areas**: cadmium and arsenic limits apply at point of harvest; local authority monitoring of classified bivalve harvesting areas is part of the control system

## Interaction with Other Regulations
- **EU 2023/915**: parent regulation; this amendment updates Annex I
- **EU 2024/1756**: earlier 2024 correction to EU 2023/915 — must be read alongside
- **EU 2024/1022**: DON limits (unrelated to metals; separate contaminants amendment)
- **EC 853/2004**: official controls on products of animal origin including fishery products
- **EU 2017/625**: official controls framework; competent authorities enforce these limits at border and in-market

- Use for questions about arsenic in fish, shellfish, or seaweed

- Use for questions about cadmium in squid, octopus, crab
- Use for questions about which fish are safe to eat during pregnancy
- Use when users cite "EU 1891" or "Regulation 1891/2025" — this is the regulation
- The distinction between total arsenic (mostly non-toxic organic forms from fish) and **inorganic arsenic** (the toxic fraction) is critical: regulations target inorganic arsenic only
