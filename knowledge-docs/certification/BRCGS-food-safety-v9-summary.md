# BRCGS Food Safety Standard — Issue 9 Summary

## Overview

The BRCGS (Brand Reputation through Compliance Global Standards) Food Safety Standard is one of the most widely recognised private food safety standards globally. Originally developed by UK retailers, it is now used by over 30,000 certified sites in 130+ countries. Issue 9 is the current edition.

Certification is awarded by BRCGS-approved certification bodies following an unannounced or announced audit.

---

## Structure of BRCGS Food Safety Issue 9

The standard comprises:
1. **Senior management commitment** (Section 1)
2. **The food safety plan — HACCP** (Section 2)
3. **Food safety and quality management system** (Section 3)
4. **Site standards** (Section 4)
5. **Product control** (Section 5)
6. **Process control** (Section 6)
7. **Personnel** (Section 7)
8. **Traded products** (Section 8)
9. **Logo use and product identification** (Section 9)

### Fundamental Requirements

Issues 9 contains **fundamental requirements** — clauses of such importance that failure results in an **automatic audit failure**. These are marked with an asterisk (*) in the standard.

Key fundamental requirements include:
- Senior management commitment and continual improvement (1.1)
- The food safety plan (HACCP system) (2.1)
- Internal audits (3.4)
- Supplier and raw material approval and performance monitoring (3.5.1)
- Corrective action procedure (3.7)
- Traceability (3.9)
- Layout, product flow, and segregation (4.3)
- Housekeeping and hygiene (4.9)
- Management of allergens (5.3)
- Control of operations (6.1)
- Training — raw material handling, preparation, processing, packing and storage areas (7.1)

---

## Section 1 — Senior Management Commitment

- Senior management must demonstrate visible commitment to food safety culture
- A documented food safety and quality policy signed by the most senior person on site
- Business objectives and improvement plans reviewed regularly
- Resources allocated to food safety
- Food safety culture programme in place (Issue 9 enhancement) — measuring, reporting, and improving staff engagement in food safety

---

## Section 2 — HACCP (Food Safety Plan)

BRCGS requires a HACCP system based on the Codex Alimentarius 7-step approach:

1. Assemble the HACCP team (multidisciplinary)
2. Describe the product
3. Identify the intended use
4. Construct the process flow diagram
5. On-site verification of the flow diagram
6. Conduct hazard analysis (list all potential hazards; assess significance)
7. Determine CCPs (using decision tree or equivalent)
8. Establish critical limits for each CCP
9. Establish monitoring system for each CCP
10. Establish corrective actions
11. Establish verification procedures
12. Establish documentation and record-keeping

HACCP must be reviewed **at least annually** and when any significant change occurs.

---

## Section 3 — Food Safety and Quality Management System

### Key Requirements

**3.4 Internal Audits**
- A programme of internal audits covering all activities affecting food safety, legality, and quality
- High-risk and high-care areas: at least 4 times per year
- Other areas: at least once per year
- Auditors must be independent from the area being audited and competent
- Non-conformances raised and corrected within defined timescales

**3.5 Supplier and Raw Material Approval**
- Approved supplier list (ASL) maintained
- Suppliers assessed by risk-based approach: audit, certification (e.g. BRCGS, SQF, FSSC 22000), or questionnaire
- Critical raw materials (allergens, microbiologically sensitive ingredients): stricter approval criteria
- Performance monitoring (micro results, complaints, audit findings)

**3.7 Corrective Action and Preventive Action (CAPA)**
- Root cause analysis for non-conformances
- Corrective action documented and verified effective
- Trend analysis to identify recurring issues

**3.9 Traceability**
- Traceability test (mock recall / trace) carried out at least **once per year**
- Must be able to trace from finished product back to raw materials (and forward to customer) within **4 hours**
- Quantities balance to ≥99% (mass balance)

## Section 4 — Site Standards

### 4.3 Layout, Product Flow, and Segregation

Sites must demonstrate product flow prevents cross-contamination:
- Raw and ready-to-eat (RTE) areas physically segregated
- High-risk and high-care areas (where applicable) with controlled access, airlock, dedicated PPE and equipment

### 4.4 Fabric of the Building

All food production areas must have:
- Suitable flooring (impermeable, non-slip, coved at wall junctions in wet-process areas)
- Smooth, washable walls to appropriate height
- Ceilings constructed to prevent dust accumulation and condensation
- Windows and external openings: insect-screened or kept closed
- Drains: covered, cleaned, and maintained

### 4.9 Housekeeping and Hygiene

- A cleaning schedule for all areas and equipment
- Cleaning procedures validated (ATP or swabbing) for food-contact surfaces
- Allergen cleaning validation documented
- Cleaning chemicals: food-grade; COSHH data sheets available; stored separately from food

### 4.11 Pest Control

- A contracted pest control programme or documented in-house programme
- Site plan showing all monitoring devices
- Regular inspections; records of findings and actions
- Proofing register with documented repairs

---

## Section 5 — Product Control

### 5.3 Allergen Management

**BRCGS treats allergen management as fundamental.**

Requirements include:
- Risk assessment for all allergens used on site and potential cross-contamination routes
- Allergen matrix for all products
- Segregated storage for allergenic raw materials
- Validated cleaning procedures when switching between allergen and allergen-free production
- Allergen testing of cleaning validation swabs and/or product (where risk assessment warrants)
- Correct labelling of all products (pre-packed, PPDS, non-prepacked)
- Staff training on allergen awareness

### 5.4 Product Authenticity, Claims, and Chain of Custody

- Risk assessment for food fraud (VACCP — Vulnerability Assessment and Critical Control Points)
- Mitigation plan for identified vulnerabilities
- Applies particularly to: high-value ingredients, complex supply chains, ingredients with history of adulteration (olive oil, honey, fish species, spices)

---

## Section 6 — Process Control

### 6.1 Control of Operations

- Defined process parameters for all CCPs and quality control points
- Equipment capable of meeting process requirements
- Changeover and start-up procedures documented

### 6.4 Quantity — Weight, Volume, and Number Control

- Net content compliance with the Weights and Measures Act 1985 (UK)
- Average system or minimum weight system

---

## Section 7 — Personnel

### 7.1 Training

- All personnel trained before working unsupervised; competency assessed
- Training records maintained for all staff
- Food hygiene training to recognised qualification for food handlers
- Annual refresher or competency review

### 7.2 Personal Hygiene

- Written personal hygiene policy
- Jewellery, nail varnish, nail extensions, and accessories policy
- Protective clothing requirements
- Illness reporting and fitness-to-work policy

## Grading Criteria

| Grade | Meaning | Audit Type |
|---|---|---|
| **AA** | Excellent — zero critical or major NCs; minimal minor NCs | Unannounced |
| **A** | Very good — zero critical NCs; minor major NCs; minimal minor NCs | Unannounced |
| **B** | Good — zero critical NCs; ≤5 major NCs | Announced or unannounced |
| **C** | Acceptable — zero critical NCs; ≤10 major NCs; ≤25 minor NCs | Announced |
| **D** | Below standard — zero critical NCs | Announced; requires action plan |
| **E** | Not certified — fundamental requirement failed | Announced |
| **F** | Not certified — critical NC found | Announced |

Non-conformance categories:
- **Critical**: immediate risk to food safety; automatic failure
- **Major**: failure to comply with a statement in the standard; significant risk to product safety, legality, or integrity
- **Minor**: partial compliance; low risk; not systematic

---

## Issue 9 Key Changes vs. Issue 8

1. **Food safety culture** — new mandatory requirement for a documented culture programme
2. **Environmental monitoring** — enhanced requirements for environmental pathogen monitoring (especially Listeria in high-care environments)
3. **Food fraud (VACCP)** — stronger requirements; must be integrated into food safety management system
4. **Allergen validation** — cleaning validation now explicitly required
5. **High-care / ambient high-care zones** — clearer definitions and requirements
6. **Storage and distribution** — scope clarification for transport and cold chain
7. **Traded goods** — strengthened requirements for businesses trading BRCGS-certified goods

## Certification Bodies (Examples)

- NSF International
- SGS
- Bureau Veritas
- Intertek
- DNV
- LRQA
- Eurofins

All must be BRCGS-approved. Audits are always conducted by the certification body, not by BRCGS itself. Certificates issued by the certification body are registered on the BRCGS Directory.
